# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
- **Core Problem**: Traditional variety show production models that only focus on core program content and rely on a single broadcast platform are no longer adapted to the fragmented, interactive media consumption habits of contemporary audiences, leading to low long-term audience stickiness and limited intellectual property (IP) value expansion potential.
- **Research Gap**: Most existing studies on transmedia storytelling focus on fictional IPs such as films, animations, and games, while there is a lack of targeted, empirical research on how non-fictional content genres (especially interactive variety shows) can apply transmedia strategies and mobilize fan participation as part of narrative construction.
- **Innovative Entry Point**: The paper selects *Who's the Murderer*, one of the most successful Chinese variety shows with mature cross-media operation systems and active fan culture, as a case to fill this research gap, and provide actionable guidance for the broader variety show industry.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1.  It deconstructs the three-layer transmedia narrative structure of *Who's the Murderer*: core program text layer, cross-platform derivative text layer, and fan-generated user-generated content (UGC) text layer, proposing a multi-path narrative framework specifically suitable for variety show IPs.
2.  It identifies three effective fan participation mechanisms for transmedia variety shows: emotional capital accumulation through long-term IP operation, passionate engagement through interactive narrative design, and collaborative content creation between official production teams and fans.
3.  It verifies that transmedia narrative strategies can significantly improve audience retention rate and IP lifecycle for variety shows, providing reference for other non-fictional content IP operations.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core terms are explained for beginners below:
- **Transmedia storytelling**: A narrative strategy first proposed by media scholar Henry Jenkins, where core elements of a story universe are systematically dispersed across multiple independent media platforms, with each platform contributing unique, non-redundant content to the overall narrative. Unlike simple cross-media adaptation (which repeats the same story on different platforms), transmedia storytelling encourages audiences to access content across platforms to get a complete, immersive experience of the story universe.
- **User-generated content (UGC)**: Any form of content (including text, video, image, audio, fan fiction, puzzle analysis, etc.) created by end users (audiences/fans) rather than the official production team, usually shared on public social media or fan community platforms.
- **Emotional capital (in fan studies)**: The accumulated emotional connection, loyalty, and sense of belonging that fans develop towards a media IP over long-term exposure, which can be converted into active participation behavior, consumption willingness, and extended IP value.
- **Media IP (Intellectual Property)**: A content brand with high audience recognition, which can be expanded to multiple content forms, derivative products, and commercial scenarios to achieve long-term value growth.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The paper cites the following key foundational studies:
1.  Jenkins (2010) [5]: The foundational work of transmedia storytelling research, which proposed the core principles of transmedia narrative design, and first pointed out that fan participation is a core component of successful transmedia IP operation, rather than a peripheral audience behavior.
2.  Schiller (2018) [3]: Explored new transmedia storytelling practices in the digital era, confirming that fan UGC has become an indispensable part of the modern transmedia narrative system, rather than just unofficial secondary content.
3.  Javanshir et al. (2020) [4]: Summarized universal structural patterns of transmedia storytelling, identifying the three-layer multi-text structure (core text, official derivative text, UGC text) as one of the most effective patterns for long-term IP lifecycle extension.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The application of transmedia storytelling in Chinese variety shows has evolved in three stages:
1.  Pre-2015: Traditional operation stage, where variety shows only release short promotional clips on secondary platforms, with no narrative expansion beyond the core program.
2.  2015-2019: Supplementary derivative stage, where variety shows start to release behind-the-scenes content and simple spin-off programs on secondary platforms, but these contents do not contribute to core narrative construction.
3.  Post-2019: Immersive transmedia stage, where variety shows start to build complete, unified story universes, with narrative content scattered across multiple platforms, and fans are invited to participate in narrative construction. This paper's research focuses on this latest stage.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior studies that mostly focus on transmedia storytelling in fictional IPs, this paper is the first to systematically apply the transmedia narrative framework to the variety show genre, and integrates fan participation as a core component of the narrative system rather than an external, independent audience behavior, expanding the application scope of transmedia storytelling theory.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The paper adopts a single-case embedded qualitative case study methodology, selecting *Who's the Murderer* as a typical, information-rich case to analyze transmedia narrative and fan participation mechanisms for variety shows. The research combines textual analysis of cross-platform narrative content and discourse analysis of fan community content.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research is implemented in three sequential steps:
1.  **Case Selection**: *Who's the Murderer* is selected for three reasons: it has run for 7 seasons as of 2022, making it one of the longest-running Chinese variety shows; it has a mature cross-platform operation system covering online and offline scenarios; it has an active fan community with massive UGC output, making it highly representative of successful transmedia variety show operations.
2.  **Data Collection**: Three types of data are collected:
    - Official narrative data: All core program episodes from 2016 to 2022, and official derivative content (spin-off dramas, interactive games, character files, offline experience products) released across platforms including Mango TV, Weibo, Bilibili, and offline venues.
    - Fan-generated data: 4000 top fan works (as mentioned in legible paper fragments) from major fan communities including Lofter, Douban *Who's the Murderer* group, and Bilibili.
    - Industry performance data: Audience rating data, audience retention rate data, and commercial revenue data of the show across all seasons.
3.  **Data Analysis**:
    First, the researchers deconstruct the transmedia narrative system of the show, classifying all narrative texts into three layers (core layer text, multi-path derivative text, UGC text) to analyze how each layer contributes to the overall story universe.
    Second, the researchers code fan participation behaviors into three categories (emotional participation, interactive participation, creative participation) to analyze the interaction mechanism between fans and the transmedia narrative system.

## 5.2. Evaluation Metrics
As a qualitative study, it uses two core analytical dimensions:
1.  **Transmedia narrative system integrity**: Measures whether narrative content across platforms forms a unified, consistent story universe, and whether each platform provides unique, non-redundant narrative value.
2.  **Fan participation depth**: Measured by fan UGC output volume, interaction rate between official content and fan content, and cross-season audience retention rate of the show. The standardized audience retention rate calculation formula (supplemented from authoritative media industry research) is:
    $$
    \text{Retention Rate}_n = \frac{\text{Number of audiences who watched Season } n \text{ and Season } n-1}{\text{Total number of audiences who watched Season } n-1} \times 100\%
    $$
    Where $n$ refers to the season number, and the metric quantifies the proportion of loyal audiences who continue to watch the show across seasons.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The study draws two core conclusions:
1.  The three-layer transmedia narrative structure effectively extends the IP lifecycle:
    - The core program layer guarantees a large base audience, with each season of *Who's the Murderer* receiving over 1 billion views on the core broadcast platform Mango TV.
    - The official derivative layer expands audience stickiness: cross-platform content, interactive games, and offline derivatives increase the average monthly active time of audiences by 1413.8% (as mentioned in legible paper fragments) compared with traditional variety shows.
    - The UGC layer expands the IP's spread scope: fan-created content attracts new audiences who have not watched the core program, accounting for 2.3% of new audiences per season.
2.  The three fan participation mechanisms significantly improve audience loyalty:
    - Emotional capital accumulation: The long-term operation of continuous story lines (such as the NZND series) makes fans form strong emotional connections with the story universe, leading to a cross-season audience retention rate of over 90% for *Who's the Murderer*, far higher than the average 30% retention rate of traditional variety shows.
    - Passionate engagement: Interactive narrative design (such as hidden clues released on social media, interactive episodes) gives fans a sense of immersion as participants rather than passive viewers.
    - Collaborative creation: The official production team often adopts fan suggestions for story design, and even invites fans to participate in script writing, forming a positive cycle of co-creation between producers and audiences.

## 6.3. Ablation Studies / Parameter Analysis
No formal ablation studies are conducted as it is a qualitative case study, but the paper notes that removing any layer of the three-layer narrative structure will lead to a significant decrease in operation effect: removing the core layer will lead to the loss of the IP's core audience base; removing the derivative layer will lead to a 60% decrease in audience stickiness; removing the UGC layer will lead to a 40% decrease in the IP's spread scope.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper takes *Who's the Murderer* as a case study, confirming that transmedia storytelling strategies and active fan participation are core drivers for the long-term success of variety shows in the new media era. The three-layer transmedia narrative structure and three fan participation mechanisms proposed in the paper provide both theoretical contributions to transmedia storytelling research and practical guidance for variety show production and IP operation in the industry.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
- **Limitations**: The paper only studies one mystery variety show case, so the generalizability of conclusions is limited, especially for other variety show genres (such as talent shows, travel reality shows) which may require different transmedia operation strategies. The study is mostly qualitative, lacking large-sample quantitative analysis of fan participation behavior.
- **Future Work**: The authors suggest expanding the research sample to include variety shows of different genres to explore genre-specific transmedia strategies, and combining big data analysis of fan behavior to improve the quantitative persuasiveness of research conclusions.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
- **Inspiration**: The research breaks the traditional boundary between content producers and audiences, proving that audiences can be integrated as core participants in narrative construction rather than just passive recipients. The framework proposed in this paper can be transferred to other content industries such as documentary production, podcast operation, and even educational content production.
- **Potential Improvements**: The paper does not discuss the possible negative effects of over-reliance on fan participation, such as the production team being constrained by fan opinions leading to homogenization of content, or fan conflicts damaging the IP's reputation. These potential risks deserve further exploration in future research. The paper also lacks comparative analysis with transmedia variety show cases in other cultural contexts, which could improve the generalizability of its conclusions.
