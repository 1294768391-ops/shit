## 1.5. Abstract
### Research Objective
To explore the core rules of modern adaptation for Chinese animated films, and reflect on approaches to balancing inheritance and innovation of classic traditional IP in the context of market-oriented film industry development.
### Core Methodology
Qualitative comparative analysis of two most commercially and culturally successful Nezha-themed animated films: *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* (1979) and *Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child* (2019), covering character design, narrative structure, aesthetic style, and value connotation.
### Main Results
Differences in adaptation focus between the two works are directly driven by changes in era context, audience aesthetic demand, and film industry development:
1. The 1979 version uses type-cast, binary `flat characters` and tragic narrative to retain traditional myth features, catering to 1970s audiences' demand for visual spectacle and clear good-evil distinctions.
2. The 2019 version uses multi-dimensional, complex `round characters` and comedy-inclined realistic narrative, integrating modern social values to meet contemporary audiences' demand for emotional resonance and non-binary character perception.
### Key Conclusions
Shifting from myth-oriented to reality-oriented adaptation, while retaining the core spiritual connotation of traditional IP and catering to current audience and market demand, is the inevitable trend of high-quality modern adaptation for Chinese animated films.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem
The rapidly growing Chinese film industry faces a persistent shortage of high-quality original content, leading to increasing focus on adaptation of classic traditional cultural IP (such as mythological characters and stories) with pre-existing public recognition. However, there is a lack of systematic, evidence-based guidance on how to balance inheritance of traditional cultural connotations and innovative adjustment to meet contemporary audience demand, leading to a large number of low-quality, poorly received adaptation attempts.
### Research Gap
Prior research on Nezha-themed animated works mostly focuses on single-film text analysis, lacks cross-era horizontal comparison to reveal how changes in social context, audience aesthetic preference, and industrial environment shape adaptation strategies. Few studies connect content-level adaptation choices to market performance and audience reception rules, limiting their practical guidance value for the animation industry.
### Entry Point & Innovative Idea
The paper selects two representative, highly successful Nezha-themed animated films across 40 years of Chinese animation development as research samples, constructs a multi-dimensional analysis framework covering character, narrative, and value connotation, and links adaptation choices to era-specific background factors to extract generalizable adaptation rules.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1. Establishes a systematic, multi-dimensional analytical framework for traditional animation IP adaptation evaluation, integrating character design, narrative mode, aesthetic presentation, and value transmission assessment.
2. Reveals the causal correlation between adaptation strategy evolution and changes in audience demand, social values, and film industry development level, filling the gap of cross-era adaptation rule research in Chinese animation studies.
3. Provides practical, evidence-based guidance for subsequent adaptation of Chinese traditional cultural IP, clarifying the core principles for balancing cultural inheritance, creative innovation, and commercial viability.
### Key Conclusions
1. Character design in Chinese animated IP adaptation has evolved from single-trait `flat characters` to multi-dimensional `round characters`, responding to modern audiences' rejection of binary non-black-and-white character settings and demand for in-depth emotional experience.
2. Narrative mode has shifted from linear, tragic myth-focused narrative to multi-line, comedy-inclined realistic narrative, integrating modern social concerns such as native family influence on child development and individual resistance to fate to resonate with contemporary audiences.
3. Successful modern adaptation must retain the core spiritual connotation of traditional IP (e.g., Nezha's rebellious spirit) while adjusting peripheral content and values to align with contemporary mainstream values, rather than pursuing either rigid faithfulness to the original or unconstrained over-adaptation.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core technical terms are explained below for beginner audiences:
1. **`Flat character`**: A literary concept coined by British theorist E.M. Forster in his 1927 work *Aspects of the Novel*, referring to a fictional character defined by a single, fixed dominant personality trait, with no complex inner world or character growth arc throughout the work. Common examples include purely evil villains with no redeeming qualities, or perfect heroes with no flaws. This type of character is easy for audiences to recognize and categorize, but lacks realism.
2. **`Round character`**: Also from Forster's character typology, referring to a multi-dimensional, complex fictional character with contradictory personality traits, inner struggles, and clear character growth, similar to real human beings. A qualified round character can surprise audiences without violating logical rationality, and can trigger deeper emotional resonance.
3. **Traditional cultural IP adaptation**: The process of re-creating pre-existing traditional cultural intellectual property (including myth stories, classic literary characters, folk tales, etc.) into new artistic works (such as films, TV series, games) for contemporary audiences, requiring balance between retaining the core recognizable features of the original IP and adjusting content to meet current audience aesthetic and value needs.
4. **`Nezha Conquers the Dragon King` (1979)**: A landmark hand-drawn 2D animated film produced by Shanghai Animation Film Studio, representative of the "Chinese Animation School" with strong traditional Chinese aesthetic features (such as ink painting style, traditional folk music soundtrack). It is adapted from chapters 12-14 of the Ming Dynasty classic novel *Investiture of the Gods*.
5. **`Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child` (2019)**: A commercially successful 3D computer-animated film, with a total box office of over 5 billion RMB, ranking among the highest-grossing films in Chinese film history. It makes large-scale innovative adjustments to the traditional Nezha myth to cater to modern audience demand.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The paper cites the following core foundational studies as theoretical support:
1. E.M. Forster's character typology theory (flat/round character): Used as the core theoretical basis for comparative analysis of character design between the two films.
2. Zhu Guangqian's tragic protagonist theory: The prominent Chinese aesthetician argues that an ideal tragic protagonist is neither completely innocent nor completely guilty, so their suffering can arouse audiences' fear and pity without damaging audiences' sense of justice. This theory is used to analyze the tragic narrative design of the 1979 *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King*.
3. Core film theory textbooks: Cited works include *Foreign Film Theory Collections* (Li Hengji, Yang Yuanying, 2006), *Introduction to Film and Television Criticism* (Shen Yizhen, 2004), *Film and Television Criticism* (Li Daoxin, 2002), and *Outline of Film and Television Criticism* (Yuan Zhizhong, Yu Ji, 2009), which provide theoretical support for narrative analysis and aesthetic evaluation.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The adaptation of traditional cultural IP in Chinese animation has gone through three main developmental stages:
1. **Early planned economy stage (1950s-1990s)**: Represented by Shanghai Animation Film Studio works, adaptations prioritize faithful inheritance of traditional story connotations, use traditional Chinese art techniques (ink painting, paper-cutting, shadow puppetry), position children as the core audience, adopt flat character design and highlight traditional collectivist values.
2. **Market transition stage (2000s-2014)**: The Chinese animation industry begins market-oriented reform, and adaptations attempt to integrate commercial elements, but most fail to balance cultural inheritance and commercial viability, with few successful cases.
3. **Modern all-age market stage (2015-present)**: Represented by works such as *Monkey King: Hero is Back* (2015) and *Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child* (2019), adaptations target all-age audiences, use mature 3D animation technology, adopt round character design, integrate modern individualist values, and achieve both commercial success and cultural influence.
   This paper's research is located at the early stage of the modern stage, summarizing successful adaptation experience to guide subsequent industry practice.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior related studies, this paper has three core innovations:
1. **Cross-era comparative perspective**: Unlike most prior single-film analysis, this paper compares two landmark works across 40 years, effectively revealing the evolution law of adaptation strategies with changes in social and industrial context.
2. **Systematic multi-dimensional analysis framework**: Prior studies mostly focus on either narrative analysis or aesthetic analysis, while this paper integrates character, narrative, aesthetic, and value connotation analysis for a more comprehensive evaluation.
3. **Practice-oriented conclusion**: The paper connects text-level adaptation choices to real audience demand and market performance, rather than conducting pure theoretical text analysis, making its conclusions highly practical for the animation industry.

# 4. Methodology
This paper adopts a qualitative comparative research method, with no mathematical formulas involved.
## 4.1. Principles
The core idea of the research is to take the two most representative, highly successful Nezha-themed animated films as typical research samples, conduct multi-dimensional horizontal comparative analysis of their content, and combine text analysis with the study of the social, industrial, and audience aesthetic background of their release eras, to extract generalizable rules for modern adaptation of Chinese animated traditional IP.
The theoretical basis of the method includes Forster's character typology theory, film narrative theory, and contemporary film and television criticism theory, to ensure the rigor and academic validity of the analysis.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research is implemented in three sequential steps:
### Step 1: Typical sample selection
The paper selects *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* (1979) and *Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child* (2019) as research samples for three reasons:
- Both are the most commercially and culturally successful Nezha-themed animated films in their respective eras, with wide public recognition and representativeness.
- They are produced in completely different eras (planned economy era vs. market economy era, hand-drawn 2D animation era vs. 3D computer animation era), so their adaptation strategies can effectively reflect the changes of adaptation concepts over time.
- Both are adapted from the same traditional Nezha myth source, so their differences can be clearly attributed to adaptation choices rather than differences in original IP.
### Step 2: Multi-dimensional comparative analysis
The paper conducts comparative analysis of the two samples from three core dimensions:
#### Dimension 1: Character image analysis
Based on Forster's flat/round character typology, compare the character setting of all main characters (Nezha, Ao Bing, Taiyi Zhenren, Li Jing, Nezha's mother Yin Shiniang) between the two films, analyze the structural differences in character design, and link these differences to the changes in audience aesthetic preference between the two eras.
#### Dimension 2: Image narrative analysis
Compare the narrative structure (linear narrative vs. multi-line dual perspective narrative), narrative tone (tragic vs. comedy-inclined), color and composition application, and soundtrack style between the two films, analyze the differences in narrative strategy, and explore the correlation between these differences and market demand changes.
#### Dimension 3: Value connotation analysis
Compare the values conveyed by the two films, analyze their respective inheritance and innovation of traditional values (filial piety, collective interest priority, rebellious spirit against power), and explore how the 2019 version integrates modern social values (individual resistance to fate, importance of native family, individual self-realization) to cater to contemporary audiences.
### Step 3: Induction of general adaptation rules
Combine the comparative results with the development background of the Chinese animation industry, summarize the core rules of modern adaptation for Chinese animated traditional IP, and put forward practical guiding suggestions for subsequent adaptation practice.

# 5. Experimental Setup
As a humanities qualitative research work, this paper does not use quantitative experimental methods, datasets, or evaluation metrics in the natural science sense. The corresponding research design elements are as follows:
## 5.1. Datasets (Research Samples)
The two film samples are described in detail below:
### Sample 1: *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* (1979)
- Production: Shanghai Animation Film Studio, hand-drawn 2D animation, runtime 65 minutes.
- Core character settings: Nezha is a perfect, brave young hero with no flaws; the Dragon King is a purely evil villain who harms civilians; Li Jing (Nezha's father) is a rigid, patriarchal official who prioritizes collective interest over his son; Taiyi Zhenren is an elegant, faultless immortal.
- Core plot: Nezha kills the evil Dragon Prince Ao Bing to protect civilians, then commits suicide by cutting his flesh and returning it to his parents to save Chentang Pass from the Dragon King's flood revenge, finally is reborn with a lotus body and defeats the Dragon King.
### Sample 2: *Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child* (2019)
- Production: Coloroom Pictures, 3D computer animation, runtime 110 minutes.
- Core character settings: Nezha is a mischievous, misunderstood "problem child" reincarnated from a demon pill, eager for public recognition; Ao Bing is a kind, gentle young man reincarnated from a spirit pill, but burdened by the dragon clan's mission to escape from underwater imprisonment; Taiyi Zhenren is a kind but careless immortal who often makes mistakes when drunk, speaks Sichuan dialect; Li Jing is a tough-on-the-outside, soft-on-the-inside father who is willing to sacrifice his own life to save Nezha; Yin Shiniang (Nezha's mother) is a brave, loving woman who takes on most of the parenting work, similar to modern mother figures.
- Core plot: Due to Taiyi Zhenren's mistake, Nezha is reincarnated from the demon pill instead of the spirit pill, and is destined to be destroyed by a heavenly tribulation after 3 years. He fights against his fate, and finally joins hands with Ao Bing to resist the heavenly tribulation, saving Chentang Pass civilians.
### Rationale for sample selection
Both samples are the most influential Nezha-themed animated works in their respective eras, and are adapted from the same original myth source, so they are suitable for controlled comparative analysis to reveal the evolution of adaptation strategies.
## 5.2. Evaluation Metrics (Qualitative Evaluation Criteria)
The paper uses three core qualitative evaluation criteria to assess adaptation effect:
1. **Character richness**: Evaluates whether character design is three-dimensional, has complex personality and reasonable growth arc, and conforms to the cognitive level and aesthetic preference of contemporary audiences.
2. **Narrative appeal**: Evaluates whether narrative logic is smooth, can arouse audience emotional resonance, and matches the viewing demand of the target audience group.
3. **Value adaptation effect**: Evaluates whether the work retains the core spiritual connotation of the original traditional IP, while conforming to the mainstream values of contemporary society.
   These are qualitative criteria with no standardized mathematical calculation formulas, and are evaluated through close text analysis of film content combined with the social background of the release era.
## 5.3. Baselines (Comparison Reference)
The baseline for comparison is the original Nezha myth recorded in the Ming Dynasty novel *Investiture of the Gods*, which contains core plot elements (Nezha making trouble in the East Sea, cutting flesh to return to parents, lotus rebirth) and core spiritual connotation (rebellious spirit against unjust power). The adaptation strategies of the two films are compared against this baseline to measure their respective inheritance and innovation of the original IP.

# 6. Results & Analysis
This paper presents all results through qualitative comparative analysis, with no quantitative tables or statistical data.
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The comparative analysis results from three dimensions are as follows:
### Dimension 1: Character image comparison
- 1979 version: All main characters are typical flat characters, with clear binary distinction between good and evil, no complex inner conflicts. Minor characters are purely functional, only existing to promote plot development, with no independent personality. This design conforms to the aesthetic preference of 1970s Chinese audiences, who were accustomed to type-cast character settings and prioritized viewing mythological visual spectacles over emotional experience.
- 2019 version: All main characters are typical round characters, with multi-faceted, contradictory personalities and clear growth arcs. For example, Nezha has both mischievous, destructive demonic traits and kind, justice-seeking human traits; Ao Bing has both kind intentions to help others and selfish motives to fulfill his clan's mission. This design conforms to modern audiences' demand for realistic, relatable characters, as contemporary audiences reject non-black-and-white binary character settings and hope to see characters with inner struggles similar to real people.
### Dimension 2: Image narrative comparison
- 1979 version: Adopts single-line linear narrative, focusing on tragic expression. The climax is Nezha's suicide scene, which uses dark blue and black color tones, and a composition of merged sea and sky to create a heavy, depressive tragic atmosphere. The soundtrack uses traditional Chinese musical instruments (guzheng, suona) to highlight traditional aesthetic features.
- 2019 version: Adopts dual-line narrative from the perspectives of Nezha and Ao Bing, integrating a large number of comedy elements (such as Taiyi Zhenren forgetting the password of the lotus treasure, Shen Gongbao stuttering when speaking important lines) to increase entertainment value. The climax is Nezha and Ao Bing jointly resisting the heavenly tribulation, with bright color tones in the *Mountains and Rivers Map* fight scene, and 3D animation technology to create a shocking visual effect. The soundtrack uses modern pop music to cater to the aesthetic preference of young audiences.
### Dimension 3: Value connotation comparison
- 1979 version: Mostly retains traditional values from the original myth, highlights the traditional virtues of filial piety and sacrificing individual interest for collective interest, retains Nezha's rebellious spirit against evil power, but retains the setting of Nezha submitting to external pressure to commit suicide, reflecting the traditional value of prioritizing collective interest over individual life.
- 2019 version: Breaks through traditional ethical constraints, shifts the core of value expression to individual self-realization, and sublimates Nezha's rebellious spirit from resistance to specific evil power to resistance to pre-destined fate, with the core line "If fate is unfair, fight it to the end" resonating strongly with contemporary young people's pursuit of individual autonomy. It also integrates modern social concerns such as the influence of native family love on child growth, making the value connotation more relatable to modern audiences.
### Comparison against the original myth baseline
Both films retain the core spiritual connotation of Nezha's rebellious spirit. The 1979 version is more faithful to the original plot, while the 2019 version makes larger innovative adjustments to the plot and value connotation to meet contemporary demand, achieving better commercial performance and audience reception.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
The paper's core conclusions are as follows:
1. The adaptation strategy of Chinese animated traditional IP has undergone systematic evolution with the development of the times: character design has shifted from flat type-cast characters to multi-dimensional round characters, narrative mode has shifted from linear tragic myth narrative to multi-line comedy-inclined realistic narrative, and value connotation has shifted from prioritizing traditional collectivist values to highlighting modern individualist values.
2. The core principle of successful modern adaptation is to retain the core recognizable spiritual connotation of the traditional IP (such as Nezha's rebellious spirit), while adjusting peripheral content, narrative mode, and value expression to cater to the aesthetic and value needs of contemporary audiences, rather than pursuing either rigid faithfulness to the original or unconstrained over-adaptation.
3. Shifting from myth-oriented to reality-oriented adaptation, and balancing cultural inheritance, creative innovation, and commercial viability, is the inevitable trend of high-quality development of Chinese animated film adaptation, and is also the core reason for the huge success of *Ne Zha: Birth of the Demon Child* (2019).
   The paper's significance lies in summarizing practical experience from successful cases, providing systematic theoretical guidance for subsequent adaptation of Chinese traditional cultural IP, and promoting the high-quality development of the Chinese animation industry.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations
1. Small sample size: The paper only selects two Nezha-themed works as research samples, so the conclusion may not be fully applicable to all types of traditional IP adaptation (such as adaptation of historical stories, folk tales, etc.).
2. Lack of empirical audience research: The paper's analysis of audience demand is based on speculative inference of era background, without empirical data such as audience surveys or box office structure analysis to verify the correlation between adaptation strategies and actual audience reception effect.
3. Insufficient discussion of negative effects of adaptation: The paper does not conduct in-depth analysis of the possible negative effects of large-scale innovative adaptation, such as the risk of teenagers misunderstanding traditional cultural content due to excessive adaptation.
### Future Research Directions
1. Expand research samples: Include more traditional IP adaptation works of different types, eras, and commercial performance levels, to further verify and improve the adaptation rules summarized in this paper.
2. Add empirical audience research: Combine text analysis with audience survey data, box office data, and online comment sentiment analysis, to make research conclusions more objective and practical.
3. Explore the balance between cultural inheritance and commercialization: Further study how to avoid excessive commercialization leading to the loss of traditional cultural connotation in adaptation, and realize the dual goals of cultural communication and commercial success.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
### Inspirations
The paper's research perspective of combining text analysis with era background, audience demand, and industrial environment is very valuable, avoiding the common problem of pure text analysis divorced from reality in traditional film studies. The multi-dimensional adaptation evaluation framework constructed in this paper can be widely used in the evaluation of other traditional IP adaptation works, not only in the animation field, but also in live-action film, TV series, and game adaptation fields.
### Transferability
The core adaptation rules summarized in this paper have strong transferability beyond Chinese animation: for any country or region's adaptation of traditional cultural IP, the core principle of retaining the core spiritual connotation of the original IP while adjusting content to meet contemporary audience demand is universally applicable. For example, the adaptation of European and American mythological IP such as Greek mythology and Norse mythology also follows the same rule of integrating modern values into traditional stories.
### Potential Issues for Improvement
The paper's conclusion that the shift from traditional soundtrack to modern pop soundtrack can be seen as both innovation and retrogression is not analyzed in depth. How to balance the application of traditional aesthetic elements (such as traditional music, art style) and modern technology in adaptation to retain cultural identity while catering to modern audiences is a key issue that needs further exploration. In addition, the paper does not discuss the boundary of innovative adaptation: excessive adaptation that completely deviates from the core connotation of traditional IP will lose the significance of using traditional IP, and this boundary needs to be further clarified in future research.
