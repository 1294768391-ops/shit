## 1.5. Abstract
### Research Objective
To fill the research gap of insufficient systematic analysis of domestic Chinese animated hero images (in contrast to abundant existing research on Japanese and American animated heroes), and to diagnose existing problems and propose optimization paths for Chinese animated hero portrayal.
### Core Methodology
The paper adopts a qualitative research paradigm, including typology classification, cross-case comparative analysis, and cultural connotation interpretation, analyzing from three dimensions: type evolution and portrayal flaws of heroes, cultural connotations of hero images, and significance and prospects of hero shaping.
### Main Results
1.  Chinese animated film heroes are divided into three categories: heroes derived from ancient literature/folk legends, heroes adapted from modern and contemporary art works, and original heroes, with original heroes showing a growing trend due to high creative freedom.
2.  Long-standing flaws in Chinese animated hero portrayal include infantilization, oversimplification, excessive moralizing, and lack of three-dimensional personality and growth arcs, leading to low audience immersion.
3.  The cultural connotations of hero images are dynamic, and adjusting traditional values to align with universal values can significantly improve cross-cultural acceptance of animated works.
### Key Conclusions
Chinese animated films should balance innovation in narrative techniques and production models with adherence to local cultural roots and educational functions, and optimize hero portrayal to enhance the overall quality, market competitiveness, and cultural export capacity of the domestic animation industry.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
For a long time, Chinese animated films have fallen into the bottleneck of low-quality hero portrayal, with flat, unrelatable hero characters that limit the audience scope, market performance, and cultural communication effect of domestic animated works.
### Importance of the Problem
Hero characters are the core driving force of animated film plot development, carry positive mainstream values and educational functions especially for young audiences, and are a core carrier of cultural export for the animation industry. In the context of global cultural competition, high-quality animated hero images are critical to enhancing the international influence of Chinese culture.
### Gap in Prior Research
Most existing studies on animated heroes focus on Japanese and American works, and there is a lack of systematic, multi-dimensional analysis of the evolution, flaws, connotations and development paths of Chinese domestic animated hero images.
### Innovative Entry Point
The paper combines criticism of existing domestic works with comparative analysis of successful foreign adaptations of Chinese cultural IP, and uses typical positive cases of domestic works to put forward practical, actionable optimization suggestions for the industry, rather than purely theoretical analysis.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1.  Establishes the first systematic classification framework for Chinese animated film heroes, and identifies the growing trend of original hero characters.
2.  Diagnoses the core structural flaws in domestic animated hero portrayal, pointing out that the lack of a mature hero narrative system (rather than lack of cultural resources) is the key cause of low-quality works.
3.  Reveals the dynamic nature of hero image connotations, and verifies through cases that balancing local cultural traits and universal values is an effective path to improve cross-cultural acceptance of animated works.
4.  Proposes a clear development path for domestic animated hero creation that balances cultural heritage, educational function and commercial value, verified by the successful case of *Monkey King: Hero is Back*.
### Key Conclusions
Chinese animated films can break through current development bottlenecks by optimizing hero portrayal: they should retain the educational function and cultural roots of animation, while innovating narrative structures and adjusting value connotations to adapt to modern audience aesthetics and cross-cultural communication needs, so as to enhance the overall competitiveness of the domestic animation industry.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
For beginners to understand this paper, the following core concepts are defined first:
1.  **Animated hero character**: A core fictional character in animated films that drives plot development, embodies positive mainstream values, and evokes admiration and empathy from audiences. Unlike live-action film heroes, animated heroes can be non-human (animals, plants, mythical creatures) due to the high stylistic freedom of animation production.
2.  **Cultural IP (Intellectual Property)**: Creative content derived from existing cultural assets (such as ancient myths, folk tales, classic literary works) that has widely recognized cultural value and audience recognition, which can be adapted into different forms of media content including animated films.
3.  **Hero narrative**: A structural storytelling framework used to portray a hero's growth process, which is a core component of commercial animated film storytelling.
4.  **Universal values**: Values that are widely accepted across different cultural groups, such as justice, courage, family love, and sacrifice for the collective, which enable media content to be accepted by cross-cultural audiences.
5.  **Hero's Journey (Monomyth)**: A universal narrative structure identified by mythologist Joseph Campbell, which is widely used in Hollywood commercial films (including animated films) to create immersive, relatable hero characters. The standard structure is as follows:
    \$
    \text{Hero's Journey} = \text{Ordinary World} \rightarrow \text{Call to Adventure} \rightarrow \text{Refusal of the Call} \rightarrow \text{Meeting the Mentor} \rightarrow \text{Crossing the Threshold} \rightarrow \text{Tests, Allies, Enemies} \rightarrow \text{Approach to the Inmost Cave} \rightarrow \text{Ordeal} \rightarrow \text{Reward} \rightarrow \text{The Road Back} \rightarrow \text{Resurrection} \rightarrow \text{Return with the Elixir}
    \$
    Explanation: This structure depicts the complete growth process of a hero from an ordinary person to a mature hero who completes his mission, which can maximize audience immersion and resonance. This is the core framework used in successful American animated films mentioned in the paper such as *Kung Fu Panda*.

## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The evolution of Chinese animated hero portrayal can be divided into three stages, and this paper's research is located at the transition point between the bottleneck stage and revival stage:
1.  **Golden Age (1940s-1990s)**: Heroes are mostly adapted from traditional cultural IP, with distinct cultural traits and clear educational functions, e.g., Sun Wukong in *Uproar in Heaven* (1961), Nezha in *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* (1979). These works are widely recognized internationally for their unique artistic style.
2.  **Bottleneck Stage (2000s-early 2010s)**: The quality of domestic animated films declines, with hero characters being flat, overly childish, and lacking depth, as described in the paper (e.g., *Legend of A Rabbit* 2011). Most works fail to attract adult audiences, limiting the market size of the domestic animation industry.
3.  **Revival Stage (post-2015)**: Domestic animated films begin to innovate in hero portrayal, combining traditional IP with modern narrative techniques and values, represented by *Monkey King: Hero is Back* (2015) cited in the paper, and later works such as *Ne Zha* (2019).

# 4. Methodology
This paper adopts a qualitative research paradigm, with no quantitative experimental design or mathematical formulas. Its core methodology is deconstructed as follows:
## 4.1. Principles
The core idea of the paper is to combine typology classification, cross-case comparative analysis, and cultural connotation interpretation to systematically diagnose the existing problems of Chinese animated hero portrayal, and explore feasible optimization paths based on successful experiences at home and abroad. Its theoretical basis includes animated film narrative theory, cross-cultural communication theory, and hero narrative theory (Campbell's Hero's Journey framework).
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research is carried out in four sequential steps:
### Step 1: Typology classification of Chinese animated heroes
The authors first sort all existing Chinese animated film heroes into three mutually exclusive categories, and analyze their evolutionary trend:
1.  **Heroes derived from ancient literature or folk legends**: Adapted from classic Chinese literary works, myths or folk tales, with high cultural recognition among Chinese audiences, e.g., Sun Wukong from *Uproar in Heaven* (1961), Nezha from *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* (1979).
2.  **Heroes adapted from modern and contemporary art works**: Adapted from modern and contemporary novels, comics or other art works, endowed with modern values recognized by contemporary audiences, different from traditional hero traits, e.g., the protagonist of *Tibetan Mastiff Duoji* (2011).
3.  **Original heroes**: Created entirely by animation production teams, not based on existing IP, with maximum creative freedom, e.g., Gaiya and Sai Xiaoxi from the *Seer* animated film series. The authors find that the number of original heroes is increasing over time due to their high creative flexibility and extensibility of the story world.
### Step 2: Comparative diagnosis of hero portrayal flaws
The authors compare the hero portrayal of representative domestic animated films with successful American animated adaptations of Chinese cultural IP (*Mulan*, *Kung Fu Panda*) to identify core flaws:
1.  **Narrative structure flaw**: Domestic animated heroes often lack a reasonable growth arc. For example, the protagonist Tu'er in *Legend of A Rabbit* (2011) obtains top martial arts skills and a leadership token directly from a dying master at the beginning of the film, without any growth process, which greatly reduces audience immersion. In contrast, *Kung Fu Panda*'s protagonist Po has a complete Hero's Journey growth arc, showing his gradual progress in martial arts and personality, which is far more relatable to audiences.
2.  **Content flaw**: Domestic animated films have long-standing problems of infantilization, oversimplification, and excessive moralizing, leading to flat, non-three-dimensional hero characters that fail to resonate with adult audiences, greatly limiting the audience scope of animated works.
### Step 3: Dynamic connotation analysis of hero images
The authors analyze the dynamic nature of hero connotations across time and space, and verify the feasibility of cross-cultural adaptation of hero values through cases:
1.  **Spatial difference of connotations**: Heroes in Chinese martial arts culture have different connotations depending on their identity: folk heroes are characterized by upholding justice and helping the weak, while heroes in official positions focus on maintaining social order and protecting the public.
2.  **Temporal difference of connotations**: In times of crisis (war, natural disaster), heroes who can turn the tide are most popular; in peaceful times, ordinary people with positive traits can also be civilian heroes. The authors use the *McDull* series as a case: McDull is an ordinary, even "failed" pig character, but his persistence in pursuing small goals and love for his mother make him a civilian hero that resonates strongly with ordinary audiences.
3.  **Cross-cultural adaptation of connotations**: The authors use *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* as a case: the original story in the classic *Investiture of the Gods* depicts Nezha committing suicide to return his body to his parents as an act of feudal filial piety, which is unacceptable to modern and non-Chinese audiences. The animated adaptation changes his motivation to sacrificing himself to protect civilians from the dragon king's disaster, making the connotation of justice and self-sacrifice universally accepted, leading to awards both domestically and internationally.
### Step 4: Development path verification based on successful cases
The authors take *Monkey King: Hero is Back* (2015) as a positive case to verify the feasibility of the proposed optimization path: the film creates two heroes (the initially decadent Sun Wukong who regains his strength and faith, and the weak but brave child Jiang Liuer), combines traditional IP, modern aesthetic, educational value and commercial elements, achieving both box office success and critical acclaim, proving that high-quality hero portrayal can achieve multiple values simultaneously.

## 5.2. Evaluation Criteria (Equivalent to Evaluation Metrics)
The authors use three core qualitative evaluation criteria for hero portrayal quality:
1.  **Audience immersion**: The degree to which audiences can relate to and empathize with the hero character, assessed by whether the hero has a reasonable growth arc and authentic, multi-dimensional personality traits.
2.  **Cultural adaptability**: The degree to which the hero's connotation can be accepted by both domestic and cross-cultural audiences, assessed by whether the values embodied by the hero balance local cultural characteristics and universal values.
3.  **Value realization**: The degree to which the hero character achieves both educational function (transmitting positive mainstream values) and commercial value (driving box office and IP derivative revenue), assessed by audience feedback, industry awards and box office performance.
    There are no standardized mathematical formulas for these qualitative criteria, as they are assessed through critical analysis of multiple data sources including audience reviews, box office data and award records.
## 5.3. Comparative Baselines
The baselines for comparison are:
1.  The mature hero portrayal system of American animated films (represented by *Mulan* and *Kung Fu Panda*), which has been verified by global market success.
2.  Classic high-quality Chinese animated works of the last century (represented by *Uproar in Heaven* and *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King*), which represent the highest level of traditional Chinese animated creation.
    These baselines are representative because they have been widely recognized by global audiences and the industry, and can serve as a reference standard for high-quality hero portrayal.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The main analytical results of the paper are:
1.  **Type evolution result**: Original heroes are growing in number, but traditional IP heroes still have greater audience recognition advantages, as seen in the huge success of *Monkey King: Hero is Back*, which is adapted from the classic *Journey to the West* IP.
2.  **Flaw diagnosis result**: The core reason for the poor performance of Chinese animated heroes in the 2000s to early 2010s is the lack of a mature narrative system, rather than lack of cultural resources. For example, *Legend of A Rabbit* copied the surface setting of *Kung Fu Panda* (animal protagonist, martial arts theme) but failed to replicate the complete hero growth arc, leading to poor audience reception.
3.  **Connotation adjustment result**: Adjusting traditional hero connotations to highlight universal values can significantly improve cross-cultural acceptance, as proven by the international awards won by *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King*, and the global success of American adaptations of Chinese IP *Mulan* and *Kung Fu Panda*.
4.  **Development path validation result**: The success of *Monkey King: Hero is Back* proves that the path of combining traditional cultural IP, modern narrative techniques, universal value connotations, and mature commercial operation is feasible, and can achieve educational, cultural and commercial goals simultaneously.
    **Comparison with baselines**: Compared to American animated hero portrayal, Chinese animated films still have gaps in narrative structure maturity and commercial operation, but have unique advantages in rich traditional cultural resources. Compared to classic Chinese animated works of the last century, contemporary works need to better balance traditional values with modern audience aesthetic preferences.

## 6.3. Case Validity Analysis (Equivalent to Ablation Studies)
The authors verify the robustness of their conclusions by comparing different adaptations of the same IP or similar themes:
1.  Comparison of different portrayals of Sun Wukong: The Sun Wukong in *Uproar in Heaven* (1961) is portrayed as a rebellious hero against feudal authority, which fits the social values of the 1960s. The Sun Wukong in *Monkey King: Hero is Back* (2015) is portrayed as a fallen hero who regains his faith and strength because of the influence of the child Jiang Liuer, which fits the modern audience's preference for character growth and emotional resonance. The different positive reception of the two portrayals confirms that hero connotations need to be adjusted to match the values of the times.
2.  Comparison of *Legend of A Rabbit* and *Kung Fu Panda*: Both have animal protagonists and martial arts themes, but the gap in narrative structure (complete growth arc vs. no growth arc) directly leads to the gap in audience reception, confirming that a complete growth arc is a core component of successful hero portrayal.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper provides the first systematic, multi-dimensional analysis of hero characters in Chinese animated films. It classifies domestic animated heroes into three categories, identifies core flaws in their portrayal including infantilization, oversimplification, excessive moralizing, and lack of three-dimensional growth arcs, reveals the dynamic and adjustable nature of hero cultural connotations, and proposes a clear development path: Chinese animated films should balance "change" (innovative themes, narrative techniques, production models) and "unchangeable" (adhere to cultural roots, educational function, and domestic market orientation), optimize hero portrayal by combining traditional cultural resources with modern narrative techniques and universal values, to enhance the overall quality, market competitiveness and cultural export capacity of domestic animated films. The success of *Monkey King: Hero is Back* proves the feasibility of this path.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations
1.  The paper's cases only cover works up to 2015, and do not include analysis of later high-profile domestic animated films such as *Ne Zha* (2019) and *Jiang Ziya* (2020), which have made further innovations in hero portrayal.
2.  The paper focuses primarily on Han Chinese cultural IP, with very limited analysis of hero characters based on Chinese ethnic minority cultural resources.
3.  The analysis is purely qualitative, lacking support from quantitative data such as audience surveys or box office correlation analysis, which reduces the persuasiveness of the conclusions to some extent.
### Future Research Directions Suggested
1.  Further explore the path of adapting traditional cultural IP to modern and cross-cultural contexts, combining quantitative audience research to verify the acceptance of different hero portrayal strategies.
2.  Study the differentiated hero portrayal strategies for animated films targeting different audience groups (children, young adults, family audiences).
3.  Explore the application of new technologies (such as 3D animation, virtual production, AI-generated content) in hero character design and portrayal.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
### Inspirations
The paper's analysis of the balance between local cultural characteristics and universal values is applicable not only to animated films, but also to other cultural and creative products such as TV series, games and cultural derivatives that aim for cross-cultural communication. The conclusion that a complete hero growth arc is the core of audience resonance is a universal rule for commercial narrative works worldwide, which has strong reference value for content creators.
### Potential Issues for Improvement
1.  The paper does not analyze the impact of policy factors on Chinese animated film hero portrayal, which is an important factor affecting the creative direction of the Chinese cultural industry, and could be added to subsequent research.
2.  The paper assumes that the educational function of animated films must be retained, but it does not discuss how to balance educational function and entertainment function effectively, to avoid the problem of excessive moralizing that reduces audience acceptance.
3.  The paper does not analyze the commercial operation system of animated films (such as marketing, IP derivative development), which is also an important part of shaping successful hero IP, as seen in the American animation industry.
