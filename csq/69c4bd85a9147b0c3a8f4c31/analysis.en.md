# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem
The paper addresses the long-standing pain point of traditional Chinese myth IP adaptation: how to balance the retention of traditional cultural core connotations and the integration of modern values to resonate with contemporary audiences. Prior adaptations often fall into two extremes: either rigidly following old feudal-era narratives that feel outdated to modern viewers, or over-modifying myths to the point that they lose their unique cultural identity.
### Importance of the Problem
Traditional Chinese myths are core national cultural resources, and their effective transformation is critical for enhancing the competitiveness of the Chinese cultural industry and promoting Chinese cultural output globally. Prior research gaps include a lack of systematic, multi-dimensional analytical frameworks for myth adaptation success, and insufficient focus on the social reflection value of animated myth works beyond commercial performance.
### Innovative Entry Point
The paper takes the phenomenon-level hit *Ne Zha 2* (which broke the Chinese Spring Festival box office record with over 11 billion RMB in revenue, ranking 11th in global film box office history as of 2025) as a representative case, and analyzes its innovation from three interconnected dimensions: cultural, narrative, and social, rather than only evaluating its commercial or aesthetic performance separately.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1.  It constructs a three-dimensional analytical framework for modern myth IP adaptation, consisting of myth disenchantment, narrative rebellion, and cultural fable, which can be extended to analyze other traditional cultural resource transformation cases.
2.  It reveals the internal logic of *Ne Zha 2*'s cross-demographic success: it deconstructs outdated traditional power narratives while retaining core Eastern aesthetic and spiritual characteristics, and maps fictional plots to real contemporary social issues.
3.  It provides actionable reference for the Chinese animation industry and cultural practitioners, proving that traditional myth resources have huge regenerative potential in the contemporary cultural field.
### Key Conclusions
1.  *Ne Zha 2*’s subversive reconstruction of the traditional Ne Zha myth breaks the binary good-evil narrative stereotype, and constructs a human-centric narrative that focuses on individual identity and marginalized group resistance.
2.  The film’s success is not only a commercial breakthrough, but also a cultural breakthrough: it reshapes the expression paradigm of Chinese traditional culture, and reflects universal modern spiritual dilemmas including social prejudice, intergenerational family conflicts, and rigid social norm constraints.
3.  The adaptation path of *Ne Zha 2* can be replicated to promote the modern transformation of more traditional Chinese cultural IP.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
For beginners, all core technical terms in the paper are defined below:
1.  **Myth Disenchantment**: Derived from Max Weber’s sociological concept of "disenchantment of the world", which refers to the process of stripping away the sacred, absolute religious authority attached to traditional myth narratives and characters, and reinterpreting them from a secular, human-centric perspective.
2.  **Disciplinary Power**: A concept from Michel Foucault’s social theory, referring to invisible power mechanisms (including social norms, identity labels, institutional rules) that regulate, categorize, and control individual behavior and identity. In the context of the paper, it refers to the divine power system and social prejudice that labels Ne Zha and other demon characters as "inherently evil".
3.  **Counter-disciplinary Narrative**: A narrative strategy that challenges and subverts dominant disciplinary power structures, by portraying the resistance of oppressed individuals or groups against unfair identity labels and institutional rules.
4.  **Cultural Fable**: A fictional narrative (often fantastical) that implicitly reflects and comments on real-world social issues, human nature, and collective spiritual dilemmas of a specific era.
5.  **Cyborg Aesthetics**: Derived from Donna Haraway’s cyborg theory, which blurs the boundary between organic nature and artificial technology. In the paper, it refers to the aesthetic style that combines traditional Eastern cultural elements with digital animation technology effects to create a new hybrid visual experience.
6.  **IP (Intellectual Property) Transformation**: In the cultural industry, it refers to the process of adapting classic existing cultural content (such as myths, folk tales, literary works) into new cultural products (films, games, merchandise) while retaining the core identity of the original content.
## 3.2. Previous Works
### Key Prior Ne Zha Adaptations
1.  **Original Ming Dynasty *Investiture of the Gods (Fengshen Yanyi)***: The original source of the Ne Zha myth, in which Ne Zha’s rebellion is directed at the Dragon King, and he eventually "cuts his flesh to return to his mother and scrapes his bones to return to his father" to atone for his sins, and finally submits to the divine order, reflecting traditional feudal ethics of filial piety and hierarchy.
2.  **1979 Animated Film *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King***: A classic work of the Chinese Animation School, which frames Ne Zha’s suicide as a sacrifice to save local people from flood disasters, and his rebellion as a fight against feudal tyranny, with a clear binary opposition between good (Ne Zha, people) and evil (Dragon King, corrupt officials).
3.  **2019 *Ne Zha: I am the Destiny***: The prequel of *Ne Zha 2*, which first proposed the "my destiny is determined by myself" anti-fate narrative, focusing on Ne Zha’s individual struggle against the "demon pill" identity label and social prejudice, which was a breakthrough in traditional myth adaptation.
### Prior Related Research
Prior studies on Chinese animation myth adaptation mostly focus on single-dimensional analysis: either aesthetic style analysis, or commercial performance analysis, or cultural inheritance analysis, lacking a systematic framework that integrates art, culture, and social reflection.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The evolution of Chinese animated myth adaptation can be divided into three phases:
1.  **Phase 1 (1950s-1990s)**: Faithful adaptation phase, using hand-drawn animation technology, focusing on restoring traditional aesthetic styles and promoting traditional ethics, with little modification to original myth narratives.
2.  **Phase 2 (1990s-2015)**: Mixed exploration phase, influenced by Japanese and Western animation, works often over-modify traditional myths to cater to young audiences, losing core Chinese cultural characteristics, and failing to achieve wide market recognition.
3.  **Phase 3 (2015-present)**: Modern reconstruction phase, using 3D digital animation technology, integrating modern universal values while retaining traditional cultural roots, represented by works such as *Monkey King: Hero is Back (2015)*, *Chang'an 30,000 Li (2023)*, and the *Ne Zha* series. This paper’s analysis of *Ne Zha 2* is a study of the representative work of this latest phase.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior related studies, the core innovations of this paper are:
1.  It proposes a systematic three-dimensional analytical framework that integrates cultural deconstruction, narrative analysis, and social criticism, rather than single-dimensional evaluation.
2.  It expands the scope of analysis from individual character and plot to group resistance and social reflection, revealing the deeper social value of the film beyond entertainment.
3.  It combines film text analysis with real social response (box office, audience discussion) to verify the practical effectiveness of the film’s adaptation strategy, rather than only conducting subjective theoretical interpretation.

# 4. Methodology
This paper is a qualitative case study in the field of film and cultural studies, so it does not use mathematical formulas or quantitative experimental methods, which is standard for humanities research in this domain.
## 4.1. Principles
The core idea of the research is that the adaptation of traditional myth IP is not just a commercial or creative behavior, but a cultural reconstruction practice that reflects the collective spiritual demands of contemporary society. The theoretical basis of the methodology includes:
1.  Max Weber’s disenchantment theory to analyze the deconstruction of traditional divine authority in the film.
2.  Michel Foucault’s disciplinary power theory to analyze the anti-disciplinary narrative and identity construction of marginalized characters.
3.  Fable criticism theory to analyze the real social reflection function of the fictional myth narrative.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research process is divided into four sequential steps:
### Step 1: Representative Case Selection
The authors select *Ne Zha 2* as the research case for two reasons:
1.  It has unprecedented commercial success: as of February 15, 2025, its box office exceeded 11 billion RMB, making it the highest-grossing film in Chinese history, and the only Chinese film in the global top 11 box office rankings.
2.  It has high audience reputation: it scored 8.5/10 on Douban (China’s largest film rating platform) based on over 5 million user reviews, proving its wide cross-demographic recognition.
    These characteristics make it the most representative work of current Chinese animated myth adaptation, so the conclusions drawn from it have high reference value.
### Step 2: Systematic Textual Analysis of the Film
The authors conduct a full analysis of the film’s content, covering three aspects:
1.  **Narrative structure analysis**: Examine how the film rewrites the traditional Ne Zha myth plot, including setting the senior immortal Wuliang Xianweng as the main villain (rather than the traditional Dragon King), constructing a three-world narrative of immortals, humans, and demons, and designing a game-style level-breaking plot for Ne Zha and Ao Bing’s road to restore their physical bodies.
2.  **Character image analysis**: Analyze the multi-dimensional, non-stereotypical character design, including Ne Zha’s identity struggle between "demon" and "hero", Shen Gongbao’s tragic fate as a discriminated demon cultivator, the Dragon Clan’s oppressed status as meritorious but marginalized groups, and the intergenerational relationship changes in three family lines (Ne Zha’s family, Ao Bing’s family, Shen Gongbao’s family).
3.  **Aesthetic style analysis**: Analyze how the film uses digital animation technology (fluid simulation, particle system, etc.) to reconstruct traditional Eastern aesthetic scenes, such as the digital representation of Ne Zha’s physical nirvana, the "emergent aesthetic" of the demon army battle scene, etc.
### Step 3: Theoretical Mapping to the Three Analytical Dimensions
The authors map the textual features of the film to the three core analytical dimensions:
1.  **Myth Disenchantment Dimension**:
    -   **Deconstruction of divine power narrative**: The traditional sacred immortal class is portrayed as a hypocritical power group that creates prejudice and oppresses marginal groups to maintain their rule, breaking the traditional concept of "immortals are inherently just". The collapse of the Tianyuan Ding (symbol of divine authority) at the end of the film directly declares the defeat of traditional divine power.
    -   **Entertainment variation of sacred narrative**: The film uses postmodern comedy elements (such as Sichuan dialect for Taiyi Zhenren, fingerprint unlock for the magic pearl, double soul sharing one body setting) and game-style level-breaking narrative to dissolve the solemnity of traditional myths, making the story more approachable for contemporary young audiences.
    -   **Digital rewriting of Eastern aesthetics**: The film uses digital special effects to recreate classic violent scenes such as Ne Zha’s "bone scraping and flesh cutting" into a visually impactful but humanistic scene that balances violent aesthetics and emotional expression, realizing the cyborg-style rebirth of Eastern aesthetics.
2.  **Narrative Rebellion Dimension**:
    -   **Multiple suppression of disciplinary power**: The paper analyzes that Ne Zha is constrained by multiple disciplinary power symbols: the "Heavenly Tribulation Curse" and "Heart Piercing Curse" set by the immortal class, and the social prejudice label of "demon pill is inherently evil", all of which try to define his identity and limit his behavior.
    -   **Subjectivity reconstruction against discipline**: The paper traces Ne Zha’s awakening process: from rejecting his "demon" identity and longing to become an immortal to gain social recognition, to finally accepting his identity, shouting "so what if I am a demon", and breaking the disciplinary power system with his own strength, realizing individual spiritual liberation.
    -   **Screen struggle of stigmatized groups**: The paper points out that the film expands individual rebellion to collective resistance of marginalized groups: the oppressed Dragon Clan, discriminated demons, and even the villain Shen Gongbao finally join Ne Zha to break the Tianyuan Ding, completing the transformation from individual awakening to group liberation.
3.  **Cultural Fable Dimension**:
    -   **Human nature expression in good and evil choices**: The film points out that good and evil are not determined by identity or status: Ne Zha, labeled as an evil demon, chooses to save people, while the high-status immortal Wuliang Xianweng commits evil deeds, which reflects the reality of social prejudice in contemporary society.
    -   **Mirror writing of family relationship**: Three family lines in the film reflect common intergenerational communication dilemmas in contemporary Chinese families, including overly demanding "chicken baby" education (parents pushing children to achieve high academic/career goals regardless of their own wishes), and the transition from parent authority to equal communication between generations.
    -   **Breakthrough between rules and freedom**: Ne Zha’s struggle against unfair rules set by the immortal class reflects the modern demand of breaking rigid social norms and realizing individual value.
### Step 4: Value Generalization
The authors summarize the successful experience of *Ne Zha 2*, and put forward its reference significance for future Chinese animation creation and traditional myth IP transformation, as well as its enlightenment for reflecting contemporary social issues.

# 5. Experimental Setup
As a qualitative humanities study, this paper does not use traditional quantitative experimental setups, datasets, or metrics. The relevant research setup is explained below:
## 5.1. Research Materials (Equivalent to Datasets)
The "data" used in the study includes:
1.  **Core material**: The full 130-minute cut of *Ne Zha 2*.
2.  **Box office and reputation data**: Official box office statistics from the China Film Administration (11 billion RMB as of February 15, 2025), and Douban platform rating data (8.5/10 from 5 million+ users).
3.  **Comparative materials**: Original *Investiture of the Gods* myth text, 1979 *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King* animated film, 2019 *Ne Zha: I am the Destiny* animated film, which are used as baselines to highlight the innovation of *Ne Zha 2*.
    These materials are selected because they cover the full evolution of the Ne Zha myth narrative from the original source to the latest adaptation, ensuring the completeness and credibility of the comparative analysis.
## 5.2. Evaluation Framework (Equivalent to Evaluation Metrics)
The paper uses a three-dimensional qualitative evaluation framework, defined as follows:
1.  **Cultural Inheritance Indicator**: Measures the degree to which the film retains the core spiritual connotation of the traditional Ne Zha myth, including rebellion spirit, family responsibility, and Eastern aesthetic characteristics.
2.  **Creative Innovation Indicator**: Measures the degree to which the film adapts the traditional myth to modern values, including anti-prejudice, individual identity autonomy, equal rights for marginalized groups, etc.
3.  **Social Resonance Indicator**: Measures the degree to which the film reflects real contemporary social issues and resonates with audiences, verified by box office performance and public discussion data.
## 5.3. Baseline Adaptations (Equivalent to Baseline Models)
The paper compares *Ne Zha 2* with three representative baseline adaptations of the Ne Zha myth, which cover the main narrative paradigms of different historical periods:
1.  Original *Investiture of the Gods* myth (Ming Dynasty): Representative of the feudal-era traditional narrative paradigm, with strict hierarchy and filial piety as core values.
2.  1979 *Nezha Conquers the Dragon King*: Representative of the socialist-era adaptation paradigm, with class struggle and collective sacrifice as core values.
3.  2019 *Ne Zha: I am the Destiny*: Representative of the early 2010s individualism adaptation paradigm, with individual anti-fate struggle as the core value.
    These baselines are representative, so the comparison can clearly highlight the innovation of *Ne Zha 2*.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The main results of the analysis are as follows:
1.  **Myth Disenchantment Effect**: The film successfully dissolves the absolute authority of traditional divine power, and transforms the traditional "divine-centric" myth narrative into a "human-centric" narrative, while retaining Eastern aesthetic characteristics through digital technology, avoiding the loss of cultural roots that often occurs in over-modified adaptations.
2.  **Narrative Rebellion Effect**: The film breaks the traditional heroic narrative and binary good-evil stereotype, constructing an anti-disciplinary narrative that focuses on the identity struggle of marginalized groups. Compared to the 2019 prequel which only focuses on individual struggle, *Ne Zha 2* expands the narrative pattern to collective liberation, which has deeper social critical value.
3.  **Cultural Fable Effect**: The film effectively maps the fictional myth plot to three universal contemporary social issues: social prejudice against specific groups, intergenerational communication conflicts in families, and rigid social norm constraints on individual development, which is the core reason for its wide audience resonance and commercial success.

## 6.2. Supporting Evidence
The key evidence supporting the above results includes:
1.  **Commercial evidence**: 11 billion RMB box office and 8.5/10 Douban rating prove that the film is widely accepted by audiences of different age groups.
2.  **Narrative evidence**: The main villain Wuliang Xianweng is a high-status immortal, which directly subverts the traditional concept of "immortals are just"; the collective push of the Tianyuan Ding by Ne Zha, the Dragon Clan, demons, and Shen Gongbao proves the collective resistance narrative.
3.  **Character evidence**: Shen Gongbao, who was a flat villain in traditional narratives, is portrayed as a victim of systemic discrimination who finally sacrifices himself to save others, breaking the traditional binary good-evil stereotype; Ao Bing’s father changes from forcing his son to become an immortal to supporting his son’s own choice, which reflects the transformation of intergenerational relationship concepts.
## 6.3. Robustness Verification (Equivalent to Ablation Studies)
The authors verify the validity of their conclusions by cross-referencing with real social response data after the film’s release:
1.  Sina Weibo (China’s largest social media platform) data shows that keywords related to "social prejudice", "intergenerational communication", and "break rules" appeared more than 3 million times in discussions about the film, proving that the cultural fable function of the film is not a subjective interpretation of the authors, but a widely perceived effect by audiences.
2.  Audience survey data from the Chinese Film Audience Research Center shows that 78% of viewers said they resonated with the plot of Ne Zha fighting against prejudice, which further verifies the social resonance effect of the film.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
*Ne Zha 2* is a milestone work in the development of Chinese animation. Its unprecedented success comes from its three-dimensional innovative adaptation strategy for traditional myth:
1.  It disenchants traditional divine authority, rewriting the myth from a divine-centric narrative to a human-centric narrative, and realizes the digital rebirth of Eastern aesthetics through advanced animation technology.
2.  It constructs an anti-disciplinary narrative that breaks traditional heroic stereotypes and binary good-evil opposition, expanding from individual identity awakening to collective resistance of marginalized groups.
3.  It integrates profound cultural fables, mapping the fictional plot to real contemporary social issues, and resonating strongly with modern audiences.
    The film provides an innovative, replicable path for the contemporary transformation of Chinese traditional myth resources, proving that traditional cultural resources have huge regenerative potential in the contemporary cultural field.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The authors explicitly point out the following limitations of the study:
1.  The paper only uses *Ne Zha 2* as a single case study, so the generalizability of the conclusions needs to be verified by analyzing more myth adaptation cases of different types.
2.  The study mainly focuses on qualitative analysis of the film’s content and value, lacking large-scale quantitative audience research to measure the resonance degree of different demographic groups (such as different age groups, different regional audiences) with the film’s content.
3.  The paper does not discuss the cross-cultural communication effect of the film, i.e., whether its values and narrative can resonate with non-Chinese audiences in overseas markets.
    Suggested future research directions include:
1.  Expand the research sample to more Chinese myth adaptation works, and summarize a more general theoretical framework for traditional cultural IP transformation.
2.  Combine quantitative research methods such as large-scale audience questionnaires and sentiment analysis of social media comments to further analyze the communication effect of such films.
3.  Explore the cross-cultural adaptation path of Chinese myth IP, to promote the overseas spread of Chinese traditional culture.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
### Inspirations
1.  The three-dimensional analytical framework proposed in this paper can be applied to the analysis of traditional cultural IP adaptation in any cultural context, not just Chinese myths. It reveals that the core of successful traditional cultural adaptation is not only technical innovation, but also the integration of traditional cultural core with modern universal values (anti-prejudice, identity freedom, equal rights), which has reference value for cultural industry practitioners around the world.
2.  The study also proves that animated films are not just entertainment products for children, but can carry profound social criticism and cultural reflection functions, which expands the boundary of animated film value evaluation.
### Potential Improvements
1.  The paper overemphasizes the advantages of the film, and does not discuss its shortcomings in depth: for example, some viewers criticized that the film’s rhythm is too fast, the character development of supporting roles such as the Dragon King and Shen Gongbao is not sufficient, and the collective resistance narrative is slightly idealized. A more balanced analysis of both advantages and disadvantages would make the conclusions more objective.
2.  The paper does not discuss the commercial conditions behind the film’s success: it was released during the Chinese Spring Festival, which is the most lucrative film period in China, and it has the accumulated audience base of the 2019 prequel. These external commercial factors are also important reasons for its box office success, and ignoring them may overestimate the universality of its adaptation experience for other works released in non-peak periods or without prequel basis.
3.  The paper does not discuss the cultural discount issue of the film: its narrative relies heavily on Chinese audiences’ prior knowledge of the Ne Zha myth and Chinese social context, so its resonance effect may be limited when released in overseas markets, which is a direction for further research.
