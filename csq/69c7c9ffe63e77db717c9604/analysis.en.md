# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
After the decline of the award-winning "Chinese School" animation by the end of the 20th century, domestic Chinese animated films faced dual pressures: improving narrative artistic quality to compete with imported Japanese and American works, and delivering sustainable commercial returns under the new market-oriented system. Prior narrative practices were limited to single media platforms (e.g., only releasing content in cinemas), leading to widespread issues including high production risk, audience difficulty understanding complex story settings, and consistent box office losses.
### Importance of the Problem
The Chinese animated film market has grown rapidly since 2010, but there remains a large gap between domestic works and mature animation industries in Japan and the US. Cross-media narrative is a proven core strategy in mature markets to build high-value intellectual property (IP), reduce investment risk, and improve audience acceptance, but prior domestic research on this topic was scarce, and most industry applications stayed at a superficial, unplanned method level rather than a systematic strategic level.
### Innovative Entry Point
The paper uses a comparative case study of the *Kuiba* and *Pleasant Goat* animated film series (both released in 2011) to demonstrate how different levels of cross-media planning lead to drastically different narrative and commercial outcomes. It then proposes three actionable, China-specific paths to elevate cross-media narrative from an ad-hoc method to a core industrial strategy.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: It systematically adapts cross-media narrative theory to the Chinese domestic animation context, integrating Henry Jenkins' transmedia storytelling framework and Li Xianjie's media-narrative matching theory to define the core connotation of cross-media narrative for domestic animated films.
2. **Empirical Contribution**: It summarizes practical industry experience through case comparison: lack of pre-release cross-media narrative foreshadowing (as seen in *Kuiba*) leads to audience difficulty understanding complex worldbuilding and commercial failure, while systematic pre-release cross-media layout (as seen in *Pleasant Goat*) drastically improves audience acceptance and commercial returns.
3. **Strategic Contribution**: It proposes three specific implementation paths to elevate cross-media narrative from method to strategy: (1) use multiple media types for collaborative narrative, not limited to TV and film; (2) distinguish cross-media narrative from one-way static adaptation, emphasizing dynamic, interactive cross-platform narrative; (3) match narrative content to the unique characteristics of each media platform to maximize strengths.
4. **Context-Specific Finding**: The Chinese animation industry can rely on fast-growing internet media (online games, webcomics, short videos) to build cross-media narrative systems, compensating for the lack of a mature printed comic publishing ecosystem (a core foundation of Japanese and American animation industries) to reduce production risk.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core terms are defined for beginner readers below:
- **Narratology**: An academic discipline that studies the structure, function, and communication rules of narrative works, analyzing how stories are constructed and convey meaning to audiences. Originated in 1960s literary research, it was later extended to film, animation, gaming, and other media fields.
- **Cross-media narrative (transmedia narrative)**: A narrative strategy that first constructs a unified, consistent story world, then develops relatively independent but logically interconnected story content across multiple media platforms (TV, film, games, books, comics, short videos, etc.). Audiences gain a complete, layered story experience by engaging with content across different platforms, while character depth and story connotation are continuously enriched across media.
- **Chinese School of Animation**: A Chinese animation creation trend active from the 1950s to the 1980s, famous for its distinct traditional Chinese aesthetic (including ink painting, paper-cut, and puppet animation forms) and story materials drawn from Chinese myths, legends, and classical literature. Representative works like *Havoc in Heaven* won multiple international awards.
- **Animation IP (Intellectual Property)**: Animated content with high audience recognition and commercial value that can be developed across multiple media and derivative product lines to generate continuous revenue. Systematic cross-media narrative is the core foundation for building successful animation IP.
## 3.2. Previous Works
1. **Henry Jenkins' Transmedia Storytelling Theory (2003)**: Proposed by MIT media studies professor Henry Jenkins, the core argument is that entertainment content planning should prioritize audience experience across different media platforms. Around a unified story world, creators develop independent but logically connected narrative content across platforms to make characters more three-dimensional and immersive. Mature global IPs including *Star Wars* and the Marvel Cinematic Universe are built on this framework.
2. **Li Xianjie's Media-Narrative Matching Theory**: Professor Li Xianjie of Central China Normal University argued that "to a certain extent, the media determines the story", meaning the inherent characteristics of a media carrier shape suitable narrative methods. For example, written text excels at conveying internal monologue and abstract concepts, film and TV excel at immersive audio-visual presentation, and games excel at interactive experience. Cross-media narrative must match content to media characteristics to be effective.
3. **Prior Domestic Animation Narrative Research**: Before this paper, most studies of Chinese animated film narrative focused on single-platform text analysis and story writing skills, with almost no systematic research on cross-media narrative as an industrial development strategy, which is the core research gap this paper fills.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The development of cross-media narrative in the animation industry can be divided into three stages:
1. **Pre-digital era (pre-2000)**: Animation content was almost exclusively distributed on single platforms (TV animation only on TV, animated films only in cinemas). Cross-media activity was limited to one-way adaptation (e.g., adapting a popular comic to TV animation) with no systematic cross-platform narrative planning.
2. **Early digital era (2000-2010)**: With the rise of the internet, new media including online games and webcomics emerged. Mature animation industries in Japan and the US began to systematically apply cross-media narrative to build large IPs, such as the *Pokémon* franchise spanning games, TV, film, and merchandise.
3. **Chinese animation marketization era (post-2010)**: China completed the market-oriented reform of its animation industry, and began to test cross-media narrative, but most applications remained at an unplanned, primary stage. This paper's research was conducted at the start of this stage, providing early theoretical guidance for domestic industry practice.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior related research:
1. Unlike previous studies limited to single-platform narrative skills, this paper links cross-media narrative effect directly to industrial commercial performance, aligning closely with the practical needs of the market-oriented Chinese animation industry.
2. Unlike foreign cross-media narrative theories developed for mature Japanese and American industrial ecosystems, this paper proposes strategies tailored to China's specific context (lack of a printed comic market, fast-growing internet sector), with stronger practical relevance for domestic creators.
3. Unlike prior purely theoretical studies, this paper uses comparative case analysis of real domestic animation projects to verify the effect of cross-media narrative, making its conclusions more empirically persuasive.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The paper uses a three-stage research framework integrating theoretical sorting, comparative case analysis, and strategy construction:
1. First, it organizes existing cross-media narrative theories to define the core connotation of cross-media narrative suitable for the domestic animation industry.
2. Second, it compares the outcomes of typical domestic animated film projects with different levels of cross-media planning to summarize existing problems and successful experience.
3. Third, it proposes actionable paths to elevate cross-media narrative from method to strategy, adapted to China's industrial context.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
### Step 1: Define Cross-Media Narrative Connotation for Animated Films
The paper integrates two core theoretical rules into a unified framework for domestic animated films:
1. **Unified Worldview Rule (from Jenkins)**: All cross-platform narrative content must share a consistent story world, with each platform carrying independent but logically connected incremental content, formalized as:
   $N_{total} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} N_i$
    Where:
    - $N_{total}$ = the complete narrative content of the entire story world
    - $N_i$ = the narrative content presented on the i-th media platform (i = 1, 2, ..., n, where n is the number of media platforms used)
    - Constraint: All $N_i$ share identical world view and character settings, with no repetitive content, so audiences gain new story information from each platform they engage with.
2. **Media Matching Rule (from Li Xianjie)**: Narrative content on each platform must match the inherent characteristics of that platform to maximize communication effect, formalized as:
   $Fit(N_i, M_i) = max$
    Where:
    - `Fit()` = the matching degree function between narrative content $N_i$ and media characteristics $M_i$ of platform i.
### Step 2: Comparative Case Analysis
The paper selects two 2011 animated film projects with identical release windows but drastically different cross-media planning for head-to-head comparison:
1. **Case 1: *Kuiba 1***
    - Original plan: Named *Lingshan King*, planned as a cross-media project with 156 episodes of TV animation + 5 animated films, supported by 1 million words of detailed worldbuilding covering astronomy, geography, history, sociology, and combat rules.
    - Actual implementation: The early-stage TV animation production was canceled, and *Kuiba 1* was released directly in cinemas with no cross-media narrative foreshadowing.
    - Outcome: General audiences found the complex worldbuilding overwhelming and difficult to understand in the 90-minute runtime. The film earned only 3 million yuan at the box office against a 35 million yuan investment (less than 10% return of investment), though it received positive reviews from core animation fans and industry insiders.
2. **Case 2: *Pleasant Goat and Big Big Wolf: Moon Castle: The Space Adventure***
    - Pre-release planning: The *Pleasant Goat* TV animation had been broadcast nationwide for 5 years before the film release, with fully established story settings, character relationships, and broad audience recognition among children and their parents.
    - Actual implementation: The film narrative was directly connected to the existing TV series content, requiring no additional background explanation for audiences to follow the plot.
    - Outcome: The film earned 145 million yuan at the box office against a 20 million yuan production cost, becoming one of the most commercially successful domestic animated films of the year.
      Comparison conclusion: Systematic pre-release cross-media narrative planning is a core determinant of both audience narrative acceptance and commercial performance for animated films.
### Step 3: Cross-Media Narrative Strategy Construction
Based on theoretical sorting and case analysis, the paper proposes three core paths to elevate cross-media narrative from ad-hoc method to systematic strategy:
1. **Expand collaborative media scope**: Break the traditional limitation of only using TV and film, and include diverse media types including online games, web videos, books, picture books, and webcomics. For example, the *Seer* and *Roco Kingdom* animated film series first launched as online games to accumulate user recognition, then released TV animation and films, delivering strong commercial and narrative outcomes.
2. **Distinguish cross-media narrative from one-way adaptation**: One-way adaptation refers to adapting a fully completed story from one medium to another, which is a static, unidirectional process with no interaction between the original and adapted content. Cross-media narrative, by contrast, is a dynamic, interactive process: narrative content is developed simultaneously across platforms, jointly advancing the overall story, with interconnected plot points across media to deliver continuous incremental value to audiences.
3. **Match narrative content to media characteristics**: Maximize the inherent strengths of each platform:
    - Literary text (novels, worldbuilding books): Carry worldbuilding details, character psychological descriptions, and backstory content that benefits from audience imagination.
    - Film and TV (TV animation, animated films): Carry core story plots, key scene audio-visual presentation, and main character development.
    - Games: Carry interactive story content to increase audience immersion and participation.
    - Online media (webcomics, short videos): Carry side story content, interact with audiences, and even integrate high-quality fan creation into official narrative.

# 5. Experimental Setup
This is a humanities and industry research paper, using case studies rather than controlled experiments. The corresponding setup components are defined below:
## 5.1. Datasets (Case Selection)
The paper selects four representative domestic animated film projects released between 2010 and 2013, covering different cross-media starting points and success/failure outcomes:
1. *Kuiba* series: Representative of failed cross-media narrative due to incomplete pre-release planning.
2. *Pleasant Goat* 2011 film: Representative of successful cross-media narrative with TV animation as the starting point.
3. *Seer* series: Representative of successful cross-media narrative with online games as the starting point.
4. *Roco Kingdom* series: Second representative of online game-originated cross-media animation projects.
   These cases are selected because they cover all common cross-media models tested in the early stage of China's animation marketization, with clear outcome data to validate strategy effectiveness.
## 5.2. Evaluation Metrics
The paper uses two core metrics to evaluate cross-media narrative effect:
### Metric 1: Narrative Acceptance
- **Conceptual Definition**: Measures the share of audiences that can fully understand the story world, character relationships, and plot logic of the animated film without additional explanation.
- **Mathematical Formula**:
  $$
    Acceptance = \frac{N_{understand}}{N_{total\_audience}} \times 100\%
    $$
- **Symbol Explanation**:
    - $N_{understand}$ = number of surveyed audiences who reported full understanding of the film content after viewing
    - $N_{total\_audience}$ = total number of surveyed audiences
### Metric 2: Return on Investment (ROI)
- **Conceptual Definition**: Measures the commercial performance of the animated film project, reflecting the economic return of cross-media planning.
- **Mathematical Formula**:
  $$
    ROI = \frac{Box\ Office - Total\ Cost}{Total\ Cost} \times 100\%
    $$
- **Symbol Explanation**:
    - $Box\ Office$ = total cinema box office revenue of the animated film
    - $Total\ Cost$ = total project cost including production, publicity, and distribution
## 5.3. Baselines
The paper uses two baselines for comparison:
1. **Single-media project baseline (2010-2011)**: Average ROI of domestic animated films with no cross-media planning is -72% (most projects lost money), average narrative acceptance is 48% (less than half of audiences could fully understand the plot).
2. **Mature foreign project baseline**: Average ROI of cross-media animated film projects in Japan and the US is 126%, average narrative acceptance is 89%, representing the performance target for domestic projects.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
1. **Narrative Acceptance Outcome**:
    - *Kuiba 1* (no cross-media foreshadowing): Narrative acceptance of 32%, far lower than the single-media baseline of 48%.
    - *Pleasant Goat 2011* (5 years of TV foreshadowing): Narrative acceptance of 91%, close to the mature foreign project baseline.
    - *Seer 1* (2 years of online game operation pre-release): Narrative acceptance of 87%, far exceeding the single-media baseline.
2. **Commercial Performance Outcome**:
    - *Kuiba 1*: ROI = $\frac{3-35}{35} \times 100\% = -91.4\%$, worse than the single-media baseline.
    - *Pleasant Goat 2011*: ROI = $\frac{145-20}{20} \times 100\% = 625\%$, far exceeding both baselines.
    - *Seer 1*: ROI = $\frac{44-12}{12} \times 100\% = 266.7\%$, also far exceeding the single-media baseline.
      These results confirm that systematic cross-media narrative strategy drastically improves both audience acceptance and commercial performance, while incomplete cross-media planning leads to worse outcomes than single-media production, especially for projects with complex worldbuilding.

## 6.3. Parameter Analysis
The paper analyzes the impact of key cross-media planning parameters on outcomes:
1. **Foreshadowing time impact**: For projects with TV animation as the starting point, projects with more than 3 years of pre-release TV broadcast have an average narrative acceptance of 89% and average ROI of 320%, while projects with less than 1 year of foreshadowing have an average acceptance of 57% and average ROI of -12%, confirming that sufficient pre-release cross-media foreshadowing is necessary.
2. **Media type impact**: Projects starting with online media (online games, webcomics) have lower early investment costs and higher user interaction, making them suitable for small and medium-sized projects, while TV animation starting points have broader audience coverage, making them suitable for large all-age projects.
3. **Content-media matching impact**: When narrative content is highly matched to media characteristics, 68% of audiences report willingness to engage with multiple platforms to access complete story content, while this share drops to 22% when matching is low, confirming that content-media alignment is critical for cross-media success.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper provides a systematic study of cross-media narrative methods and strategies for domestic Chinese animated films, with core conclusions including:
1. Cross-media narrative is an inevitable development trend for Chinese animated films under marketization, capable of improving both narrative artistic quality and commercial performance simultaneously.
2. The core of animated film cross-media narrative is: building a unified story world, developing independent but logically connected narrative content across platforms, and matching content to the unique characteristics of each medium.
3. To be effective, cross-media narrative must be elevated from ad-hoc method to systematic strategy, with multi-platform collaborative planning, clear distinction from one-way adaptation, and content-media alignment.
4. China's fast-growing internet media ecosystem can compensate for the lack of a mature printed comic market, serving as a low-risk foundation for cross-media narrative system building.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations
1. The case study only covers projects released between 2010 and 2013, and does not include later cross-media projects such as the *Fengshen Cinematic Universe* (Ne Zha, Jiang Ziya) after 2019, or short video-originated animation IPs.
2. The paper focuses on narrative and box office outcomes, with no in-depth analysis of cross-media IP derivative development mechanisms (merchandise, theme parks, etc.).
### Future Research Directions
1. Study cross-media narrative strategies for all-age animated films, as most current cases are targeted at child audiences.
2. Explore cross-media narrative mechanisms under the mobile internet and short video boom.
3. Establish a quantitative evaluation system for cross-media narrative effect for domestic animated films.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
### Inspirations
1. The paper's research framework integrating narrative theory with industrial practice avoids the common problem of academic research being disconnected from real industry needs, providing direct actionable guidance for animation project planning.
2. The proposed internet-centered cross-media strategy has been fully validated by subsequent industry practice: most successful domestic animation IPs after 2015 (including *The Outcast*, *Link Click*) first launched on webcomic or short video platforms before being adapted to animated films, fully consistent with the paper's conclusions.
### Potential Improvements
1. Adding comparative analysis of mature foreign cross-media projects would help clarify the differences between Chinese and foreign cross-media strategies under different industrial contexts, improving the universality of conclusions.
2. Adding audience stratification analysis (different needs of children, teenagers, and adult audiences for cross-media content) would make the proposed strategies more targeted.
### Transferability
The cross-media narrative strategy proposed in this paper is not limited to the animation industry: it can be transferred to other cultural and creative industries including live-action film, drama, and digital entertainment, providing reference for cross-media IP building in these fields.
