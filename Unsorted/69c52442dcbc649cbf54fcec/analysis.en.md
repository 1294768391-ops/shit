## 1.5. Abstract
Hou Hsiao-hsien is a pioneer of Taiwan New Cinema and a world-renowned contemporary film master. His works combine cultural reflection and poetic expression, focus on individual perception and diversity amid urban modernization, and center on marginalized groups impacted by social changes, forming a unique humanistic mode of film art expression. Two core cultural motifs run through his works: the contrast between rural and urban ideologies, and the pain of individual life experiences amid historical transformation. His works avoid deliberate dramatic tension construction, obscure metaphors and rhetorical argumentation, and pursue the traditional Chinese poetic artistic conception of effortless, natural charm. Against the background of global commercial film dominance, studying art films like Hou’s works has strong practical significance.
This paper takes Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films as the research object from the perspective of film narratology, and analyzes their narrative texture to provide new ideas for the development of Chinese art films. The full text is divided into six core parts: the introduction summarizes the research origin and current status of Hou’s film research; Chapter 1 studies his narrative themes, exploring Taiwan identity exploration from three dimensions: personal memory, local historical vision, and alienated urban phantom; Chapter 2 starts from Shen Congwen’s "border culture" and Deleuze’s "silent power" concept, studies his narrative style that breaks traditional dramatic rules, constructs Chinese poetic imagery, and presents multi-dimensional realistic sounds; Chapter 3 studies his narrative time and space art, analyzing the sculpture and reconstruction of narrative time, and the construction and imagination of narrative space, extending the logical starting point of time-space aesthetics in film art; Chapter 4 studies his narrative strategies, showing humanistic concern from three aspects: individual existence, realistic living texture of "dust aesthetics", and reverence for traditional Eastern culture; The conclusion summarizes Hou’s narrative features and looks forward to its inspiration for the future development of Chinese films.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### 2.1.1. Core Problem to Solve
Hou Hsiao-hsien is one of the most internationally influential Chinese film directors, with works of unique artistic value and cultural connotation. However, previous studies on Hou’s films are mostly concentrated in three narrow categories: aesthetic research focusing on his formal film language and style, cultural research focusing on identity and historical expression, and comparative research comparing him with directors like Jia Zhangke and Yasujiro Ozu. Most of these studies adopt a single-text analysis approach, lack a systematic and holistic narrative analysis framework, and fail to fully reveal the internal logic of his entire narrative system across his 40-year career.
### 2.1.2. Importance of the Problem
Against the background that commercial films dominate the global film market, Chinese art films face a persistent dilemma of balancing artistic expression and audience acceptance. Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films, as representative successful Chinese art films that have won wide international recognition, have narrative experience with important reference value for the high-quality development of Chinese art films. Systematic sorting of his narrative system can help domestic film creators learn from his practice and promote the construction of a distinct Chinese film aesthetic style.
### 2.1.3. Research Entry Point & Innovation
This paper takes film narratology as the core theoretical basis, and constructs a four-dimensional analytical framework (narrative theme, narrative style, narrative time-space, narrative strategy) to conduct a systematic and holistic study of Hou’s representative works across all his creation stages, which fills the gap of previous single-text and fragmented research on Hou’s film narrative.
## 2.2. Main Contributions & Findings
### 2.2.1. Primary Contributions
1. Constructed a systematic analytical framework for Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film narrative, covering four interrelated dimensions from theme to form, from content to strategy, which provides a new, holistic research perspective for Hou’s film studies.
2. Realized localized integration of theoretical resources: combines Western narrative theories (Gérard Genette’s narrative time theory, Gilles Deleuze’s time-image theory, David Bordwell’s film style theory) with Chinese traditional aesthetic theories (blank art, poetic imagery, golden mean thought), avoiding the mechanical application of Western theories to Chinese film research.
3. Systematically sorted out the evolution logic of Hou’s narrative across five different creation stages, revealing the internal connection between his personal growth experience, Taiwan’s social and historical changes and his narrative transformation.
### 2.2.2. Key Conclusions
1. The core of Hou’s narrative theme is the exploration of Taiwan identity, embodied in three progressive levels: personal memory and nostalgia, local historical reflection, and urban alienation observation, forming a complete identity exploration system from individual to collective, from history to reality.
2. Hou’s narrative style presents a unique "silent power": it breaks traditional dramatic narrative rules, constructs a Chinese poetic aesthetic space through blank space, empty shots and traditional imagery, and uses multi-dimensional sound (dialects, ambient sound, minimalist music) to enhance the sense of reality and emotional appeal of the film.
3. Hou’s narrative time and space construction breaks traditional linear narrative logic: through the sculpture of four time forms (real time, omitted time, duration time, inverted time), it gives time a sense of life and three-dimensionality; through the construction of three space forms (family life space, aesthetic moving space, exotic space with Chinese imagination), it realizes the integration of space, narrative, emotion and culture.
4. Hou’s narrative strategy is rooted in profound humanistic concern: focusing on the dignity and value of every individual (including marginalized groups like women, gang members, displaced youth), adhering to the "dust aesthetics" that restores the real texture of daily life, and pursuing the expression of traditional Chinese culture, making his films have both high artistic value and humanistic temperature.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
The core professional concepts involved in this paper are explained below for beginner readers:
### 3.1.1. Film Narratology
Film narratology is a branch of film studies that studies the narrative rules and characteristics of films, developed on the basis of literary narratology. Its core research content includes how films tell stories, the composition of narrative elements (theme, character, plot, time, space, perspective, etc.), and the interaction between narrative form and content. It emerged as an independent discipline in the 1980s, with representative scholars including Gérard Genette, François Jost, and David Bordwell. Different from literary narratology, film narratology focuses on the unique narrative methods of audio-visual media such as lens, editing, and sound.
### 3.1.2. Long Take
Long take is a film shooting technique that refers to shooting a scene continuously for a long time (usually more than 30 seconds) without cutting, forming a complete shot segment. Long take can maximize the restoration of the real material world, retain the integrity of time and space, and give the audience enough space for independent thinking. It is one of the representative techniques of realist film aesthetics, and Hou Hsiao-hsien’s "long-distance long take" (shooting characters from a far distance with a fixed lens) is one of his most iconic aesthetic labels.
### 3.1.3. Time-Image
Time-image is a concept proposed by French philosopher Gilles Deleuze in his work *Time-Image*. Different from traditional "movement-image" which subordinates time to movement and plot, time-image breaks linear narrative logic, presents time directly through irrational editing, still shots, and empty shots, allowing the audience to perceive the passage of time directly, and brings space for imagination and thinking. Hou’s films are typical representative works of time-image cinema.
### 3.1.4. Identity Recognition
Identity recognition (or identity) in cultural studies refers to an individual or group’s cognition and affirmation of their own cultural identity, ethnic identity, and regional identity. For Taiwanese society, due to its special historical experience (colonized by Japan for 50 years, separated from the Chinese mainland after 1949), identity recognition has always been a core issue concerned by Taiwanese cultural and art creators, and it is also the core thematic thread running through all of Hou’s films.
## 3.2. Previous Works
As summarized in the literature review section of this paper, previous studies on Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films are mainly divided into three categories:
### 3.2.1. Aesthetic Research
This type of research focuses on Hou’s film ontology language, shooting concept and aesthetic style. Representative studies include Meng Hongfeng’s *On Hou Hsiao-hsien’s Style* which summarizes his aesthetic concept as "stuffy, dazed, muddy", and points out the influence of Chinese writer Shen Congwen on his style formation; Sun Weichuan’s research on his "Sadness Trilogy" analyzes the differences between his documentary aesthetics and André Bazin’s realist theory, pointing out his obvious nationalization characteristics.
### 3.2.2. Cultural Research
This type of research focuses on the cultural connotation of Hou’s films, using theories such as psychoanalysis, national allegory, etc., to study identity recognition, historical consciousness and other issues. Representative studies include Dai Jinhua’s research using Fredric Jameson’s "national allegory" theory to analyze his early autobiographical works and historical trilogy; Hu Chucheng’s research on the exploration of Taiwanese identity in his films, sorting out the problem of Taiwanese identity loss across different creation stages.
### 3.2.3. Comparative Research
This type of research compares Hou with other directors with similar styles, including comparison with mainland Chinese director Jia Zhangke (focusing on the similarities and differences of long take use and aesthetic concepts), comparison with Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu (focusing on the expression of Eastern aesthetics), and comparison with Taiwanese director Edward Yang (focusing on the differences in narrative style and social observation perspectives).
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The development of film narratology can be divided into three stages:
1. **Origin stage (1960s-1970s):** Literary narratology was formally established with Tzvetan Todorov’s proposal of the term "narratology" in 1969, and scholars began to try to apply narratology to film studies.
2. **Development stage (1980s-1990s):** Scholars such as Gérard Genette and David Bordwell systematically constructed the theoretical system of film narratology, expanding the research content to narrative time, space, perspective, sound and other dimensions.
3. **Localization stage (2000s-present):** Chinese scholars began to apply Western film narratology to the study of Chinese films, and explored the construction of localized film narrative theory combined with Chinese traditional aesthetic thought.
   Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film creation runs through the above three stages, and his unique narrative practice has also promoted the development of Chinese film narratology research. This paper’s research is at the localization stage, and it is a typical practice of applying Western narrative theory to the study of Chinese director’s works combined with Chinese traditional aesthetics.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with previous studies, this paper has two core differences and innovations:
1. **Research perspective innovation:** Different from previous studies that mostly adopt a single perspective (aesthetic, cultural or comparative), this paper constructs a four-dimensional systematic narrative analysis framework, covering from thematic content to formal style, from time-space construction to narrative strategy, realizing a holistic study of Hou’s narrative system.
2. **Research object innovation:** Different from previous studies that mostly focus on single classic works (such as *A City of Sadness*, *The Puppetmaster*), this paper selects 14 representative works of Hou across all five creation stages (from early commercial films to later exotic works), revealing the evolution logic of his narrative across his entire career.
3. **Theoretical application innovation:** This paper integrates multiple theoretical resources, combining Western narrative theory, time-image theory with Chinese traditional aesthetic theory, avoiding the mechanical application of Western theory, and more accurately revealing the Eastern aesthetic characteristics of Hou’s narrative.

## 4.2. Core Analytical Framework
The paper constructs a four-dimensional analytical framework for Hou’s film narrative, which is the core of the research:
### 4.2.1. Dimension 1: Narrative Theme - Identity Recognition Exploration
This dimension analyzes how Hou explores Taiwan identity through three levels of themes:
1. **Experience and Nostalgia: Personalized Memory Ark:** Focusing on his "growth tetralogy" (*The Boys from Fengkuei*, *A Summer at Grandpa's*, *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, *Dust in the Wind*), analyzing the expression of youth rebellion, the decline of patriarchy, and the outlook on life and death, reflecting the personal memory and nostalgia of Taiwan’s local youth.
2. **Reference and Reflection: Local Historical Vision:** Focusing on his "Taiwan history trilogy" (*A City of Sadness*, *The Puppetmaster*, *Good Men, Good Women*), analyzing the exploration of national positioning, the construction of personal history, and the pursuit of historical memory, reflecting the reflection on Taiwan’s modern history and ethnic integration.
3. **Boundary and Otherness: Alienated Urban Phantom:** Focusing on his urban-themed works (*Goodbye South, Goodbye*, *Millennium Mambo*, *Three Times*), analyzing the expression of past rupture, urban alienation, the dissolution of redemption, and the pursuit of local memory, reflecting the confusion and alienation of urban youth in the process of Taiwan’s modernization.
### 4.2.2. Dimension 2: Narrative Style - Conveyance of Silent Power
This dimension analyzes how Hou constructs a unique narrative style with "silent power" inspired by Shen Congwen’s "border culture" and Deleuze’s "silent power" concept:
1. **Breaking of Image Dramatic Rules:**
   - Jump cut: Used to focus emotional tension, such as the jump cut of the protagonist Ah Qing in *The Boys from Fengkuei* when he first arrives in the city, revealing the inner depression and confusion of rural youth entering the city.
   - Long-distance long take: Used to express profound thoughts, with an average shot length of 42 seconds in *A City of Sadness* and 82 seconds in *The Puppetmaster*, retaining the integrity of time and space, and giving the audience space to think.
   - Intertextuality of natural and cultural landscapes: Natural landscapes (such as Jiufen Mountain in *A City of Sadness*) and cultural landscapes (such as the three family dinner scenes in *A City of Sadness*) echo each other, implying the changes of family and historical destiny.
2. **Construction of Chinese Poetic Imageries in Shots:**
   - Blank space as the narrative driving force: A large number of blanks are used in *The Assassin*, and the audience fills in the missing narrative information through imagination, achieving the aesthetic effect of "endless meaning beyond words".
   - Empty shots that turn scenery into emotion: Empty shots are widely used, such as the 1-minute horizontal panning empty shot of the countryside after Ah Yuan learns of his lover’s marriage in *Dust in the Wind*, which implicitly expresses the pain of lovelorn and the relief of growth.

   - Coordination and intertextuality of music: Minimalist music is used, such as the pure wooden guitar soundtrack in *Dust in the Wind* and the drum soundtrack in *The Assassin*, which echoes the image content and enhances the emotional expression.
### 4.2.3. Dimension 3: Narrative Time-Space - Aesthetic Logical Starting Point
This dimension analyzes Hou’s unique time-space aesthetic construction:
1. **Sculpture and Reconstruction of Narrative Time:**
   - Perceptual experience of real time: Restore the real time of daily life, such as the 1-minute black screen when the power goes out before the father’s death in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, which is consistent with the real time of finding matches and lighting candles in daily life, enhancing the audience’s immersive experience.
   - Minimalist symbol of omitted time: Omit unnecessary transitional time through editing, such as the three meeting scenes of the artist and the scholar in *Three Times* "Freedom Dream" chapter, omitting the time between meetings, and showing the emotional changes of the characters through the subtle differences of the three scenes.
   - Re-implantation imagination of duration time: Use still shots, empty shots to create "silent time", allowing the audience to perceive the passage of time directly, such as the still shots of dusk, stations, and letters in *Dust in the Wind*, bringing space for the audience’s imagination and emotional resonance.
   - Memory presence of inverted time: Use flashback and reverse narration to present memory, such as the flashback of Ah Qing’s memory of his father after his father’s death in *The Boys from Fengkuei*, highlighting the growth pain of the protagonist.
2. **Construction and Imagination of Narrative Space:**
   - Family memory living space: Family is the core space of Hou’s films, which is both a harbor for the soul and a place reflecting the changes of the times, such as the Japanese-style house in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* and the Lin family’s residence in *A City of Sadness*.
   - Moving space with image beauty: Use means of transportation such as trains, cars, and ferries to create dynamic space, carrying the emotional state of the characters, such as the train scene in *Dust in the Wind* and the car scene of Vicky leaning out of the window in *Millennium Mambo*.
   - Exotic space of "Chinese imagination": In his later exotic works (*Café Lumière*, *Flight of the Red Balloon*), integrate the expression of Chinese culture, such as Yangzi’s search for Taiwanese composer Chiang Wen-yeh in *Café Lumière* and the collection of the Great Wall postcard by the French hostess in *Flight of the Red Balloon*, reflecting the imagination and dissemination of Chinese culture in an international context.
### 4.2.4. Dimension 4: Narrative Strategy - Humanistic Concern
This dimension analyzes the humanistic connotation of Hou’s narrative strategy:
1. **Individuals of the Essence of Life:** Focus on the dignity and value of every individual, including:
   - Observation and contemplation of female portraits: From the traditional female image of the mother and grandmother in the early works, to the progressive female image of Kuan Mei in *A City of Sadness*, to the independent female image of Vicky in *Millennium Mambo*, showing the evolution of female consciousness and the exploration of female subject value.
   - Ambition and destiny of gang bosses: The gang boss images such as Kao in *Goodbye South, Goodbye* and Jie in *Millennium Mambo* are not the traditional negative gang images, but have the characteristics of loyalty, responsibility, and struggle against fate, reflecting the director’s sympathy and concern for marginalized groups.
2. **Realistic Life Texture of "Dust Aesthetics":**
   - "Plain reading" method to restore daily rhythm: Avoid dramatic plot construction, present the real daily life of the characters, such as the family dinner scenes in *A City of Sadness* and the life scenes of Li Tianlu in *The Puppetmaster*, which are as natural as real life.
   - "Wanderer" perspective to gaze at the human-land relationship: Use the perspective of children and youth wanderers to observe the relationship between people and land, the conflict between rural civilization and urban civilization, such as Dongdong’s perspective in *A Summer at Grandpa's* and the perspective of youth entering the city in *The Boys from Fengkuei* and *Dust in the Wind*.
3. **Pursuit of Traditional Chinese Culture with Oriental Background:**
   - Extension of deep collective psychology of folk culture: Present traditional folk customs such as worship, wedding and funeral ceremonies, such as the "worship of heaven" ceremony and the funeral and wedding scenes in *A City of Sadness*, reflecting the same cultural origin between Taiwan and the mainland.
   - Image reconstruction of the golden mean: Adopt the aesthetic principle of moderate emotional expression, avoid excessive rendering of sadness and violence, such as the expression of death scenes and political violence scenes, which are implicit and restrained, reflecting the influence of Confucian golden mean thought.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
Through the systematic analysis of 14 selected films, the paper draws the following core results:
1. **Narrative theme level:** Hou’s narrative theme forms a complete identity exploration system: the personal memory theme completes the individual identity construction, the historical reflection theme completes the collective identity construction, and the urban observation theme completes the modern identity construction. This system is closely related to Hou’s personal growth experience (growing up in southern Taiwan, family origin in Guangdong) and the special historical process of Taiwan, which is a true portrayal of the identity confusion and exploration of Taiwanese people in the second half of the 20th century.
2. **Narrative style level:** Hou’s "silent power" narrative style breaks the dual shackles of traditional Chinese dramatic narrative and Hollywood three-act dramatic narrative, and constructs an Eastern poetic narrative system with Chinese aesthetic characteristics. Compared with the traditional narrative style that pursues dramatic conflict and plot tension, Hou’s style pays more attention to emotional expression and the audience’s independent thinking, which provides a new possibility for the diversification of film narrative.
3. **Time-space construction level:** Hou’s narrative time and space construction breaks the traditional linear narrative logic of "time serves the plot, space serves the scene", giving time and space independent aesthetic value and narrative function. His time sculpture expands the audience’s perception of time, and his space construction integrates emotional, cultural and narrative functions, which enriches the theoretical connotation of film time-space aesthetics.
4. **Humanistic connotation level:** Hou’s narrative strategy always takes "people" as the core, even marginalized groups (women, gang members, urban youth) are given equal dignity and value expression. His "dust aesthetics" restores the real texture of daily life, avoids the alienation of film from real life, and his pursuit of traditional Chinese culture provides a reference for the construction of Chinese film’s national style.
   Compared with previous research conclusions, this paper’s results are more systematic and holistic: it not only verifies the previous conclusions about Hou’s aesthetic style and cultural connotation, but also reveals the internal logical connection between theme, style, time-space and strategy, forming a complete narrative system.

1. The application of Gérard Genette’s narrative time theory accurately reveals the characteristics of Hou’s time sculpture (real time, omitted time, inverted time).
2. The application of Gilles Deleuze’s time-image theory accurately explains the aesthetic effect of Hou’s duration time and "silent time".
3. The combination of Chinese traditional aesthetic theory (blank, poetic imagery, golden mean) accurately reveals the Eastern aesthetic characteristics of Hou’s narrative, which cannot be explained by Western theories alone.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper systematically studies Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film narrative from the perspective of film narratology, and draws the following core conclusions:
1. Hou’s film narrative is a complete system with internal logical connection: the theme of identity exploration is the core content, the "silent power" narrative style is the formal feature, the unique time-space construction is the aesthetic carrier, and the humanistic concern narrative strategy is the value connotation.
2. Hou’s narrative is rooted in the special historical and cultural soil of Taiwan, and at the same time absorbs the essence of Chinese traditional aesthetics, forming a unique Eastern film narrative model, which has important reference value for the development of Chinese art films and the dissemination of Chinese culture internationally.
3. The research method of integrating Western narrative theory with Chinese traditional aesthetic theory is applicable to the study of Chinese films, which can avoid the mechanical application of Western theory and more accurately reveal the national characteristics of Chinese films.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
