## 1.5 Abstract
The research addresses the problem where some immersive theatre works over-rely on digital technology ("digitalization" and "immersion"), leading to a loss of balance between device form and narrative content, causing semantic disorder. The paper aims to construct a design methodology for immersive theatre interactive devices based on semiotic theory. By analyzing the encoding and decoding process of dramatic symbols, it builds a paradigm for the symbolic meaning of plots, situations, and emotions.
-   **Core Methodology:** A three-step approach: 1) Semiotic interpretation of devices; 2) Construction of the "Narrative Field"; 3) "Symbolic Restoration" design methods (Plot, Situation, Emotion reduction).
-   **Key Results:** Proposed a framework for designing devices that highlight theatricality rather than obscuring it. Validated through the design practice of an installation titled "Insight" (《洞见》) based on Bertolt Brecht's play *Der Gute Mensch Von Sezuan* (The Good Person of Sichuan).
-   **Key Conclusions:** Successful application of semiotics resolves the conflict between technology and drama, ensuring devices serve the narrative and emotional depth.

# 2. Executive Summary

## 2.1 Background & Motivation
The rapid advancement of digital technology has empowered theatre art, giving rise to new forms like immersive theatre. Digital interactive devices have become mainstream tools for stage creation. However, a critical problem has emerged: many creators excessively pursue "digital" and "immersive" phenomena, causing an imbalance between the form of the device and the narrative content. This often leads to semantic confusion and a loss of theatricality, where the device obscures the essence of the performance rather than enhancing it.
-   **Specific Challenge:** Prior research often lacks a systematic theoretical framework connecting digital devices directly to dramatic narrative semantics. There is a gap in understanding how to encode/decode dramatic symbols through devices to maintain the integrity of the story.
-   **Entry Point:** The author enters the field by applying **Semiotics** (the study of signs and symbols) to immersive theatre. The goal is to treat the device not merely as a visual tool but as a carrier of dramatic signifiers, ensuring technology serves the narrative rather than dominating it.

## 2.2 Main Contributions / Findings
The paper makes three primary contributions:
1.  **Theoretical Framework:** It establishes a semiotic interpretation system for immersive theatre devices, defining their textual, metalinguistic, and coding characteristics.
2.  **Design Pathway:** It proposes a "Narrative Field" construction path involving "Action Fields," "Aesthetic Fields," and "Perceptual Fields."
3.  **Methodology:** It introduces the **"Symbolic Restoration" Design Method**, consisting of **Plot Reduction** (Intertextual Decoding), **Situation Reduction** (Multireconstruction), and **Emotion Reduction** (Interactive Coding).

    These findings solve the problem of "tech-over-content" by providing concrete strategies to embed dramatic themes into interactive experiences, validated by the creation of the "Insight" installation.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work

## 3.1 Foundational Concepts
To understand this paper, a novice must grasp the following fundamental concepts:

-   <strong>Semiotics (符号学):</strong> The study of signs and symbols and their use or interpretation. Key figures include Ferdinand de Saussure (Linguistic Model) and Charles Sanders Peirce (Logical-Rhetorical Model). In this context, it provides the vocabulary (Signifier/Signified, Code, Context) to analyze how objects convey meaning in a performance.
-   <strong>Immersive Theatre (沉浸式戏剧):</strong> A genre of live performance where the audience is not seated passively but moves through the performance space, often interacting with actors and environment. It breaks the "Fourth Wall" (the invisible barrier between actors and audience). Examples mentioned include *Sleep No More*.
-   <strong>Digital Interactive Installation (数字交互装置):</strong> Artworks that incorporate digital technology allowing real-time interaction with users. Unlike static sculpture, these respond to touch, movement, or sound, creating a dynamic experience.

## 3.2 Previous Works
The author reviews extensive literature to establish the context:
-   **Semiotic Studies:** Early Chinese scholars like Zhao Yuanren introduced the term. Wang Mingyu emphasized combining Western theory with Chinese cultural characteristics. Notable figures include Hu Miaosheng, who applied semiotics to drama space, arguing stage design creates a "symbolic space."
-   **Immersive Theatre Studies:** Traced from 19th-century realism to Richard Schechner's "Environmental Theatre" (1968). Modern works include *Sleep No More* (Punchdrunk, UK), which pioneered large-scale audience exploration.
-   **Interaction Design:** Explores the evolution from early projection (Erwin Piscator) to modern AR/VR/MR technologies. Artists like Daniel Rozin created physical mirrors that react to viewers, setting a precedent for interactive materiality.

## 3.3 Technological Evolution
The timeline of technology in theatre shows a progression:
1.  **Slide/Projection (Early):** Static background images (Erwin Piscator, Josef Svoboda).
2.  **Real-time Video (Late 20th Century):** Live camera feeds projected onto sets (Svoboda's "TV Theatre").
3.  **Sensor-Based Interaction (Current):** Using motion capture, infrared, and sensors to allow the set to react to human presence (e.g., TeamLab works, holographic interactions).
    This paper fits into the current era, advocating for the integration of these sensor technologies with deep narrative theory.

## 3.4 Differentiation Analysis
Compared to existing works:
-   **Previous Focus:** Most prior research focuses on the *technical implementation* of devices or general audience psychology.
-   **Current Innovation:** This paper uniquely bridges the gap by treating the device itself as a **semiotic object**. It doesn't just ask "How does the device work?" but "What sign does the device send?" It differentiates by proposing a specific **design methodology** (Restoration of Plot/Situation/Emotion) derived from semiotic theory, rather than just artistic intuition.

# 4. Methodology

## 4.1 Principles
The core idea is **Symbolic Restoration**. The methodology posits that digital devices must reduce back to their original dramatic function—serving the plot, building the situation, and evoking emotion—rather than acting as standalone entertainment. The theoretical basis is **Semiotics**, specifically Peirce's triadic model (Representamen-Object-Interpretant) adapted for design. The device (Sign) represents a dramatic concept (Object) that generates an emotional/thoughtful response (Interpretant) in the audience.

## 4.2 Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)

The proposed design process consists of three main phases: Semiotic Interpretation, Narrative Field Construction, and Symbolic Restoration.

### Step 1: Semiotic Interpretation of the Device
Before design begins, the device is analyzed as a sign system.
-   **Concept:** The device is broken down into its textual components.
-   **Process:**
    1.  **Analyze Accompanying Text:** Identify explicit words (dialogues), generative meanings (subtext/emotions), and explanatory contexts (background culture).
    2.  **Identify Metalinguistics:** Determine how the device manipulates space (Fluidity), time/presence (Presence of actors/audience), and boundaries (Bridging gaps between virtual and real).
    3.  **Define Codes:** Categorize the device's functional codes: Technical Expression (sensors/LEDs), Image Reproduction (projection), and Media Interpretation (meaning given by the audience).
-   **Formula/Logic:** While not a mathematical formula, the logic follows: `Device = f(Technology + Space + Audience)`. The designer must define the variables in this function based on the script.

### Step 2: Construction of the "Narrative Field"
The device is placed within a specific narrative context.
-   **Origin Point:** The script is the coordinate origin.
    -   **Representation:** Does the device express the theme? (e.g., Greed represented by a mirror that distorts face).
    -   **Instruction:** Does it direct audience behavior? (e.g., A glowing path implies movement).
    -   **Resemblance:** Does the physical form look like something else? (e.g., A prop resembling a weapon to suggest violence).
-   **Field Construction:** Three distinct fields are constructed around the device:
    1.  **Action Field:** Where physical movement happens (pathways, zones).
    2.  **Aesthetic Field:** Visual style (colors, textures, lighting).
    3.  **Perceptual Field:** Sensory experience (sound, smell, touch).
-   **Ontological Metaphor:** The device is designed as a metaphor (Static/Dynamic, Abstract/Concrete, Virtual/Real). For example, a static wall that becomes dynamic when touched represents the "hidden nature of reality."

### Step 3: Symbolic Restoration Design (The Core Practice)
This is the actionable phase where the design is executed. It involves three specific reduction methods.

#### 3.1 Plot Reduction Method (Intertextual Decoding)
The goal is to align the device with the story's plot.
-   **Step 3.1.1 Thematic Reference:** Analyze the background. If the play is about industrial decay, the device uses metal/rust textures.
-   **Step 3.1.2 Style Permeation:** Match the visual style to the narrative tone. A noir play gets high-contrast shadows; a comedic play gets bright colors.
-   **Step 3.1.3 Path Mapping:** Map the audience's movement to the plot's progression. Interactions trigger key plot points (e.g., pressing a button reveals a backstory).

#### 3.2 Situation Reduction Method (Multireconstruction)
The goal is to recreate the atmosphere or "situation" of the scene.
-   **Step 3.2.1 Functional Architecture:** Design the input/output mechanism. Sensors collect data $\rightarrow$ Controller processes $\rightarrow$ Output changes light/sound.
    $$ \text{Input}_{sensor} \xrightarrow{\text{Controller}} \text{Processing}_{algorithm} \xrightarrow{\text{Actuator}} \text{Output}_{environment} $$
    *(Note: This logical flow replaces a specific mathematical formula, describing the system architecture).*
-   **Step 3.2.2 Spatial Layout:** Arrange the physical space. Use color, scale, and symmetry to evoke feeling. Cold blue tones for isolation; warm yellow for intimacy.
-   **Step 3.2.3 Lighting & Sound Rendering:** Use dynamic lighting to simulate day/night cycles or tension. Audio cues (surround sound) match location changes.

#### 3.3 Emotion Reduction Method (Interactive Coding)
The goal is to trigger specific emotional responses.
-   **Step 3.3.1 Material Carriers:** Choose materials that feel right. Rough wood for history; smooth glass for fragility.
-   **Step 3.3.2 Graphic Language:** Use imagery inside screens to tell stories. Red implies danger/passion; Green implies life/hope.
-   **Step 3.3.3 Action Extension:** Design the interaction itself to be emotional. Touching a cold surface triggers a memory sequence; lifting a heavy prop creates a sense of burden.
    -   **Feedback Loop:** When Audience Action ($A$) occurs, System Response ($S$) changes, which influences Audience Perception ($P$).
        $P_{new} = S(A)$
    *(Note: This illustrates the feedback loop of emotional coding).*

# 5. Experimental Setup

## 5.1 Datasets
Since this is a design research thesis, the "experiment" is a **Case Study Implementation** and **User Survey**.

-   **Primary Dataset (Survey):** An online questionnaire collected from the public to gauge preferences for immersive theatre devices.

    -   **Context:** The design of an interactive installation named "Insight" (《洞见》).

# 6. Results & Analysis

## 6.1 Core Results Analysis
The study successfully demonstrated that integrating semiotic theory improves device design.
-   **Survey Findings:**
    -   **High Demand:** 44.57% of respondents were willing to interact with devices; 42.29% agreed it helps immersion.
    -   **Critical Pain Points:** The biggest issue with current devices is "monotonous repetitive content" (53.71%) and "insufficient fusion of story and interaction" (46.86%).
    -   **Preference:** 48.84% prefer "free exploration using props" over simple interface control.
-   **Validation:** The "Insight" project addressed these pain points by prioritizing story-driven interaction (Plot Reduction) and sensory depth (Emotion Reduction).

## 6.3 Ablation Studies / Parameter Analysis
While not an algorithm, the author conducted a comparative assessment of three design schemes for the "Insight" project.
-   **Scheme 1:** Pillar Projection Mapping.
    -   *Critique:* Too expensive, high technical uncertainty.
-   **Scheme 2:** Embedded Screen + Prop Light.
    -   *Selection:* Chosen for balance of cost, effect, and feasibility.
-   **Scheme 3:** Full Relief Column.
    -   *Critique:* Too monotonous, details lost in darkness.
-   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The success of the chosen scheme depended heavily on the **calibration of infrared sensitivity** (light beam detection distance ~5 meters) and the **timing of scent release** (4 seconds upon proximity, continuous during interaction). Adjustments to these parameters ensured seamless transitions between modes (Idle vs. Active).

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections

## 7.1 Conclusion Summary
This thesis successfully constructs a design framework for immersive theatre interactive devices grounded in semiotics.
-   **Main Findings:**
    1.  **Semiotic Necessity:** Devices must be interpreted as signs to avoid semantic disorder.
    2.  **Narrative Field:** Placing devices in "Action/Aesthetic/Perceptual" fields ensures they contribute to the whole.
    3.  **Restoration Method:** The "Plot-Situation-Emotion" restoration strategy effectively balances technology and narrative.
-   **Significance:** It provides a theoretical basis for designers to create devices that enhance, rather than overshadow, theatricality. The "Insight" project proves the practical viability of these theories.

## 7.2 Limitations & Future Work
The author acknowledges several limitations:
-   **Theoretical Depth:** The application of semiotics could be more rigorous, potentially exploring cross-cultural differences in symbol interpretation.
-   **Technological Scope:** Newer tech like AI or advanced VR/AR is not fully explored due to constraints.
-   **Stability:** Ensuring smooth transitions in multi-device setups remains a challenge (e.g., latency, interference).
-   **Future Directions:** Suggestions include expanding to cross-cultural studies, integrating AI for smarter adaptive narratives, and conducting larger-scale practical implementations to refine the design guidelines.

## 7.3 Personal Insights & Critique
**Inspiration:** The paper's most valuable insight is shifting the question from "How cool can the tech be?" to "What story does the device tell?". This prevents the common pitfall of "gadget overload" where impressive visuals confuse the audience.

**Transferability:** The "Symbolic Restoration" method can be applied to other interactive art forms, such as museum exhibits or theme park attractions, wherever narrative needs to be conveyed through physical interfaces.

**Potential Issues:** One unverified assumption is that "interactivity equals better immersion." Some audiences may prefer passive observation. The design must offer choices (as suggested in the "Action Field" section) to accommodate different participation levels. Additionally, the reliance on specific scent modules adds maintenance overhead that could limit scalability compared to purely visual systems.
