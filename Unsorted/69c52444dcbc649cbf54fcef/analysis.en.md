# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The paper focuses on the narrative system of Taiwanese New Cinema director Hou Hsiao-hsien, with the official English title *A Study on Hsiao-Hsien Hou movie narrative* (Chinese original: 侯孝贤电影叙事研究). It analyzes Hou's narrative from four interconnected dimensions: structural construction, thematic content, audiovisual language, and critical evaluation.

## 1.5. Abstract
### 1.5.1. Research Objective
The paper aims to systematically deconstruct Hou Hsiao-hsien's unique narrative system, which integrates Chinese traditional aesthetic thought and modern film techniques, filling gaps in existing fragmented studies of Hou's work that focus only on single elements (e.g., long takes) rather than the full narrative architecture.
### 1.5.2. Core Methodology
The paper combines close textual analysis of Hou's complete filmography with two core theoretical frameworks: 1) Mikhail Bakhtin's polyphony theory to analyze parallel narrative space, 2) Wang Guowei's "enter-exit" aesthetic theory to analyze flexible narrative time. It also incorporates traditional Chinese aesthetic concepts of *qiyun* (rhythmic vitality) to explain Hou's editing style.
### 1.5.3. Main Results
The paper identifies three core layers of Hou's narrative:
1.  Narrative structure: Polyphonic parallel narrative space and non-linear, rhythm-driven narrative time
2.  Narrative themes: Three core thematic clusters of personal memory, historical reflection, and urban disillusionment
3.  Narrative language: A distinct audiovisual style including single heavy color tones, diverse dialect use, and extensive voiceover/subtitle integration
### 1.5.4. Key Conclusions
Hou's narrative system creates a unique "Hou-style" poetic, prose-like film aesthetic that has had a profound influence on Taiwanese, Chinese, and global art cinema. It has high cultural and historical value as a record of Taiwan's social changes, but also has limitations including narrow thematic scope and low commercial appeal.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### 2.1.1. Core Problem to Solve
Hou Hsiao-hsien is widely recognized as the most representative figure of Taiwan New Cinema, and his unique film style has been the subject of extensive prior research. However, existing studies are mostly fragmented: they focus on isolated elements such as his long take technique, poetic aesthetic, or historical themes, but do not analyze his narrative as an integrated, interconnected system. This paper aims to address this gap by constructing a complete analytical framework for Hou's narrative.
### 2.1.2. Importance of the Problem
Hou's work is not only artistically significant, but also functions as a "cultural record" of Taiwan's social and historical changes from the post-WWII period to the early 2000s. Understanding his narrative system helps clarify the development of Chinese-language art film, and the intersection between traditional Chinese aesthetics and modern film art.
### 2.1.3. Innovative Entry Point
The paper's unique innovation is its integration of Western literary theory (Bakhtin's polyphony) and traditional Chinese aesthetic theory (Wang Guowei's "enter-exit" theory, *qiyun* aesthetics) to analyze both the formal and cultural dimensions of Hou's narrative, rather than relying solely on Western film theory frameworks.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1.  **Structural framework construction**: The paper is the first to systematically divide Hou's narrative into three interdependent layers: narrative construction (structure), narrative theme (content), and narrative language (form), creating a unified analytical model for his work.
2.  **Theoretical application**: It successfully applies polyphony theory to explain Hou's parallel narrative space, demonstrating that his films have multiple equally valid independent "voices" (character consciousnesses, storylines) rather than a single dominant authorial narrative.
3.  **Critical evaluation**: It provides a balanced assessment of both the artistic contributions (unique poetic aesthetic, cultural record value) and limitations (narrow thematic scope, low commercial accessibility) of Hou's narrative system, avoiding the overly celebratory tone of many prior studies.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
To understand this paper, readers need to be familiar with the following core concepts, which are explained for beginners:
### 3.1.1. Taiwan New Cinema
A film movement that took place in Taiwan from 1982 to 1987, led by Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, and other young directors. It rejected the formulaic commercial melodramas and political propaganda films that dominated Taiwanese cinema before the 1980s, and instead focused on realistic, autobiographical depictions of Taiwanese daily life, social history, and personal memory. Key formal characteristics include location shooting, non-professional actors, long takes, and slow, understated pacing.
### 3.1.2. Polyphony Theory
Originated as a music term referring to a composition with two or more independent, equally important melody lines played simultaneously, with no single dominant line. Russian literary theorist Mikhail Bakhtin adapted the term for literary studies to describe narratives that feature multiple independent, equally valid character consciousnesses/voices, rather than a single "monologue" voice controlled entirely by the author.
### 3.1.3. Long Take
A shot that runs for an extended period (often several minutes) without cutting, preserving the complete continuity of time and space in the scene. It is the opposite of *montage*, the technique of cutting together short separate shots to create symbolic meaning or advance plot quickly. Hou's signature long takes often use a fixed, stationary camera position.
### 3.1.4. *Qiyun* Editing
A unique editing style developed by Hou and his long-time editor Liao Qingsong, based on the traditional Chinese aesthetic concept of *qiyun* (literally "qi rhythm," referring to the inherent rhythmic vitality of a work of art). Unlike traditional editing, which follows linear plot logic, *qiyun* editing cuts between shots based on the emotional rhythm of the scene, blurring the boundary between past, present, and future.
### 3.1.5. Wang Guowei's "Enter-Exit" Theory
From the 20th century Chinese literary theorist Wang Guowei's work *Ren Jian Ci Hua* (Remarks on Lyric Poetry of the Human World). The theory states that a creator must both "enter" (immerse themselves fully in the world they are depicting to capture its authenticity and emotional truth) and "exit" (step outside of the depicted world to observe it objectively, achieving a detached, transcendent artistic perspective).
## 3.2. Previous Works
The paper categorizes existing research on Hou Hsiao-hsien into four main groups:
1.  **Thematic studies**: Focus on the content of Hou's films, particularly their exploration of Taiwanese identity, collective memory, and post-colonial history.
2.  **Aesthetic studies**: Analyze Hou's poetic, prose-like aesthetic style, often comparing it to traditional Chinese landscape painting or lyric poetry.
3.  **Narrative studies**: The largest body of prior work, which focuses almost exclusively on Hou's use of long takes and fixed camera techniques, without analyzing how these formal techniques integrate with narrative structure and theme.
4.  **Comparative studies**: Compare Hou's work with other directors, most frequently Taiwanese New Cinema co-founder Edward Yang, or Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu, whose slow, family-focused style is often cited as an influence on Hou.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Hou's own creative career evolved in four distinct phases, which parallel the development of Taiwanese cinema and global art film:
1.  Early commercial phase (1980-1982): Hou directed formulaic romantic comedies to establish himself in the Taiwanese film industry, with only subtle hints of his later realist style.
2.  Memory phase (1983-1987): Following the start of the Taiwan New Cinema movement, Hou shifted to autobiographical, personal memory-focused films, establishing his signature long take style.
3.  Historical phase (1989-1995): Hou expanded from personal memory to collective historical reflection, creating his famous "Sadness Trilogy" that addressed previously suppressed periods of Taiwan's history (Japanese colonial rule, 228 Incident, White Terror).
4.  Urban phase (1996-2005): Hou shifted his focus to contemporary urban life, exploring the disorientation of Taiwanese youth amid rapid globalization and urbanization.
    This paper's work covers all four phases, placing Hou's narrative evolution within the broader context of Taiwan's social and cultural changes.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior research, this paper's core innovation is its holistic approach: rather than analyzing single formal or thematic elements in isolation, it demonstrates how Hou's narrative structure, thematic content, and audiovisual language form a unified, interdependent system. It also moves beyond purely descriptive analysis to provide a balanced critical evaluation of the strengths and limitations of Hou's narrative system.

# 4. Methodology
The paper's core methodology is close textual analysis of Hou's complete filmography, combined with theoretical framing from literary and aesthetic theory. It is structured in four layers, as outlined below:
## 4.1. Principles
The paper's core analytical principle is that narrative is not limited to plot structure, but is an integrated system consisting of three mutually supportive layers:
1.  The structural layer (narrative construction: space and time organization)
2.  The content layer (narrative themes)
3.  The formal layer (narrative language: audiovisual techniques)
    Changes in any one layer affect the other two, so they must be analyzed in connection with each other.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The analysis proceeds in four sequential steps:
### 4.2.1. Step 1: Analysis of Narrative Construction (Structural Layer)
This step uses two theoretical frameworks to analyze how Hou organizes narrative space and time:
#### 4.2.1.1. Parallel Narrative Space (Polyphony Theory)
The paper argues that Hou's films use a polyphonic structure with multiple independent, equally weighted narrative "voices" (semi-independent storylines, character consciousnesses) that develop in parallel, with no single dominant plot or protagonist. Three specific narrative strategies are used to achieve this:
1.  **Parallel narration**: Multiple disconnected timelines or storylines run simultaneously, often connected only by thematic or emotional links rather than plot causality. For example, *Good Men, Good Women* (1995) features three fully parallel storylines: actress Liang Jing's present-day life, her memories of her past relationship with her deceased boyfriend, and the historical story of 1950s political activist Jiang Biyu that Liang Jing is playing in a film. The three storylines are given equal narrative weight, with no single "main" plot.
2.  **Background rotation**: Storylines are switched by changing the physical setting/background, similar to curtain changes in traditional theater, rather than explicit plot transitions. For example, *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998) has no exterior shots at all, and switches between different brothel rooms using fade in/fade out transitions, with oil lamp lighting used to signal the start and end of each scene.
3.  **Character juxtaposition**: Multiple characters in the same shared setting have parallel, equally weighted narrative arcs, with no single protagonist. For example, *A City of Sadness* (1989) follows four brothers from the same Lin family during the 1947 228 Incident, each with their own independent tragic fate, and all given roughly equal screen time.
#### 4.2.1.2. Flexible Narrative Time (Wang Guowei's "Enter-Exit" Theory)
Hou's narrative does not follow linear chronological time, but jumps freely between past, present, and future, requiring the director, actor, and audience to "enter" (immerse in) and "exit" (detach from) different narrative contexts freely. Two formal techniques enable this flexible time:
1.  **Long take**: The extended, uncut shot preserves the continuity of time within a single scene, allowing the audience to fully immerse in the emotional rhythm of the moment without being pulled out by cuts.
2.  **Qiyun editing**: Cuts between shots are made based on the emotional rhythm of the scene rather than linear plot logic, which blurs the boundary between different time periods. For example, *Millennium Mambo* (2001) has no clear chronological order, and cuts between different periods of the protagonist Vicky's life based on her emotional state, rather than a linear timeline of events.
### 4.2.2. Step 2: Analysis of Narrative Themes (Content Layer)
This step categorizes all of Hou's films into three core thematic clusters, which correspond to his four creative phases:
1.  **Personal memory themes**: Focus on individual growth across the lifespan, including childhood naivety (*A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, 1985), adolescent rebellion and first love (*The Boys from Fengkuei*, 1983; *Dust in the Wind*, 1986), and old age recollection of a lifetime of experience (*The Puppetmaster*, 1993).
2.  **Historical reflection themes**: Focus on Taiwan's turbulent 20th century history through the perspective of ordinary people, avoiding grand, top-down historical narratives. These films form the "Sadness Trilogy": *A City of Sadness* (1947 228 Incident), *The Puppetmaster* (Japanese colonial rule 1895-1945), *Good Men, Good Women* (1950s White Terror period).
3.  **Urban disillusionment themes**: Focus on the conflict between traditional rural civilization and modern urban consumer culture, depicting the disorientation and disillusionment of young people in contemporary Taiwanese cities. Representative works include *Goodbye South, Goodbye* (1996) and *Millennium Mambo* (2001).
### 4.2.3. Step 3: Analysis of Narrative Language (Formal Layer)
This step analyzes the audiovisual techniques that support Hou's narrative structure and themes, grouped into three categories:
1.  **Color**: Hou uses a single, heavy overall color tone for each film, with a consistent preference for warm pale yellow tones to create a nostalgic, calm, dreamy atmosphere. For example, *The Puppetmaster* uses a yellowed, aged tone to evoke the feeling of old memories, while *Flowers of Shanghai* uses dim yellow oil lamp light to create an ambiguous, enclosed, dream-like atmosphere in the brothel setting.
2.  **Sound**: Three distinct characteristics define Hou's sound design:
    - Diverse character languages: Characters speak a wide range of dialects (Minnan, Hakka, Suzhou dialect, Japanese, Cantonese) depending on their identity, background, and the historical period of the film, reflecting Taiwan's multi-cultural colonial and immigrant history.
    - Light, melodic background music: Scores are often acoustic, slow, and understated, avoiding dramatic swells that would manipulate audience emotion.
    - Extensive use of subtitles and voiceover: Subtitles are used to provide historical context at the start/end of films, while voiceover is used to convey character memory and inner psychology without breaking the calm narrative rhythm.
3.  **Camera and editing**: Long takes, fixed stationary camera positions, and *qiyun* editing are used consistently across all phases of Hou's career to support his slow, poetic narrative rhythm.
### 4.2.4. Step 4: Critical Evaluation of Hou's Narrative System
The final step provides a balanced assessment of the strengths and limitations of Hou's narrative system, as outlined in the results section below.

# 5. Research Corpus & Analytical Framework
As a humanities film studies thesis, this work does not use STEM-style experimental datasets and metrics, but instead uses the following research corpus and analytical criteria:
## 5.1. Research Corpus
The paper analyzes the full filmography of Hou Hsiao-hsien up to 2012, covering 17 feature films directed by Hou between 1980 and 2005, plus his short films. The corpus is grouped into the four creative phases defined in Section 3.3 above.
This corpus is fully representative of Hou's career, as it includes all of his most critically acclaimed award-winning works, and covers every stage of his stylistic evolution.
## 5.2. Analytical Criteria
The paper uses three interconnected criteria to evaluate the effectiveness of Hou's narrative system:
1.  **Narrative structure validity**: Measures whether the polyphonic parallel structure and flexible time processing successfully create multiple equally valid narrative voices, and produce the intended calm, immersive aesthetic effect.
2.  **Narrative theme depth**: Measures whether the themes of personal memory, historical reflection, and urban anxiety effectively reflect Taiwan's social and cultural changes, and resonate with audience experiences.
3.  **Narrative language expressiveness**: Measures whether the audiovisual language (color, sound, camera work, editing) effectively supports the narrative structure and themes, and forms a consistent, unique artistic style.
    For each criterion, the paper uses close analysis of specific film examples to verify effectiveness.
## 5.3. Baseline Narrative Models
The paper compares Hou's narrative system against two dominant baseline narrative models in film history to highlight its uniqueness:
1.  **Traditional commercial film narrative**: Features a single protagonist, clear cause-effect plot, strictly linear chronological time, fast editing, and explicit dramatic conflict designed to attract mass audiences.
2.  **Traditional art film narrative**: Often uses linear or simple flashback structure, a single core theme, and focuses on a single central character's psychological arc.
    Hou's polyphonic, non-linear, multi-voice narrative differs significantly from both baseline models.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
### 6.1.1. Narrative Structure Results
The analysis finds that Hou's polyphonic narrative structure successfully breaks the constraints of single linear narrative:
- The parallel narrative space creates a democratic narrative where no single character or storyline is given priority, allowing audiences to focus on the emotional state of each character rather than following a pre-determined plot. For example, the three parallel timelines in *Good Men, Good Women* blur the boundary between personal memory, current life, and historical memory, creating a complex reflection on how individual lives are shaped by larger historical forces.
- The flexible narrative time, enabled by long takes and *qiyun* editing, creates a "flowing" sense of time similar to the aesthetic of traditional Chinese landscape painting, where time is experienced immersively rather than as a linear sequence of events. For example, *Dust in the Wind* cuts between scenes based on the quiet, understated emotional rhythm of the young couple's love story, rather than plot twists, creating a poetic feeling of youth slowly passing by.
### 6.1.2. Narrative Theme Results
Hou's three thematic clusters form a comprehensive, multi-generational record of Taiwan's social changes from the 1940s to the 2000s:
- Personal memory films are not purely autobiographical, but reflect the collective experience of the entire post-war generation of Taiwanese who grew up in rural areas amid rapid social change, with strong nostalgia for traditional rural values.
- Historical films fill a major gap in Taiwan's cinematic representation of suppressed historical events, using ordinary people's individual suffering to make abstract historical trauma tangible and emotionally resonant, avoiding the propaganda tone of official historical narratives.
- Urban films reflect the universal experience of young people in rapidly globalizing East Asian cities, depicting the anxiety, disorientation, and loss of traditional values that accompany rapid urbanization.
### 6.1.3. Narrative Language Results
Hou's audiovisual language is highly aligned with his narrative structure and themes:
- The single, heavy color tone and preference for warm yellow tones effectively create a nostalgic, calm atmosphere that matches the themes of memory and historical reflection.
- The use of diverse dialects accurately reflects Taiwan's multi-cultural historical background, while the understated music and extensive use of voiceover/subtitles preserve the calm, detached narrative rhythm without breaking audience immersion.
## 6.2. Strengths and Limitations Analysis
### 6.2.1. Strengths of Hou's Narrative System
1.  **Structural innovation**: The polyphonic parallel structure breaks the linear narrative model, providing more space for audience interpretation and reflection, rather than forcing a single authorial meaning on the audience.
2.  **Cultural and historical value**: The focus on ordinary people's daily lives creates a detailed "cultural record" of Taiwan's social changes over half a century, with significant historical research value.
3.  **Aesthetic influence**: The unique poetic, prose-style aesthetic created by long takes, fixed camera, and *qiyun* editing has influenced generations of East Asian art film directors, including mainland Chinese directors Jia Zhangke and Diao Yinan, and Japanese director Hirokazu Kore-eda.
### 6.2.2. Limitations of Hou's Narrative System
1.  **Narrow thematic and formal scope**: The consistent focus on Taiwan's local memory and history, combined with the fragmentary, slow-paced plot structure, makes the films difficult to access for audiences unfamiliar with Taiwan's history and culture, limiting their cross-cultural appeal.
2.  **Low commercial accessibility**: The lack of dramatic conflict, slow pacing, and long takes lead to poor box office performance compared to commercial films, and make the films unappealing to mass audiences accustomed to fast-paced commercial narrative.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This thesis provides the first systematic, holistic analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien's narrative system, integrating Western and Chinese aesthetic theory to demonstrate how Hou's narrative structure, thematic content, and audiovisual language form a unified, unique artistic system. It concludes that Hou's narrative represents a successful fusion of traditional Chinese aesthetic spirit (detached observation, *qiyun* rhythm, poetic implication) and modern film art, which has made an important contribution to the development of Chinese-language art film, and provides a new direction for art film narrative that prioritizes emotional truth over plot drama.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The author identifies the following limitations of the study, and suggests future research directions:
1.  The paper focuses only on formal and thematic analysis of the films themselves, and does not conduct empirical research on audience reception, particularly cross-cultural reception among non-Chinese speaking audiences.
2.  The analysis of Hou's later urban phase films is relatively less detailed than the analysis of his memory and historical phase films, and does not fully address the transformation of his narrative style when he shifted to more universal, non-Taiwan specific themes (e.g., *Café Lumière*, 2003, set in Japan as a tribute to Yasujiro Ozu).
3.  Future research could conduct comparative studies between Hou's narrative and that of other East Asian art film directors, to identify common characteristics of East Asian art film narrative, or study Hou's influence on contemporary young Chinese-language directors.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper is a highly valuable foundational study for understanding Hou Hsiao-hsien's work, and its holistic analytical framework can be adapted to study the narrative of other art film directors with strong personal styles. However, it has several areas that could be improved:
1.  The paper's discussion of the limitations of Hou's narrative is relatively superficial: it frames the low commercial appeal and narrow thematic scope as flaws specific to Hou's work, but these are common, structural challenges faced by nearly all art film directors who prioritize artistic expression over mass market appeal. A deeper analysis could explore the productive tension between artistic pursuit and commercial accessibility in art film, rather than framing it as a personal limitation.
2.  The paper's application of polyphony theory is mostly descriptive, and does not address potential downsides of Hou's polyphonic structure: for example, the equal weighting of multiple storylines sometimes weakens the emotional impact of his historical themes, as audiences are not given a central character to identify with.
3.  The paper does not address the gender dimensions of Hou's narrative: his films are almost exclusively focused on male experiences and perspectives, with female characters often framed as secondary objects of male memory or desire, which is a notable limitation of his thematic scope that is not discussed in the paper.
    Overall, this paper provides a clear, comprehensive introduction to Hou's narrative system, and is an excellent starting point for further research on this important director.
