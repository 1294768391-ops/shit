## 1.5. Abstract
The research objective is to analyze how Hou Hsiao-hsien’s creative subjectivity is embodied in his formal cinematic techniques. Its core methodology combines close formal analysis of Hou’s film texts, comparative analysis against Bazinian realist film theory, and linkage of his techniques to traditional Chinese aesthetic and philosophical concepts. Its main findings confirm that Hou’s film language is an organic combination of contemplative long takes, multi-functional empty shots, and anti-linear editing, which balances an objective recording perspective with the subjective expression of artistic conception. It also finds that while Hou’s style shares formal similarities with Bazinian realism, it differs fundamentally by integrating the creator’s subjective consciousness rather than pursuing pure objective recording. Its key conclusion is that Hou’s film aesthetics transcends the subject-object opposition of Western theoretical frameworks, merges documentary and poetic film traits, and achieves a perfect combination of realism and freehand (xieyi) expression.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem & Field Importance
Prior global film studies often either categorize Hou Hsiao-hsien’s style as a direct application of Western Bazinian realist theory, or only discuss his formal techniques in isolation without linking them to his creative subjectivity and Eastern cultural roots. This gap is significant because it erases the originality of East Asian cinematic traditions, framing non-Western film works as derivatives of Western theoretical paradigms.
### Research Entry Point
The paper’s innovative entry point is framing Hou’s three core formal techniques (long take, empty shot, anti-linear editing) as a unified, conscious expression of his subjectivity, rooted in traditional Chinese aesthetics (poetry, painting, philosophy) rather than Western film theory. It argues that his style intentionally transcends the subject-object divide embedded in Western realist frameworks.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
The paper’s four primary contributions are:
1.  It systematically deconstructs the dual realist-lyrical nature of Hou’s long take aesthetics, clearly distinguishing it from both Bazinian objective realism and the dynamic long take traditions of Western directors such as Andrei Tarkovsky and Theodoros Angelopoulos.
2.  It explains the three core functions of Hou’s empty shots, linking them directly to the traditional Chinese art concepts of `liubai` (blank space in painting) and `xing` (evocative rhetorical device in poetry).
3.  It analyzes his unique anti-linear editing methods (`qiyun`/rhythmic vitality editing, cloud block editing) as rooted in the Chinese philosophical principle of `yi wu guan wu` (viewing things as they are, without subjective bias).
4.  It concludes that Hou’s film aesthetics is a highly original, independent East Asian cinematic system that merges documentary realism and poetic expression, rather than a derivative of Western film theory.

    These findings solve the core problem of mis-categorization of Hou’s work in prior research, providing a culturally rooted, nuanced framework for analyzing his style.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core technical and cultural terms are defined below for beginner readers:
1.  **Long take**: A continuous, uncut shot that lasts for an extended duration (usually 30 seconds or longer, with no strict universal definition), as opposed to *montage* (the practice of cutting together short, discontinuous shots to create meaning). Long takes preserve the continuity of on-screen time and space, and are traditionally associated with realist film aesthetics.
2.  **Empty shot**: Also called landscape shot, a shot that contains no human characters, usually depicting natural scenery, inanimate objects, or built environments. Unlike plot-driven shots, empty shots are primarily used to convey emotion, set mood, create metaphor, or build artistic conception.
3.  **Anti-linear narrative**: A narrative structure that does not follow conventional chronological cause-and-effect order (past → present → future). It often uses flashbacks, intentional omissions, jump cuts, or blurred temporal boundaries to prioritize emotional or thematic logic over straightforward plot progression.
4.  **Bazinian realism**: A film theory framework developed by French critic André Bazin, which argues that film’s core strength is its ability to objectively record reality. It advocates for long takes and deep focus shots, and discourages manipulative montage that forces a single specific interpretation on audiences, to preserve the inherent ambiguity and multi-layered meaning of real life.
5.  **`Liubai` (blank space)**: A core concept in traditional Chinese painting and poetry, referring to intentional empty, unmarked areas left in a work of art. These spaces invite audiences to use their own imagination to fill in gaps, amplify artistic conception, and highlight the core subject of the work.
6.  **`Xing` (evocation)**: One of three core rhetorical devices in ancient Chinese poetry (alongside *fu* [direct narration] and *bi* [metaphor]), defined as "first describing unrelated natural phenomena to引出 the core theme you wish to express". It uses subtle, indirect association to trigger emotional resonance in audiences, rather than explicit statement.
7.  **`Yi wu guan wu` (viewing things as they are)**: A philosophical concept proposed by Northern Song dynasty scholar Shao Yong, referring to the practice of observing the world without imposing personal subjective biases, desires, or judgments, so as to perceive the true nature of things. It is contrasted with *yi wo guan wu* (viewing things from the self’s perspective), which distorts reality to fit personal emotion and interest.
8.  **`Jing you` (mirror roaming) aesthetics**: A Chinese film theory concept proposed by scholar Lin Niantong, which links Chinese cinematic aesthetics to the viewing logic of traditional Chinese scroll painting. It describes a style where the camera moves slowly across a scene, as if the viewer is unrolling a scroll and observing it gradually, using scattered perspective rather than the fixed linear perspective of Western painting.
## 3.2. Previous Works
Key prior studies referenced by the paper include:
1.  **André Bazin, *What is Cinema?* (1967)**: The foundational text of realist film theory, which introduces the "mummy complex" (film as a mechanism to preserve reality by replicating its physical appearance), argues for the inherent objectivity of photographic imagery, and advocates for long takes and deep focus to minimize directorial manipulation of audiences. This is the core Western theoretical baseline the paper uses to contrast Hou’s work.
2.  **Jiao Xiongping, *Reflections on Chinese Cinema* (2005)**: A core work of Chinese film scholarship that first argues Hou Hsiao-hsien’s style is not primarily derived from Western film influence, but is a natural expression of aesthetic sensibilities shaped by Chinese philosophy, religion, and poetic traditions. The paper builds directly on this argument.
3.  **Lin Niantong, *Chinese Film Aesthetics* (1991)**: Introduces the `jing you` aesthetics framework, which the paper uses to explain the logic of Hou’s later moving long take style in works like *Flowers of Shanghai* and *The Assassin*.
4.  **Liao Qingsong, editing theory (1990, 2015)**: Hou Hsiao-hsien’s long-time collaborating editor, who first documented the `qiyun` editing and cloud block editing methods developed for Hou’s films, and linked these editing principles to the Taoist concept of `yi wu guan wu`. This is the primary source for the paper’s analysis of Hou’s anti-linear editing techniques.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The evolution of relevant film aesthetic traditions is traced below:
- 1940s–1950s: André Bazin develops realist film theory, which becomes the theoretical foundation for Italian Neorealism and later global new wave cinema movements that use long takes to achieve realistic, unmanipulated depictions of life.
- 1960s–1980s: Western art cinema directors (Andrei Tarkovsky, Theodoros Angelopoulos, Yasujirō Ozu) develop distinct personal long take aesthetics, combining realist formal traits with individual poetic expression.
- 1980s: The New Taiwan Cinema movement (led by Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, and others) emerges, developing a localized cinematic style that draws both on Western realist techniques and traditional Chinese aesthetic traditions to tell stories of Taiwan’s local history and social change.
- 1990s–2000s: Prior research on Hou’s work mostly focuses either on isolated formal analysis of his long takes, or thematic analysis of his depictions of Taiwan history, without systematically linking form to his creative subjectivity and Eastern aesthetic roots. This 2021 paper is part of a later wave of research that frames Hou’s work as an independent, original East Asian cinematic system, rather than a derivative of Western theory.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior related work, this paper has three core innovations:
1.  **Systematic formal analysis**: Unlike earlier studies that only discuss one of Hou’s techniques (e.g., only long takes), this paper analyzes the organic combination of all three core formal components of his style (long take, empty shot, anti-linear editing) as a unified expression of his creative subjectivity.
2.  **Clear theoretical distinction from Western realism**: Unlike studies that categorize Hou’s work as a direct application of Bazinian theory, this paper clearly distinguishes the two: Hou’s realism is infused with subjective lyrical expression and Eastern aesthetic thought, rather than pursuing pure objective recording as Bazin advocated.
3.  **Culturally rooted explanation**: Unlike prior formal analysis that does not link technique to local cultural contexts, this paper connects each of Hou’s techniques to specific traditional Chinese aesthetic and philosophical concepts, providing a more accurate, context-specific explanation of his style.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The paper’s core methodological principle is **formalist close reading of film texts**, combined with **cross-theoretical comparative analysis** between Western film theory and Chinese traditional aesthetic/philosophical thought. Its underlying intuition is that cinematic form is not a neutral technical choice, but a direct expression of a creator’s worldview, aesthetic orientation, and creative subjectivity. The method aims to demonstrate how Hou’s formal techniques work together to create a unique aesthetic system that transcends the subject-object opposition embedded in Western film frameworks.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The paper’s analysis proceeds in three interlinked, sequential layers:
### Layer 1: Analysis of Long Take Aesthetics
Step 1: Identify formal features of Hou’s long takes across his career:
- Early career (1980s works such as *The Boys from Fengkuei*, *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*): Predominantly fixed-position, distant long takes, with minimal camera movement, very few close-ups, and up to 1/5 of all shots being wide landscape shots.
- Mid-to-late career (1990s–2010s works such as *Flowers of Shanghai*, *The Assassin*): Slow-moving long takes that follow the `jing you` aesthetics of Chinese scroll painting, with the camera panning gradually to reveal scenes, rather than using dynamic, expressive movement as seen in Western long take works.
  Step 2: Compare against Bazinian realism baseline:
- Point of similarity: Both use long takes to preserve the continuity of on-screen time and space, avoiding manipulative montage that forces a single interpretation on audiences.
- Point of difference: Bazin advocated long takes to eliminate directorial subjective interference and preserve the objective ambiguity of reality, while Hou uses long takes to deliberately create lyrical mood and artistic conception, pursuing psychological and emotional realism (authenticity of character mood and experience) rather than just surface-level factual realism.
  Step 3: Link to Eastern aesthetic roots:
- Hou’s fixed distant long take style is explicitly influenced by writer Shen Congwen’s lyrical objective perspective, which treats even tragic life events (death, conflict, loss) with a detached, non-judgmental distance. This creates a psychological distance (per Edward Bullough’s psychological distance theory) that allows audiences to observe events objectively while also accessing their aesthetic and emotional meaning, achieving a balance between realism and lyricism.
### Layer 2: Analysis of Empty Shot Functions
Step 1: Identify formal features of Hou’s empty shots:
- Large volume of empty shots (up to 1/5 of all shots in works such as *Dust in the Wind*), almost exclusively depicting natural scenery (mountains, sea, sky, weather changes, rural landscapes).
  Step 2: Link to `liubai` (blank space) concept in Chinese painting:
- Empty shots act as cinematic blank space, inviting audiences to use their own imagination to fill in emotional gaps, deepen the film’s artistic conception, and express the traditional Chinese ideal of *tian ren he yi* (harmony between human beings and nature). Humans are framed as small, integrated parts of the larger natural world, rather than the absolute center of the narrative.
  Step 3: Link to `xing` (evocation) concept in Chinese poetry:
- Empty shots of natural changes (e.g., dark clouds before a character’s death, thunder during a gathering of dissident intellectuals) are used to evoke emotion, hint at plot development, and convey thematic meaning without explicit explicit explanation, aligning with the indirect, suggestive expressive logic of traditional Chinese poetry.
### Layer 3: Analysis of Anti-linear Editing Logic
Step 1: Identify formal features of Hou’s editing style:
- Complete rejection of conventional commercial film editing techniques (fast cutting, close-ups for dramatic emphasis, cross-cutting, linear chronological sequencing).
- Use of two unique editing methods developed with editor Liao Qingsong:
  1.  **`Qiyun` (rhythmic vitality) editing (used in *City of Sadness*)**: Editing decisions are based on the emotional and rhythmic connection between shots, rather than chronological or causal plot logic. This blurs the boundaries between past, present, and future, prioritizing the overall poetic atmosphere of the work over clear narrative progression.
  2.  **Cloud block editing (used in *The Puppetmaster*)**: Documentary footage of real-life subject Li Tianlu’s first-person narration is interwoven with fictional dramatic footage of his life, with no clear transitions between the two types of footage. The edits follow the natural rhythm of Li’s speech, overlapping like cloud blocks in the sky to blend historical and present temporalities seamlessly.
      Step 2: Link to `yi wu guan wu` philosophical principle:
- Both editing methods align with the principle of viewing things as they are, requiring the creator to set aside personal subjective bias and avoid forcing explicit dramatic or interpretive guidance on audiences. Instead, audiences are invited to perceive the work’s inherent emotional and thematic logic intuitively, accessing the truth of the story without directorial interference.

They are fully representative of his creative practice, making them effective for validating the paper’s argument about the unity and continuity of his film language and subjectivity.
## 5.2. Analytical Framework (Equivalent to Evaluation Metrics)
The paper uses a dual, balanced analytical framework to evaluate Hou’s film language:
1.  **Western film theory evaluation criteria**: Measures the alignment of Hou’s work with Bazinian realist principles, with core indicators including: degree of objective recording of reality, degree of directorial subjective intervention in the text, and preservation of temporal and spatial continuity.
2.  **Chinese aesthetic evaluation criteria**: Measures the alignment of Hou’s work with traditional Chinese aesthetic and philosophical principles, with core indicators including: strength of artistic conception, lyrical quality, expression of harmony between human and nature, and degree of non-interventionist presentation of reality.
## 5.3. Comparative Baselines
The paper compares Hou’s techniques against two representative baseline traditions in global cinema:
1.  **Western realist long take tradition**: Represented by Bazinian theoretical principles and the works of Western long take masters (Andrei Tarkovsky, Theodoros Angelopoulos) who use dynamic, expressive long takes. This baseline is representative because it is the dominant existing framework for analyzing long take aesthetics in global film studies.
2.  **Conventional commercial narrative tradition**: Represented by standard Hollywood editing and shooting techniques (fast cutting, close-ups for dramatic emphasis, linear cause-and-effect narrative, cross-cutting), which prioritize plot advancement and audience immersion in dramatic tension. This baseline is representative because it is the dominant conventional film language that Hou deliberately rejected in his creative practice.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The paper’s three core findings from its layered analysis are outlined below:
### Finding 1: Long take aesthetics balances realism and lyricism, distinct from Western traditions
- Compared to Bazinian realism: While both use long takes to preserve temporal and spatial continuity, Hou’s long takes are infused with subjective lyrical intent, pursuing psychological and emotional realism rather than Bazin’s ideal of pure objective recording. For example, Hou’s long takes of violent conflict in *The Boys from Fengkuei* and *City of Sadness* avoid close-ups and dramatic framing, instead using distant fixed shots to aestheticize violence and create emotional distance, rather than just recording it objectively.
- Compared to Western long take masters: Unlike Tarkovsky and Angelopoulos who use dynamic, moving long takes to express personal philosophical views, Hou’s early long takes are almost entirely fixed and distant, while his later moving long takes follow the slow, gradual `jing you` logic of Chinese scroll painting, rather than the expressive, intentional movement of Western long takes.
### Finding 2: Empty shots serve three core culturally rooted functions
- **Mood setting function**: Natural scenery empty shots establish a quiet, understated tone that matches the subtle, undramatic narrative of Hou’s works. For example, the 12 pure empty shots of mountains, sea, and sky in *Dust in the Wind* reinforce the film’s theme of quiet, fleeting young love, without explicit narrative explanation.
- **Philosophical expression function**: Empty shots express the traditional Chinese view of harmony between human and nature, framing humans as small parts of the larger natural world rather than the narrative center. For example, multiple long shots in *Dust in the Wind* show characters as tiny, almost invisible figures in wide mountain landscapes, emphasizing that individual human stories are part of the larger, eternal natural order.
- **Evocative (`xing`) function**: Empty shots of weather or environmental changes hint at plot development and character fate without explicit statement. For example, the 37-second fixed empty shot of a landscape with thunder in the background, cut after a group of intellectuals sing a patriotic protest song in *City of Sadness*, directly hints at their tragic future persecution under the Kuomintang government, without any explicit depiction of violence.
### Finding 3: Anti-linear editing prioritizes emotional and thematic logic, rooted in `yi wu guan wu` principle
- `Qiyun` editing in *City of Sadness* blurs temporal boundaries between 1945 and 1949, allowing the film to present the complex, traumatic history of Taiwan’s post-war period through subtle, indirect depiction, while still retaining strong internal emotional tension and epic quality, avoiding the simplistic, one-sided historical narrative of conventional historical dramas.
- Cloud block editing in *The Puppetmaster* seamlessly blends documentary and fictional footage, creating a poetic, lingering presentation of Li Tianlu’s personal life and Taiwan’s Japanese occupation era, prioritizing the emotional truth of historical experience over strict chronological narrative accuracy.
  Across all three techniques, the analysis confirms that Hou’s film language is a unified system that balances objective perspective and subjective artistic conception, surpassing the subject-object opposition of Western film theory, merging documentary and poetic traits to achieve a perfect combination of realism and freehand expression.
## 6.2. Component Effect Analysis (Equivalent to Ablation Studies)
The paper implicitly analyzes the independent and combined effects of each of Hou’s three core techniques:
- **Long takes alone**: Provide the realist, grounded texture of Hou’s work, but without empty shots and anti-linear editing, they would lack the lyrical, artistic conception that defines his style.
- **Empty shots alone**: Provide the aesthetic space for audience imagination and emotional resonance, but without the realist foundation of long takes and the emotional continuity of anti-linear editing, they would feel disconnected from the narrative and like irrelevant decorative scenery.
- **Anti-linear editing alone**: Provides the structural logic for poetic narrative, but without the realist texture of long takes and the mood setting of empty shots, it would feel like a formal experimental gimmick disconnected from emotional reality.
  Only the combination of all three techniques creates the unique, original Hou Hsiao-hsien aesthetic.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
The paper concludes that Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film language, consisting of contemplative long takes, multi-functional empty shots, and anti-linear editing, is a unique, intentional expression of his creative subjectivity. While it shares formal similarities with Bazinian realist theory, it is fundamentally distinct in that it integrates the creator’s subjective consciousness and Eastern aesthetic thought, rather than pursuing pure objective recording. His aesthetic system transcends the subject-object opposition embedded in Western film theory, merges documentary realism and poetic film traits, and achieves a perfect combination of realism and freehand expression, representing a highly original East Asian cinematic aesthetic rooted in Chinese philosophy, poetry, and art traditions.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Implicit Limitations
The paper does not explicitly state its limitations, but two key gaps are identifiable:
1.  It focuses almost exclusively on the formal and aesthetic dimensions of Hou’s work, with minimal analysis of how his creative subjectivity is shaped by his specific historical and social context (Taiwan’s post-war social changes, the political context of the New Taiwan Cinema movement, his personal positionality as a mainland-born waishengren director in Taiwan).
2.  It only compares Hou’s work to Western realist traditions and Chinese aesthetic traditions, with no cross-comparison to other East Asian poetic cinema directors (e.g., Yasujirō Ozu, Apichatpong Weerasethakul, Hong Sang-soo) that would further highlight the uniqueness of his style.
### Future Research Directions
Potential future research pathways include:
1.  Exploring the interaction between Hou’s formal techniques and his thematic content, particularly his political positioning in his representations of Taiwan’s traumatic 20th-century history.
2.  Conducting cross-cultural comparisons between Hou’s aesthetic system and other East Asian poetic film traditions, to develop a more generalized framework for analyzing non-Western art cinema.
3.  Conducting audience reception studies to verify whether the intended aesthetic effects of Hou’s techniques (e.g., intuitive emotional resonance, perception of artistic conception) are consistent across audiences from different cultural backgrounds.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper is a highly valuable contribution to global film studies, as it moves beyond the common tendency in Western-centric scholarship to frame non-Western film styles as derivatives of Western theoretical paradigms, instead providing a culturally rooted, context-specific analysis of Hou’s work as an independent, original aesthetic system. Its methodological approach of linking formal cinematic techniques to local aesthetic and philosophical traditions can be transferred to the analysis of other non-Western film directors, helping to de-center Western film theory and develop more diverse, inclusive frameworks for global film studies.
Potential areas for improvement include:
- It could incorporate concrete quantitative formal data (e.g., average shot length of Hou’s films compared to Western long take works, statistical breakdown of the share of empty shots across his filmography) to provide more empirical support for its formal analysis.
- It could explore the political dimension of Hou’s subjectivity more explicitly, for example how his `yi wu guan wu` editing approach to representing Taiwan’s history is a deliberate political choice to avoid simplistic, one-sided historical narratives that serve partisan ideological interests.
- It could add analysis of the global influence of Hou’s aesthetic system, particularly its impact on later generations of Chinese-language and global art film directors, to further demonstrate the significance of his work beyond the East Asian context.
