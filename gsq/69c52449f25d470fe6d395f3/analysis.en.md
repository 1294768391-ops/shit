# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The full title is *On The Culture Documental Features of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s Film* (Chinese: 侯孝贤电影的文化记录性研究). Its core topic is to systematically explore the unique cultural recording function of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film works, integrating ethnography theory, film aesthetics, and comparative analysis with other representative Chinese directors.
## 1.2. Authors
- Primary author: Xiong Xiaoshuang, master’s student in the Comparative Literature and World Literature program at Jinan University (Guangzhou, China).
- Supervisor: Zhang Shijun, PhD, Professor at Jinan University, an expert in comparative literature and film studies.
- Defense committee chair: Ai Xiaoming, Professor at Sun Yat-sen University, a leading scholar in gender studies and Chinese documentary film research.
## 1.3. Publication Venue
This is a formally awarded master’s degree thesis from the Department of Chinese Language and Literature, Jinan University. In the early 2000s, mainland Chinese academic research on Taiwan cinema was still relatively underdeveloped, so this thesis is an important early work in this sub-field, contributing to the systematic study of Taiwan New Film Movement in mainland academia.
## 1.4. Publication Year
2005, which coincides with the 100th anniversary of Chinese cinema, making the thesis timely in the context of industry-wide reflection on the development path of Chinese cinema.
## 1.5. Abstract
- **Research Objective**: To analyze how Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films, rooted in Chinese realistic film tradition, develop cultural documentary features similar to ethnographic films, while being distinct from pure non-fiction documentaries.
- **Methodology**: The paper uses cultural anthropology (ethnography) as its core theoretical framework, conducts close text analysis of Hou’s representative works, analyzes his ethnographic recording mode and documentary-style film language, and carries out comparative research with Zhang Yimou (mainland 5th generation director) and Ang Lee (international Chinese director).
- **Key Conclusions**: Hou’s films record Taiwan’s more than 100-year pluralistic integrated cultural pattern through a chronological time mode, panoramic space mode, and detailed display of life and folk customs. His innovative use of film language (long takes, fixed camera positions, empty shots) guarantees the authenticity of cultural recording while creating a unique poetic realism style. This cultural documentary feature provides an effective path for developing national cinema and culture in the context of globalization.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien had five key gaps at the time of writing:
1. Over-reliance on Western realism theories (André Bazin’s long take theory, Italian neorealism) when analyzing Hou’s realistic style, ignoring his distinct Chinese national cultural roots.
2. Insufficient attention to the influence of Hou’s fixed core production team (screenwriters, cinematographers) on his film style.
3. Lack of systematic research on the dissemination, reception, and cross-cultural influence of Hou’s films.
4. Most existing studies are appreciative or impressionistic rather than theoretically rigorous, with overly single theoretical frameworks.
5. Comparative studies are limited to directors with similar styles (e.g. Japanese director Ozu Yasujiro, Taiwanese director Edward Yang) and lack comparison with other influential Chinese directors such as mainland 5th generation directors or international Chinese director Ang Lee.

   In addition, in the context of globalization, Chinese cinema faced a core dilemma: how to balance international dissemination and authentic expression of national culture, avoiding either catering to Western exoticist expectations or being closed off from global cultural exchanges. The paper’s innovative entry point is to apply ethnography theory from cultural anthropology to analyze the cultural recording function of Hou’s films, filling the gap in mainland academia.
## 2.2. Main Contributions & Findings
1. **Theoretical Innovation**: It proposes that Hou’s films have a unique cultural documentary feature that bridges narrative feature films and non-fiction documentaries, rooted in Chinese realistic film tradition rather than purely Western realism.
2. **Ethnographic Mode Construction**: It systematically summarizes the three-dimensional ethnographic recording mode of Hou’s films:
   - Time dimension: Chronological recording covering more than 100 years of Taiwanese history, merging personal experience and grand historical narrative from the perspective of ordinary people.
   - Space dimension: Panoramic recording covering all regions of Taiwan, including both traditional rural spaces and modern urban spaces.
   - Detail dimension: Objective recording of daily life details and folk customs, presenting Taiwan’s pluralistic integrated cultural pattern (integrating indigenous, Hakka, mainland immigrant, Japanese colonial, and modern Western cultures).
3. **Style Analysis**: It analyzes how Hou’s documentary-style production techniques (real location shooting, synchronous sound, non-professional actors, innovative use of long takes and empty shots) guarantee the authenticity of cultural recording while forming a unique poetic realism style with Chinese traditional aesthetic characteristics.
4. **Comparative Research**: Through comparison with Zhang Yimou and Ang Lee, it points out that Hou’s cultural documentary feature avoids the exotic national allegory tendency of Zhang’s films and the cross-cultural compromise orientation of Ang Lee’s films, providing a localized, authentic path for the development of Chinese national cinema.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
For beginner readers, the core specialized concepts involved in this paper are explained below:
1. **Cultural Documentary Feature of Film**: Refers to the characteristic of narrative feature films that use documentary-style production techniques to record social history, national culture, and folk customs. Such works have archival and literature value similar to non-fiction documentaries, while retaining the artistic expression of feature films.
2. **Ethnography / Visual Ethnography**: Ethnography is the core research method of cultural anthropology, which records the culture of a community or ethnic group through long-term fieldwork and detailed, objective description. *Visual ethnography* (also called ethnographic film) uses film and video as the recording medium, with unique advantages of intuitiveness and concreteness in recording material culture, production methods, and folk rituals.
3. **Poetic Realism**: A film style that combines the authenticity of realism with the aesthetic expression of poetry. It avoids both the rigid duplication of pure documentaries and the overly dramatic fictionalization of commercial feature films, focusing on creating implicit artistic conception and expressing subtle emotions.
4. **Taiwan New Film Movement**: A film movement that emerged in Taiwan in the early 1980s, represented by Hou Hsiao-hsien and Edward Yang. It is characterized by realistic themes, innovative film language, and focus on Taiwan’s local history, social reality, and personal growth experience, marking the modernization of Taiwan cinema.
5. **Long Take Theory (Bazin)**: Proposed by French film theorist André Bazin, a *long take* refers to a shot that lasts for a relatively long time (usually more than 30 seconds) without editing. It retains the integrity of time and space, presents reality objectively, and avoids the subjective intervention of montage editing.
## 3.2. Previous Works
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien is divided into three main categories:
1. **Thematic Studies**: Focus on the themes of Hou’s films, such as historical narrative, national identity, and social context, using theories like Marxism, new historicism, and postcolonialism. Representative works include Ye Yueyu’s research on female characters in *A City of Sadness* and Chen Ruxiu’s analysis of *Good Men, Good Women*.
2. **Stylistic Studies**: Analyze Hou’s film language (long takes, editing, sound, image) and aesthetic style. Representative works include Meng Hongfeng’s *On Hou Hsiao-hsien’s Style* and Yuan Yuqin’s research on his long take and artistic conception creation that combines Chinese classical art and Western culture.
3. **Comparative Studies**: Compare Hou with directors of similar aesthetic styles. Representative works include Guo Xiaolu’s comparison between Ozu Yasujiro and Hou Hsiao-hsien, and Lin Wenqi’s comparison between *Farewell My Concubine* and *The Puppetmaster*.

   Key theoretical foundations cited by the paper:
- John C. Wakefield’s 1973 paper *Feature Films as Cultural Records*, which first proposed that narrative feature films can be used as valid cultural research materials, laying the theoretical foundation for this paper.
- Fei Xiaotong’s *Pluralistic Integrated Pattern of the Chinese Nation* theory, which is used to analyze Taiwan’s pluralistic cultural structure in the paper.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
In the field of ethnographic film: Early 20th century ethnographic films focused on simple recording of indigenous non-mainstream cultures; mid-20th century works emphasized objectivity and avoidance of subjective intervention; by the late 20th century, academia began to recognize that narrative feature films also have important cultural documentary value.
In Chinese cinema: The realistic tradition runs through from early works like *Spring Silkworms* (1933) and *Street Angel* (1937), to Taiwan’s *Healthy Realism* in the 1960s, Taiwan New Film in the 1980s, and mainland 5th generation films in the 1980s-1990s.
This paper’s work is the first in mainland academia to systematically apply ethnography theory to the study of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films, connecting the two fields of cultural anthropology and Chinese film studies.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior studies, this paper’s core innovations are:
1. It breaks through the limitation of using only Western realism theory, demonstrating that Hou’s realism is rooted in Chinese film tradition and Chinese local literature (e.g. Shen Congwen’s autobiographical works).
2. It uses ethnography as a new theoretical framework, systematically analyzing the cultural recording function of Hou’s films from time, space, and detail dimensions.
3. It expands the scope of comparative studies, comparing Hou with Zhang Yimou and Ang Lee to explore different paths for Chinese cinema to express national culture in the context of globalization.

# 4. Methodology
This is a humanities research paper combining theoretical analysis, close text analysis, and comparative research, with no mathematical formulas involved.
## 4.1. Principles
The core research principle is to integrate cultural anthropology (ethnography) theory and film studies theory, conduct in-depth text analysis of Hou’s representative works, and use comparative research to highlight the uniqueness of his cultural documentary feature. The theoretical basis includes Bazin’s long take theory, visual ethnography theory, Fei Xiaotong’s pluralistic integrated pattern of the Chinese nation, and Chinese traditional poetic aesthetics.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The research is carried out in four sequential layers:
### Layer 1: Classification and sorting of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s works
The paper divides Hou’s directorial works into four stages based on their style and theme:
1. **Commercial Film Period (1980-1982)**: Works include *Cute Girls* (1980), *Play While You Play* (1981), *The Green, Green Grass of Home* (1982). This is the germination period of his realistic style, with preliminary display of humanistic care and attention to Taiwan’s social reality.
2. **Growth Film Period (1983-1987)**: Works include *All the Youthful Days* (1983), *A Summer at Grandpa's* (1984), *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* (1985), *Dust in the Wind* (1986), *Daughter of the Nile* (1987). These works focus on personal growth experience, combining personal memory with Taiwan’s social and historical changes.
3. **Taiwan Trilogy Period (1989-1995)**: Works include *A City of Sadness* (1989), *The Puppetmaster* (1993), *Good Men, Good Women* (1995). These works focus on Taiwan’s modern history, with a mature artistic style and clear intention to record Taiwan’s collective history.
4. **Style Transformation Period (1996-2005)**: Works include *Goodbye South, Goodbye* (1996), *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998), *Millennium Mambo* (2000), *Coffee Time* (2004), *The Best of Times* (2005). These works turn to urban themes while retaining Hou’s core realistic and poetic style.
   The paper’s main research objects are the growth films and Taiwan Trilogy, with supplementary analysis of later works.

### Layer 2: Analysis of Hou’s ethnographic recording mode
The paper analyzes how Hou’s films construct a complete ethnographic record of Taiwan’s culture from three dimensions:
1. **Time dimension**: All representative works have clear, specific historical time markers. Hou merges personal life experience with grand historical events: e.g. *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* is based on Hou’s own childhood experience, *The Puppetmaster* is based on the life of Taiwanese glove puppetry master Li Tianlu. Taken as a whole, his works form a chronological record of Taiwan’s history from 1895 (Treaty of Shimonoseki, Taiwan ceded to Japan) to the early 21st century, covering the Japanese colonial period, retrocession of Taiwan, 228 Incident, White Terror period, economic take-off, and urbanization.
2. **Space dimension**: Hou’s works cover all regions of Taiwan, including Penghu Islands, Kaohsiung, Taipei, Miaoli, Jiufen, Tainan, etc. They present both traditional rural spaces (in early works) and modern urban spaces (in later works), forming a panoramic record of Taiwan’s geographical and social space.
3. **Detail dimension**: Hou’s films record a large number of daily life details and folk customs, including: the parallel use of multiple dialects (Hakka, Minnan, Mandarin, Japanese); life rituals (funerals, sacrifices, festivals); traditional folk art (glove puppetry, folk songs); and era-specific daily objects (stamps, simple baseball equipment, record players, etc.). These details objectively present Taiwan’s pluralistic cultural characteristics.

### Layer 3: Analysis of documentary-style production techniques
The paper analyzes how Hou’s production techniques guarantee the authenticity of cultural recording while forming his unique poetic style:
1. **Scene selection and framing**: Hou insists on real location shooting and uses natural light as much as possible: e.g. *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* was shot in Hou’s actual hometown, *A Summer at Grandpa's* was shot in screenwriter Zhu Tianwen’s grandfather’s actual home. He also uses irregular frames, such as "window composition" (shooting characters through doors and windows), to create a sense of objectivity and observational distance.
2. **Sound design**: He uses synchronous sound to ensure the authenticity of sound effects. He flexibly uses voiceover (narration, monologue) to explain background information and express characters’ inner feelings: e.g. *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* uses Hou’s own voiceover to narrate his childhood memories. He uses era-specific music and arranges for characters to sing in the film, avoiding excessive subjective soundtrack interference.
3. **Character setting**: Hou prefers to use non-professional actors: e.g. Li Tianlu (glove puppetry master) plays himself in *The Puppetmaster*, and Xin Shufen had no professional acting experience before appearing in Hou’s films. He encourages improvisation during shooting, and adjusts the script according to the actual performance effect: e.g. the role of Grandpa in *Dust in the Wind* was added many extra lines because of Li Tianlu’s wonderful natural performance.
4. **Use of long takes, fixed camera positions and empty shots**: Long takes retain the integrity of time and space: e.g. *The Puppetmaster* only has about 100 shots in total, *Flowers of Shanghai* only has more than 40 shots. Fixed camera positions avoid subjective director intervention, presenting reality objectively. *Empty shots* (shots without characters, showing scenery or environment) are similar to the "blank space" technique in traditional Chinese painting, creating implicit poetic artistic conception: e.g. the final empty shot of mountains and fields in *Dust in the Wind* expresses the protagonist’s peaceful state of mind after growing up without explicit lines.

### Layer 4: Comparative analysis with other Chinese directors
The paper compares Hou with two other internationally influential Chinese directors to highlight the uniqueness of his cultural documentary feature:
1. **Comparison with Zhang Yimou**: Hou’s films are based on real history and personal experience, focus on current and future Taiwanese society, use Eastern-style observational narration to present culture through small daily details, and record culture authentically. Zhang’s films are mostly adapted from literary works, focus on fictional ancient Chinese settings, use Western-style narrative and symbolic techniques, and construct exotic national allegories that cater to Western aesthetic expectations.
2. **Comparison with Ang Lee**: Hou’s films are completely localized, produced by Taiwan local teams, and focus on the panoramic display of Taiwan’s social history and culture. Ang Lee’s films are international co-productions, focus on cross-cultural conflicts and universal family ethics, and integrate Chinese and Western cultural elements to cater to global mainstream audiences.

The paper uses two core evaluation dimensions:
1. **Cultural Recording Authenticity**: Evaluates whether the film accurately records the social history, folk customs, and cultural characteristics of the target period and region, with archival value. This dimension draws on the evaluation standards of ethnographic films (authenticity, comprehensiveness, objectivity).
2. **Artistic Expression**: Evaluates whether the film has unique aesthetic value and artistic style, drawing on film aesthetics standards, especially the aesthetic characteristics of Chinese traditional poetic art (artistic conception, implicit expression, harmony between man and nature).

2. **Other representative Chinese directors (Zhang Yimou, Ang Lee)**: This baseline highlights the uniqueness of Hou’s cultural documentary feature in the landscape of Chinese cinema.

# 6. Research Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The paper’s core findings are as follows:
1. **Cultural root of Hou’s style**: Hou’s film’s cultural documentary feature is rooted in Chinese realistic film tradition, not just influenced by Western realism. His style is deeply influenced by Chinese local literature (Shen Congwen’s autobiographical works, Hu Lancheng’s aesthetic thought) and Taiwan’s local realistic film tradition, with distinct Chinese national characteristics.
2. **Ethnographic record value**: Hou’s films construct a complete ethnographic record of Taiwan’s pluralistic integrated cultural pattern:
   - His time dimension avoids the grand narrative of official history, presenting major historical events from the perspective of ordinary people, providing a supplementary, bottom-up historical record.
   - His space dimension objectively reflects the social changes brought by Taiwan’s modernization, recording both the disappearing traditional rural culture and the emerging modern urban culture.
   - His detailed recording of folk customs and daily life has important archival reference value: e.g. the 30-minute display of glove puppetry art in *The Puppetmaster* is a precious visual record of this intangible cultural heritage.
3. **Poetic realism style**: Hou’s documentary-style techniques combine authenticity and aesthetic charm: the combination of long takes, fixed camera positions, empty shots, natural light, synchronous sound, and non-professional actors makes his films have both authenticity close to documentaries and the aesthetic charm of traditional Chinese poetry, avoiding the rigidity of pure ethnographic documentaries and the excessive fiction of commercial feature films.
4. **Reference value for Chinese cinema**: Hou’s cultural documentary feature provides a unique path for Chinese national cinema: unlike Zhang Yimou’s exotic national allegories and Ang Lee’s cross-cultural compromise works, Hou’s films adhere to localized expression, authentically recording national culture, which can help Chinese cinema maintain cultural independence while participating in global cultural exchanges. This is validated by Hou’s international recognition: his works have won many top international film awards (Venice Film Festival Golden Lion, Cannes Film Festival Jury Prize, etc.) without catering to Western exotic tastes.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper systematically analyzes the cultural documentary feature of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s films from the perspective of ethnography, and draws the following core conclusions:
1. Hou’s films are rooted in Chinese realistic tradition, with a unique cultural documentary function that bridges narrative feature films and non-fiction documentaries.
2. His works construct an ethnographic record of Taiwan’s more than 100-year pluralistic integrated cultural pattern from time, space, and detail dimensions, with important historical and archival value.
3. His innovative use of documentary-style film techniques forms a unique poetic realism style, combining the authenticity of cultural recording with the aesthetic charm of Chinese traditional art.
4. Compared with other representative Chinese directors, Hou’s localized, authentic cultural recording path provides a valuable reference for the development of Chinese national cinema in the context of globalization, helping Chinese cinema balance international dissemination and cultural independence.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The paper implicitly points out its own limitations, and suggests future research directions:
1. **Limitations of the current study**:
   - The research focuses on the cultural recording of Hou’s Taiwanese local theme works, and does not conduct in-depth analysis of his non-Taiwanese theme works (*Flowers of Shanghai*, *Coffee Time*).
   - The comparative analysis only selects Zhang Yimou and Ang Lee, and does not cover other important Chinese directors (e.g. Jia Zhangke, Wong Kar-wai).
   - The research on the global dissemination and cross-cultural reception of Hou’s films is not sufficient.
2. **Future research directions**:
   - In-depth analysis of Hou’s later works and his influence on younger generations of Chinese directors.
   - Research on the global reception and cross-cultural interpretation of Hou’s films.
   - Applying the analytical framework of cultural documentary feature to other Chinese realistic film creators and new media forms (e.g. realistic online dramas, documentary-style short videos).
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper is a pioneering work in mainland China’s research on Hou Hsiao-hsien, especially its application of ethnography theory provides a new research perspective for Chinese film studies. Its core contribution is to break through the previous research paradigm that only analyzes Hou’s works from the perspective of film style, and reveals the historical and cultural value of his films as a "living fossil" of Taiwanese culture.

However, there are areas that can be improved:
1. The paper does not fully explore the influence of Hou’s core production team (screenwriter Zhu Tianwen, cinematographer Chen Kunhou) on the formation of his cultural documentary feature, which is one of the research gaps pointed out in the paper’s introduction but not fully addressed.
2. The paper does not discuss the limitations of Hou’s cultural recording: his perspective is still mainly that of the male mainland immigrant group, and the presentation of indigenous culture and female perspective is relatively insufficient.
3. The paper’s conclusion still has important practical significance for current Chinese cinema: many current Chinese films excessively cater to the global market or traffic, losing the authenticity of cultural expression. Hou’s path of adhering to localized authentic recording provides an important reference for Chinese cinema to balance commercial value, artistic value, and cultural value. In addition, the analytical framework of cultural documentary feature can also be extended to the study of new media forms in the current digital age, which have important cultural recording functions for contemporary Chinese society.
