# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The paper's full title is *Re-planting the Existence: on the construction of the daily scene and cultural origins of Hou Hsiao-hsien films* (original Chinese: 《再植存在——论侯孝贤电影的日常场景建构及其精神渊源》). Its central topic is a systematic study of how Taiwanese New Cinema director Hou Hsiao-hsien constructs mundane everyday scenes as the core of his cinematic aesthetic, tracing the historical, literary, and traditional Chinese philosophical roots of this approach, centered on Hou's self-defined concept of "re-planting existence" (the idea that cinematically constructed reality is equivalent to actual real life and can function as an independent, authentic representation of human experience).

## 1.5. Abstract
The paper argues that Hou Hsiao-hsien's films intentionally avoid fixed, complete narrative structures, requiring audiences to co-construct the story themselves, aligning with Jean-Luc Godard's idea that the most vivid cinematic experience occurs between the screen and the viewer. Hou's core creative principle is "re-planting existence": the reality constructed in his films is equivalent to real, lived reality, presented through unremarkable everyday scenes that strip away dramatic plot contrivances and Western "dream-making" cinematic tropes to bring film closer to ordinary life.
Technically, his signature long takes and deep-focus cinematography draw on André Bazin's realist film theory, but diverge from Western realism to adopt an Eastern lyrical style rooted in traditional Chinese aesthetics and philosophy, especially the Taoist concept of the *unity of man and nature*. The films also address Taiwan's unique geopolitical context: recurring themes of "loss of father" and "loss of hometown" reflect generations of Taiwanese people's search for individual and national identity, especially amid the "de-Sinicization" political context in Taiwan. Hou prioritizes depictions of ordinary people's state in time and space over explicit political conflict, creating a distinct aesthetic of the *unity of self and object*.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien (one of the most influential figures in global Chinese-language cinema) had two key gaps:
1. Most studies focused either on technical analysis of his formal stylistic choices (long takes, fixed shots) in isolation, or on political/cultural criticism that read his works primarily through the lens of Taiwanese national identity and colonial history. Few studies explored the deep Eastern cultural and philosophical connotations of his focus on everyday life, which the paper argues is the core of his aesthetic, not just a narrative backdrop.
2. Mainland Chinese research on Hou was particularly late and underdeveloped, consisting mostly of introductory overviews rather than systematic, in-depth analysis.
### Importance of the Problem
Hou is the leading representative of the 1980s Taiwan New Cinema movement, and his work has had a global impact on realist cinematic aesthetics. Understanding the cultural roots of his style helps clarify the unique value of Eastern cinematic traditions that are distinct from dominant Western film theory frameworks.
### Innovative Entry Point
The paper takes Hou's self-defined concept of "re-planting existence" as its core entry point, integrating formal film textual analysis and cultural analysis to connect his construction of everyday scenes to their roots in Taiwan's historical experience, Chinese literary traditions, and traditional Chinese philosophy.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
The paper makes three key contributions to Hou studies:
1. **Formal System Deconstruction**: It systematically breaks down Hou's construction of everyday scenes into three interconnected dimensions: time (real time, continuous duration), space (painterly space, lived space, moving space), and performance (naturalistic acting with non-professional and professional actors). It connects these technical choices to his philosophical worldview, rather than treating them as isolated stylistic flourishes.
2. **Spiritual Origin Tracing**: It identifies three interconnected layers of spiritual roots for Hou's aesthetic:
   - Historical trauma: The recurring themes of "loss of father" and "loss of hometown" stem from Taiwan's unique geopolitical and colonial history, and are shaped by Shen Congwen's literary perspective of detached, compassionate observation of ordinary life.
   - Poetic spirit: His lyrical, non-narrative style draws on traditional Chinese poetic techniques of *bi* (comparison/metaphor) and *xing* (evocation), using empty shots and emotional montage structured around mood rather than linear plot.
   - Traditional philosophy: His aesthetic integrates Confucian values (the doctrine of the mean, acceptance of fate, emphasis on ordinary daily life as the core of human existence) and Taoist values (unity of man and nature, detachment from life and death).
3. **Intermedial Comparison**: It compares cinematic and literary constructions of everyday life, using Hou's adaptation of *The Flowers of Shanghai* as a case study to argue that successful adaptation prioritizes cinematic medium specificity over strict fidelity to literary text. It also critiques earlier Chinese adaptations (like 1984's *Border Town*) that treat everyday scenes as decorative background rather than core content.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
Below are detailed beginner-friendly explanations of all core technical and cultural terms required to understand the paper:
### Film Studies Terms
- **Long Take**: A continuous, unedited shot that lasts for an extended duration (usually multiple minutes, in contrast to the fast-cut short takes of classical Hollywood cinema). It preserves the unbroken continuity of time and space in a scene, allowing viewers to choose what to focus on rather than being guided by editing, and is a core technique of realist cinema.
- **Depth of Field / Deep Focus**: A cinematographic technique where all elements in the frame (from the closest foreground to the farthest background) are in sharp, clear focus. This allows multiple layers of action to happen simultaneously, giving viewers greater interpretive freedom, and is closely associated with André Bazin's realist film theory.
- **André Bazin's Realist Film Theory**: Bazin was a mid-20th century French film critic and founder of the influential journal *Cahiers du Cinéma*. His core argument was that film's essential nature is to objectively record reality, preserving its inherent ambiguity and continuity. He argued that long takes and deep focus are aesthetically superior to montage (editing), which manipulates reality by cutting to force a specific interpretation on the viewer.
- **Taiwan New Cinema**: A film movement that emerged in Taiwan in the early 1980s, led by Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, and others. It rejected the previous dominant modes of commercial melodrama and KMT propaganda film, instead focusing on realistic depictions of Taiwanese local life, personal memory, and historical trauma, using innovative techniques like long takes, location shooting, and non-professional actors.
- **Montage**: A film technique of selecting, editing, and piecing together separate shots to create a meaning that does not exist in any individual shot. Classical Hollywood uses continuity montage to create a seamless linear narrative, while Soviet montage uses shot juxtaposition to create ideological meaning. The paper discusses *emotional montage*, Hou's approach of editing based on the flow of mood and emotion rather than linear plot or logical cause and effect.
- **Empty Shot**: A shot with no human characters present, often used for scene transitions or to convey mood. In Hou's films, empty shots function similarly to traditional Chinese poetic techniques, evoking emotion through natural scenery rather than explicit dialogue or acting.
### Cultural Terms
- <strong>Bi (比) and Xing (兴)</strong>: Core rhetorical techniques in traditional Chinese poetry. *Bi* (comparison) uses a concrete object to metaphorize an abstract emotion or idea. *Xing* (evocation) opens a poem with a description of a seemingly unrelated natural scene to set the mood, introduce the main theme, and create emotional resonance, rather than directly stating the core idea.
- <strong>Doctrine of the Mean (Zhong Yong, 中庸)</strong>: A core concept of Confucian philosophy, referring to the ideal of emotional moderation, avoiding extreme expression of joy or sorrow, and acting in appropriately measured, balanced ways in all situations. It also emphasizes the value of ordinary, everyday life as the foundation of human existence.
- <strong>Unity of Man and Nature (Tian Ren He Yi, 天人合一)</strong>: A core concept of Taoist philosophy, holding that human beings are an integral part of the natural world, not separate from or superior to it. It emphasizes harmony between human life and the natural order, and detachment from worldly concerns like personal gain and loss.
## 3.2. Previous Works
### Global Realist Film Traditions
- **Lumière Brothers Early Actualities (1890s)**: The earliest films, which recorded unscripted everyday events (workers leaving a factory, a baby eating breakfast) without staging or narrative, establishing film's inherent capacity for recording reality.
- **Italian Neorealism (1940s–1950s)**: Post-WWII film movement that used location shooting, non-professional actors, and depictions of ordinary working-class life to critique post-war poverty and inequality, with works like Vittorio De Sica's *Bicycle Thieves* and *Umberto D.* as core examples.
- **French New Wave (1960s)**: Influenced by Bazin's realist theory, filmmakers like Jean-Luc Godard and François Truffaut used location shooting, long takes, and experimental narrative structures to reject the conventions of mainstream studio cinema.
### Hou Hsiao-hsien Studies
Prior to this paper, most Hou research fell into two camps:
1. Formal analysis that focused on his long takes and cinematographic style in isolation, without connecting these choices to their cultural or philosophical context.
2. Political analysis that read his films (especially 1989's *A City of Sadness*, the first Chinese-language film to win the Venice Golden Lion) primarily as depictions of Taiwanese national identity and colonial trauma, ignoring his broader aesthetic focus on everyday life.
   Few studies explored the roots of his aesthetic in traditional Chinese culture, which is the core gap this paper addresses.
### Chinese Literary Adaptation Studies
Prior Chinese adaptation research often prioritized fidelity to the original literary text over cinematic medium specificity, leading to adaptations that rely heavily on voiceover, narration, and dialogue to convey plot and inner thought, rather than using visual cinematic language.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Realist cinema has evolved globally through distinct regional stages:
1. Early Lumière actualities → 2. Italian Neorealism (socially critical realism) → 3. French New Wave (experimental, personal realism) → 4. East Asian realist traditions (Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu's depictions of domestic life and traditional ethics; Hou Hsiao-hsien's Eastern lyrical realism; mainland Chinese director Jia Zhangke's depictions of postsocialist transformation).
   Each stage builds on the core realist focus on recording everyday life, but adds region-specific cultural and aesthetic characteristics. This paper situates Hou's work as a distinct evolution of realist cinema that moves beyond Western theoretical frameworks to incorporate Eastern philosophical and aesthetic values.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
### Compared to Western Realist Cinema
Western realist traditions often have an explicit social critical purpose (e.g., Italian Neorealism critiques systemic inequality). Hou's realism, by contrast, avoids explicit social or political critique, focusing instead on the broader existential experience of ordinary people in time, with a detached, compassionate tone rooted in Chinese philosophy. His long takes are not just for objective recording, but to create a poetic mood and sense of duration aligned with Eastern perceptions of time.
### Compared to Prior Hou Studies
Most prior studies either treat his formal techniques as stylistic flourishes, or read his films only through the lens of Taiwanese identity politics. This paper instead frames everyday scene construction as the core of his aesthetic, tracing its roots in both historical trauma and traditional Chinese culture to provide a more holistic understanding that integrates form, theme, and cultural background.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The paper uses an integrated methodology that combines formal film textual analysis and cultural analysis, drawing on two complementary analytical traditions:
1. **Bruce Kawin's *How to Read a Film***: A rigorous technical approach to film analysis that focuses on the specific properties of the cinematic medium (shot length, composition, editing, sound) rather than just narrative content.
2. **Donald Richie's *Ozu***: An approach that contextualizes formal cinematic choices within the broader cultural background and value systems of the filmmaker, exploring how cultural symbols shape a film's emotional and thematic tone.
   Its theoretical foundation draws from two core realist film theorists:
- Siegfried Kracauer's *Theory of Film: The Redemption of Physical Reality*, which argues that film's essence is to record concrete material reality, and that everyday life is the most suitable subject for cinematic expression.
- André Bazin's *What is Cinema?*, which argues that film's unique capacity for objective recording makes long takes and deep focus the most authentic cinematic techniques, as they preserve the ambiguity and continuity of real life.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The paper's analysis proceeds in three sequential, interconnected stages:
---
### Step 1: Analysis of Daily Scene Construction (Formal Analysis)
This stage breaks down Hou's system for constructing authentic everyday scenes into three mutually dependent dimensions, with shot-by-shot analysis of key scenes from his filmography:
#### Dimension 1: Time Construction
Hou rejects the compressed, dramatic time of classical Hollywood cinema (which speeds up time to highlight conflict and tension), instead prioritizing two approaches to time:
1. **Real Time**: He preserves the actual duration of mundane everyday actions, so viewers experience events alongside characters without dramatic foreshadowing, just as they would occur in real life.
   *Example from *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* (1985): When the protagonist's father dies during a power outage, the film includes a full minute of completely black screen with only the sound of matches being struck, matching the actual time it would take to find a match and light a candle in a blackout. There is no dramatic score or foreshadowing of the death, making the event feel as sudden and arbitrary as real loss.
2. **Duration (Continuous Time)**: He uses long takes and fixed static shots to create a sense of unbroken, flowing time, rather than cutting to highlight only "important" moments.
   *Example from *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*: A 6-minute long take of the protagonist's mother and older sister chatting casually before the sister's marriage, with no dramatic plot developments. The shot captures the mundane, meandering flow of everyday conversation, with undercurrents of emotion from the mother's memories of her own difficult marriage. This differs from long takes in films like Alfred Hitchcock's *Rope* (1948), which use continuous shooting but still follow a tightly scripted dramatic plot, while Hou's long takes capture the unstructured, uneventful quality of real daily life.
#### Dimension 2: Space Construction
Hou's approach to space integrates Western cinematographic theory and traditional Chinese painting aesthetics, with three distinct types of cinematic space:
1. **Painterly Space**: He combines Western deep-focus cinematography (foreground, midground, background all in sharp focus) with Chinese traditional painting's *scattered perspective* and *three distances (gaoyuan, high distance; shenyuan, deep distance; pingyuan, level distance)* principles, creating frames that extend beyond the boundaries of the screen.
   *Example from *The Boys from Fengkuei* (1983): Fight scenes are often shot with characters running partially or fully off-screen, forcing viewers to imagine the off-screen space, similar to Chinese painting's focus on implying space beyond the frame. Hou rarely uses close-ups, preferring medium and long shots that place characters within vast natural landscapes, as in *Dust in the Wind* (1986), where characters appear small against the backdrop of mountains, reflecting the Taoist idea that human beings are an integral part of the natural order.
2. **Lived Space**: He uses actual, lived-in locations (not stylized studio sets) that shape characters' lives and relationships, rather than functioning as a neutral backdrop for action.
   *Example from *The Boys from Fengkuei*: The courtyard apartment where the young migrant protagonists live has a layout that allows the lead character to peer into his neighbors' lives, reflecting their marginalized, rootless state as recent arrivals to the city. Even in *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998), which uses fully constructed studio sets (since 19th century Shanghai brothels no longer exist), the closed, ornate sets reflect the enclosed, stagnant nature of the brothel world, and each prostitute's room is designed to match her personality: Shen Xiaohong's room uses dark red, heavy furniture to match her decadent, proud personality; Zhou Shuangzhu's room uses simple blue and white porcelain to match her calm, wise demeanor.
3. **Moving Space**: He frequently captures the subjective perspective of characters in motion (on trains, cars, motorcycles) to reflect their rootless, drifting state.
   *Example from *Dust in the Wind*: The opening shot of a train emerging from a tunnel, carrying the young lead couple away from their rural hometown for the city, with the moving landscape reflecting their uncertain future. In *Goodbye South, Goodbye* (1996), shots from inside a car looking out at Taipei's skyscrapers, tinted green by the protagonist's sunglasses, reflect his alienation from modern urban life.
#### Dimension 3: Performance Construction
Hou rejects theatrical, exaggerated acting, prioritizing naturalistic performances that feel indistinguishable from real everyday behavior:
1. **Non-professional Actors**: He often casts non-professional actors whose real life experiences match their characters, giving them no fixed scripted lines, only a scene context, so they improvise dialogue naturally as they would in real life.
   *Example: Li Tianlu, a real-life traditional glove puppeteer, plays the grandfather figure in *Dust in the Wind* and *A City of Sadness* (1989). His performance has no trace of theatrical acting, perfectly matching the natural, understated tone of the films. Non-professional actors are especially suited to Hou's long takes, as they can sustain a natural, continuous performance without the "acting cues" professional actors use for short, edited takes.
2. **Naturalistic Professional Actor Performance**: For professional actors, he asks them to engage in routine everyday actions rather than "perform" emotions, to help them enter a natural, unscripted state. He famously told Tony Leung Chiu-wai to "just read books" and Shu Qi to "move around doing everyday tasks" on set to avoid forced, exaggerated acting. He avoids close-ups, which pressure actors to perform exaggerated facial emotions, using medium and long shots to let actors behave naturally.
   ---
### Step 2: Analysis of Spiritual Origins of Daily Scene Construction (Cultural Analysis)
After deconstructing the formal system, the paper traces its spiritual roots in three interconnected layers:
#### Layer 1: Historical and Literary Roots
1. **Taiwan's Collective Historical Trauma**: Hou's recurring themes of "loss of father" (weak, absent, or deceased father figures in nearly all his films) and "loss of hometown" reflect Taiwan's unique geopolitical experience: 50 years of Japanese colonial rule (1895–1945), followed by the KMT retreat to Taiwan in 1949, decades of cross-strait tension, and ongoing identity uncertainty. For both mainland migrants who lost their hometowns and local Taiwanese who lack a clear national identity, mundane everyday life becomes the only stable anchor amid political upheaval, especially amid the "de-Sinicization" context in Taiwan, where Hou's insistence on Eastern cultural features functions as a form of quiet cultural persistence.
2. **Shen Congwen's Literary Influence**: Hou has repeatedly stated that Shen Congwen's *Autobiography* gave him a new perspective on looking at the world: a detached, compassionate, almost bird's-eye view of life's suffering and joy, without exaggerated emotion or explicit judgment, just presenting life as it is even when depicting violence and death. This is reflected in his understated depiction of tragic events: when the eldest brother of the Lin family is shot dead in *A City of Sadness*, there is no dramatic build-up, no close-up of pain, just a sudden, unemotional depiction of the death, as arbitrary and unexpected as real loss.
#### Layer 2: Poetic Spirit
Hou's films are fundamentally lyrical rather than narrative, drawing heavily on traditional Chinese poetic techniques:
1. **Empty Shots as Bi and Xing**: Empty shots are not just used for transition, but function as poetic *bi* and *xing*.
   *Example from *Dust in the Wind*: After the protagonist finds out his girlfriend has married a postman, he cries in bed, and the film cuts to a 1-minute empty shot of treetops swaying in the wind at sunset, accompanied by a flute score. This is *xing*: the natural scene evokes the character's pain and the passage of time, avoiding the melodrama of focusing on his crying face, while also reflecting the Taoist idea that human suffering is small against the vast, eternal natural world.
2. **Emotional Montage**: Instead of editing based on linear narrative or cause and effect, Hou edits based on the flow of emotion and mood.
   *Example from *A City of Sadness*: The scene where Japanese woman Shizuko gives a gift to the female lead Kuanmei cuts to unmarked flashbacks of Shizuko playing the piano, arranging flowers, and her brother writing calligraphy, with no clear indication of whose memory the flashbacks belong to. This creates a lyrical, dreamlike mood that conveys the collective sense of loss at the end of the Japanese colonial era, rather than just advancing the plot.
#### Layer 3: Confucian and Taoist Philosophical Traditions
Hou's aesthetic is deeply rooted in traditional Chinese philosophy:
1. **Confucian Doctrine of the Mean**: He emphasizes emotional moderation, avoiding extreme expression of joy or sorrow, and frames ordinary everyday life as the core of human existence.
   *Example from *A City of Sadness*: Even after the Lin family suffers multiple deaths and tragedies amid the 228 Incident, the final scene depicts the remaining family members sitting around eating dinner as usual, showing how life continues even after great trauma, with no dramatic outpourings of grief. Confucian *zhi ming* (knowing fate) is also reflected: characters do not fight against fate melodramatically, but accept the parts of life they cannot control while continuing to live their daily lives as best they can.
2. **Taoist Unity of Man and Nature**: Human beings are framed as an integral part of nature, not separate from it, so Hou frequently places characters within vast natural landscapes, using long shots that make characters small against the environment to reflect that human life is part of the larger natural order.
   *Example from *The Boys from Fengkuei*: The scene where the young migrant protagonists play by the sea, with the vast ocean functioning as a space that accepts their youth and confusion, making their personal troubles feel small against the eternal natural world. Taoist detachment from life and death is also reflected in his understated depiction of death as just another part of the natural cycle.
---
### Step 3: Comparison of Cinematic and Literary Daily Scene Construction (Intermedial Analysis)
The final stage compares how film and literature construct everyday scenes, using adaptation as a case study:
#### Core Similarities and Differences Between Film and Novel
- **Similarities**: Both film and the modern novel reject the closed dramatic structure of theater, focusing instead on depicting the endless, open flow of everyday life.
- **Core Differences**: Film is a *material continuity*: it conveys meaning entirely through recording concrete material objects, scenes, and actions. Literature is a *spiritual continuity*: it can directly depict internal thoughts, emotions, and abstract ideas that have no visible material form. Successful adaptation cannot just translate the plot of a novel; it must convert literary content into cinematic material reality, using visual language rather than dialogue or voiceover.
#### Case Study: *The Flowers of Shanghai* Adaptation
Hou's 1998 adaptation of Han Bangqing's late Qing novel *The Flowers of Shanghai* (a Wu-dialect novel about 19th century Shanghai brothels, focused on the mundane daily life of prostitutes and clients) is a model of successful adaptation because it prioritizes cinematic medium specificity:
- He cuts the novel's more melodramatic plotlines (like the rags-to-riches story of Zhao Baoer, the tragic love story of Li Shufang and Tao Yufu), focusing only on the mundane daily scenes of eating, drinking, chatting, and smoking opium in the brothels.
- He uses extremely long takes: the 110-minute film has only 40 shots, with an average of 3 minutes per shot, to capture the slow, stagnant rhythm of brothel life.
- He uses closed studio sets to reflect the enclosed, stagnant nature of the brothel world, and visual details (room design, small actions, subtle facial expressions) to convey character personality and relationships, rather than relying on voiceover or explanatory dialogue.
  The result is a fully cinematic work rather than a "filmed novel", that captures the core mood of the original text while being entirely independent as a film.
#### Critique of Prior Failed Adaptations
The paper criticizes earlier Chinese adaptations like 1984's *Border Town* (adapted from Shen Congwen's novel) and 1982's *My Memories of Old Beijing* (adapted from Lin Haiyin's novel) for treating everyday scenes as mere *scenic decoration* (镶边) rather than core content. These adaptations prioritize the dramatic plot, use extensive voiceover to explain the story and characters' inner thoughts, and beautiful location shots function only as a decorative backdrop that can be removed without affecting the plot, failing to use cinematic visual language to convey the poetic mood of the original novels.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The paper's main analytical results fall into three categories:
### Formal Analysis Results
Hou's construction of everyday scenes is a holistic, integrated system of time, space, and performance techniques, all aligned to his core concept of "re-planting existence": creating a cinematic reality that is equivalent to real life, rather than a dramatic or stylized representation of it. This system diverges from Western realist film theory in its focus on poetic mood and Eastern philosophical perceptions of time and space, rather than just objective recording of reality.
### Cultural Analysis Results
The spiritual origins of Hou's aesthetic are three interconnected layers:
1. **Historical trauma**: The "loss of father" and "loss of hometown" themes are not just personal, but reflect the collective historical experience of generations of Taiwanese people. Everyday life becomes a stable anchor amid political upheaval and identity confusion, especially amid the "de-Sinicization" context in Taiwan, where Hou's insistence on Eastern cultural features becomes a form of quiet cultural persistence.
2. **Literary influence**: Shen Congwen's detached, compassionate perspective on life shapes Hou's narrative tone, avoiding explicit judgment or melodrama, just presenting ordinary people's lives as they are.
3. **Traditional philosophy**: The integration of Confucian emphasis on ordinary daily life and emotional moderation, and Taoist unity of man and nature and detachment from life and death, is the core of his unique Eastern aesthetic, distinguishing him from all Western realist directors.
### Adaptation Analysis Results
Successful adaptation of everyday life-focused literary works requires prioritizing cinematic medium specificity: converting the spiritual continuity of literature into the material continuity of cinema, using visual language to convey the mood and spirit of the original work, rather than just translating the plot. Hou's *Flowers of Shanghai* is a model of this approach, while previous adaptations like *Border Town* fail because they treat everyday scenes as decorative rather than core content, relying on non-cinematic devices like voiceover.
#### Comparison to Baselines
- Compared to Western realist films like *Umberto D.*: Hou's films have a stronger lyrical, poetic quality, and avoid explicit social critique, focusing instead on the broader existential experience of ordinary people in time, rooted in Eastern philosophy.
- Compared to Ozu's films: Ozu's works are focused on Japanese family ethics and social structure, with very rigid, symmetrical compositions. Hou's films are more focused on personal memory and historical trauma, with more flexible, open compositions that draw on Chinese painting aesthetics.
- Compared to prior Chinese adaptations: *Flowers of Shanghai* avoids the common flaw of over-reliance on literary devices, fully using cinematic visual language to create atmosphere and convey meaning, making it an independent cinematic work rather than a filmed novel.

## 6.3. Component Analysis
The paper effectively conducts a "component analysis" of Hou's aesthetic system, finding that all three dimensions of daily scene construction are mutually dependent and essential to the overall effect of "re-planting existence":
- If the time component is removed (using compressed dramatic time instead of real time and duration), the sense of authenticity and immersion is lost, as viewers no longer experience scenes alongside characters.
- If the space component is removed (using stylized sets instead of lived-in, authentic spaces), the connection between characters and their environment is lost, and themes of rootlessness and belonging cannot be conveyed.
- If the performance component is removed (using theatrical, exaggerated acting instead of naturalistic performance), the sense of everyday reality is broken, as characters feel like actors performing rather than real people.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper provides a holistic, systematic analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien's cinematic aesthetic centered on the concept of "re-planting existence":
- Formally, Hou constructs everyday scenes through an integrated system of time (real time, continuous duration), space (painterly, lived, moving space), and performance (naturalistic acting), creating a cinematic reality that is equivalent to real life, distinct from both dramatic Hollywood cinema and Western realist traditions.
- Spiritually, this aesthetic is rooted in three interconnected layers: Taiwan's unique historical trauma of "loss of father" and "loss of hometown", the literary influence of Shen Congwen's detached compassionate perspective on life, and traditional Chinese Confucian and Taoist philosophy, especially the emphasis on ordinary daily life and the unity of man and nature.
- In terms of adaptation, his *Flowers of Shanghai* provides a model for successful adaptation of everyday life-focused literary works, prioritizing cinematic medium specificity and converting literary spiritual continuity into cinematic material continuity, avoiding the common flaw of treating everyday scenes as mere decorative backdrops.
  The paper fills a key gap in prior Hou studies, which had focused only on technical analysis or political interpretation, and provides a deeper understanding of the unique Eastern cultural and philosophical roots of his aesthetic, which has made him one of the most important figures in global cinema.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations
The paper has three key limitations, implicitly acknowledged by its focus on Hou's pre-2000 works:
1. **Limited analysis of later works**: It focuses mostly on Hou's films from 1983 to 1998, with less analysis of his 2000s transnational works set in Japan (*Café Lumière*) and France (*Flight of the Red Balloon*), so the analysis of the evolution of his aesthetic in his later career is less comprehensive.
2. **Lack of critical engagement with political debates**: It focuses mostly on the positive value of Hou's understated aesthetic, with less engagement with critiques from some scholars that his depictions of political violence and historical trauma depoliticize Taiwan's history, reducing structural political struggle to individual existential experience.
3. **Narrow adaptation analysis**: It only analyzes *Flowers of Shanghai*, so its conclusions about adaptation are less generalizable to other literary adaptations.
### Future Research Directions
1.  Analyze Hou's later transnational works, exploring how his everyday scene aesthetic adapts to different cultural contexts, and whether Chinese cultural roots remain core to these works.
2.  Conduct comparative research between Hou and other Chinese-language realist directors (Jia Zhangke in mainland China, Ann Hui in Hong Kong), exploring different expressions of everyday life aesthetics in different Chinese regional contexts.
3.  Research the influence of Hou's aesthetic on younger generations of Chinese-language filmmakers, and how his approach to everyday scene construction is being adapted in contemporary digital cinema, where shooting technology has changed dramatically.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper is a highly valuable, comprehensive study of Hou Hsiao-hsien's work, especially its integration of formal and cultural analysis, which avoids the common flaws of either purely technical formal analysis that ignores cultural context, or purely political analysis that ignores cinematic medium specificity.
Its most important contribution is that it decenters Western film theory as the only valid framework for analyzing non-Western cinema, showing that Hou's aesthetic is not just a "local variation" of Western realism, but a distinct, independent aesthetic system rooted in Chinese traditional culture and Taiwan's specific historical context, with universal value for global cinema that expands the possibilities of realist film aesthetics.
Potential areas for improvement include more analysis of the role of sound (music, ambient sound, dialogue) in Hou's scene construction, as the paper focuses mostly on visual elements, while sound is also a core component of creating a sense of everyday reality in his films.
The paper's insights are also applicable beyond film studies: its analysis of everyday life as a site of resistance and identity formation amid historical upheaval and cultural conflict is relevant to cultural studies and sociology, especially in contexts of identity uncertainty and cultural globalization, where ordinary everyday life often becomes the core place where people construct their sense of belonging and cultural identity.
