# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The full title is *Emotion, Scene, Object: The Literariness Expression of Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films*. Its core topic is interpreting the unique literary and aesthetic characteristics of Taiwanese director Hou Hsiao-hsien's film works from the perspective of Chinese classical literary theory, using three core traditional aesthetic categories: emotion (qing), scene (jing), and object (wu) as the analytical anchor.
## 1.2. Authors
The author is Lou Jinlin, a master's student in Drama and Film Studies, supervised by Professor Pei Yali, a scholar in the field of film and literary studies. This is an academic master's thesis completed for degree requirements.
## 1.3. Publication Venue
This work is a publicly available master's degree thesis in the discipline of Drama and Film Studies, submitted to the author's university for degree conferral in June 2022. It is a representative piece of research on Hou Hsiao-hsien in Chinese-language film studies.
## 1.4. Publication Year
2022
## 1.5. Abstract
This paper takes Hou Hsiao-hsien's film works as the research object, breaks away from the dominant Western film theoretical framework in prior Hou studies, and anchors its analysis in Chinese classical literary theory. It first traces the intersection of Hou's creative practice with literary trends (Taiwan Nativist Literature Movement, long-term collaboration with writer Chu Tien-wen) to confirm his conscious pursuit of literary quality in film creation. It then systematically analyzes the expression of literariness in Hou's films through three interlinked dimensions: emotion, scene, and object, linking each dimension to traditional Chinese aesthetic concepts (lyrical tradition, artistic conception theory, Wang Guowei's "state" theory) and related East Asian aesthetic thought (mono no aware). The core conclusion is that the lyrical quality rooted in Chinese literary tradition, rather than formal techniques such as long shots, is the core source of Hou's unique cinematic style and global influence, and the interplay of emotion, scene, and object constitutes the specific expression path of his film's literariness.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
For a long time, research on Hou Hsiao-hsien, one of the most representative Eastern film masters, has mostly relied on Western film theoretical frameworks (such as auteur theory, realism theory, formalist film theory). Prior studies mostly focus on his formal features (long shots, fixed shots, non-linear narrative, weak dramatic conflict) and often reduce his well-recognized "lyrical" or "poetic" quality to a by-product of these formal techniques, ignoring the deep connection between his aesthetic practice and Chinese native literary and aesthetic traditions. This interpretive paradigm cannot fully explain the unique Eastern cultural charm of Hou's films.
### Importance of the Problem
As Chinese-language films gain increasing global influence, building a local interpretive system based on Chinese native aesthetic theory to replace the one-sided application of Western frameworks is an urgent need in Chinese film studies, to avoid erasing the cultural specificity of local film works.
### Research Gap
Few prior studies systematically interpret Hou's film literariness from the perspective of Chinese classical literary theory, especially using the three core traditional aesthetic categories of emotion, scene, and object, which run through all Chinese traditional art forms (poetry, painting, calligraphy, opera).
### Innovative Entry Point
This paper takes the three core categories of emotion, scene, and object in Chinese classical literary aesthetics as the entry point, combines close textual analysis of Hou's films and historical contextualization of Taiwan's social and cultural environment, to explain the literariness of his films as an inheritance of the Chinese lyrical tradition, rather than just a formal innovation.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1.  **Contextual Contribution**: It systematically sorts out the internal and external literary influences on Hou's creation: the external influence of the Taiwan Nativist Literature Movement and Taiwan New Cinema Movement, and the internal influence of his 30-year collaboration with writer Chu Tien-wen, proving that Hou's pursuit of literary quality is conscious, not accidental.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution**: It constructs an analytical framework of "emotion-scene-object" based on Chinese classical literary theory, which effectively explains the unique lyrical quality of Hou's films, fills the gap of local theoretical interpretation of Hou's works, and proves that Chinese classical aesthetic theory has strong explanatory power for Chinese-language films.
3.  **Conclusion Contribution**: It refutes the mainstream view that Hou's lyrical quality is only derived from formal techniques, and points out that formal techniques such as long shots are only carriers to realize the interplay of emotion, scene, and object, and the core of his style is the literariness rooted in Chinese lyrical tradition, which is the fundamental reason why his films have unique global influence.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core concepts are explained for readers with no prior background in film studies or Chinese literary theory:
1.  **Literariness**: The concept was first proposed by Russian formalist scholar Roman Jakobson, referring to the unique attribute that makes a work a literary work, different from ordinary language or non-literary texts. In the context of this paper, literariness specifically refers to the aesthetic traits of Hou's films that align with the Chinese lyrical tradition, expressed through the interplay of emotion, scene, and object, focusing on implicit emotion expression and artistic conception construction.
2.  **Chinese Lyrical Tradition**: A core theoretical proposition proposed by Chinese-American literary scholar Chen Shih-hsiang, pointing out that unlike the Western literary tradition which is centered on epic and drama, the Chinese literary tradition as a whole is a lyrical tradition, with emotion expression as the core, running through all literary genres from the *Book of Songs* (11th-6th century BCE) to modern literary works.
3.  **Taiwan Nativist Literature**: A literary movement that emerged in Taiwan in the 1970s, which advocates that literary works should focus on the real life of local Taiwanese people, reflect the suffering and demands of ordinary people, oppose excessive Westernization of literature, and have obvious anti-colonial and local awareness characteristics. It became the mainstream of Taiwanese literature after the 1977 Nativist Literature Debate.
4.  **"Selfless State" (wú wǒ zhī jìng) and "With Self State" (yǒu wǒ zhī jìng)**: Two core concepts of artistic conception from Chinese scholar Wang Guowei's *Poetry Remarks in the Human World* (1908):
    - *With Self State*: The creator observes objects from the perspective of the self, so all objects in the work are colored by the creator's subjective emotion.
    - *Selfless State*: The creator observes objects in a relatively objective way, integrating the self into the object, so the boundary between the self and the object is blurred, and emotion is implicitly hidden in the description of the object.
5.  **Mono no Aware (wù āi)**: A core Japanese aesthetic concept first systematically defined by Edo-period scholar Motoori Norinaga, referring to the gentle, tolerant emotional response to the impermanence of all things, including three layers: emotional movement by people (especially love and family affection), emotional movement by social phenomena (suffering and change in worldly life), and emotional movement by natural objects. Due to Taiwan's 50-year history of Japanese colonial rule (1895-1945), this aesthetic thought has a certain influence on Taiwanese cultural creation.
## 3.2. Previous Works
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien can be divided into four categories:
1.  **Formal Studies**: The largest category, focusing on the formal features of Hou's films, including his non-linear narrative, extensive use of long shots, fixed shots and empty shots, and realistic style. Representative studies include Meng Hongfeng's *On Hou Hsiao-hsien's Style* (1993) and Li Xiang's research on his authorial traits via formal techniques.
2.  **Content Studies**: Focusing on the themes of Hou's films, including growth, nostalgia, historical reflection, and female image construction. Representative studies include Sun Weichuan's research on his "Sadness Trilogy" (focusing on historical reflection on Taiwan's modern history) and studies on the evolution of female images in his works.
3.  **Comparative Studies**: Comparing Hou with other directors, including Chinese director Jia Zhangke (difference in the use of long shots), Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu (difference in the expression of Eastern aesthetics), and Taiwanese director Edward Yang (difference in narrative style and attitude towards modernity).
4.  **Literariness Studies**: A small number of studies mention the "poetic quality" or "lyrical trait" of Hou's films, but they almost always reduce this trait to the effect of long shot techniques, and do not systematically link it to the Chinese classical literary tradition, nor use a systematic local theoretical framework for interpretation.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
In recent years, Chinese film studies have gradually shifted from one-sided application of Western theoretical systems to exploring local interpretive frameworks based on Chinese classical aesthetic theory, to better capture the cultural specificity of Chinese-language films. This thesis is a representative practice of this research trend, focusing on the most recognized Eastern film master Hou Hsiao-hsien as the research object to test the effectiveness of local aesthetic theory in interpreting Chinese-language films.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior research, the core innovations of this paper are:
1.  **Framework Innovation**: It uses the three core categories of emotion, scene, and object in Chinese classical literary aesthetics as the analytical framework, which is completely different from prior Western-theory-based frameworks, and can capture the unique cultural connotations of Hou's films that cannot be explained by Western theories.
2.  **Viewpoint Innovation**: It refutes the mainstream view that Hou's lyrical quality is derived from formal techniques, and points out that formal techniques are only carriers serving the expression of literariness rooted in the Chinese lyrical tradition.
3.  **Context Innovation**: It links Hou's aesthetic practice to both Chinese literary tradition and Taiwan's special post-colonial cultural context, explaining the integration of Chinese lyrical tradition and mono no aware aesthetic in his works, which has not been systematically discussed in prior studies.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The core principle of this paper is to break away from the over-reliance on Western film theory, anchor the analysis in Chinese classical literary and aesthetic theory, use the three interlinked categories of emotion, scene, and object as the analytical framework, combine close textual analysis of Hou's representative films, historical contextualization of Taiwan's social and cultural environment, to trace the origin and concrete expression of the lyrical quality (core of literariness) of his films.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research is carried out in four sequential steps:
### Step 1: Contextualize the intersection of Hou's creation with literary traditions
This step proves that Hou's pursuit of literary quality is conscious, from both external and internal dimensions:
1.  **External Influence Dimension**: Place Hou's creation in the social and cultural context of Taiwan in the 1980s:
    - Political: Taiwan gradually lifted martial law in the 1980s, relaxing control over cultural creation, providing a loose environment for the Taiwan New Cinema Movement.
    - Economic: Taiwan's rapid industrialization in the 1970s-1980s led to drastic social changes, providing rich realistic themes for film creation.
    - Cultural: After the 1977 Nativist Literature Debate, Nativist Literature became the mainstream of Taiwanese literature, advocating focusing on the real life of local people and reflecting local experience. The Taiwan New Cinema Movement, in which Hou was a core leader, directly inherited the spiritual core of Nativist Literature, and many of Hou's early works were adapted from Nativist literary works (e.g., *The Sandwich Man* adapted from Huang Chunming's novel of the same name).
2.  **Internal Influence Dimension**: Analyze the 30-year creative collaboration between Hou and writer Chu Tien-wen:
    - Chu Tien-wen, a representative Taiwanese writer, introduced Hou to the works of Chinese writer Shen Congwen, whose "god's eye view" (observing life and suffering from a distant, non-interventional perspective without excessive emotional venting) became a core creative concept of Hou.
    - Chu's female perspective and lyrical writing style deeply shaped the expression of emotion in Hou's films, especially the shift from male-centered narrative in early works to female-centered narrative in later works.
### Step 2: Analyze "Emotion" as the core of literariness expression
Link to Chinese classical literary theory: The core of the Chinese lyrical tradition is emotion expression, as stated in the *Mao Preface to the Book of Songs* (1st century BCE): "Emotion is stirred inside and takes form in words", and Lu Ji (261-303 CE) proposed "Poetry originates from emotion", clarifying that emotion is the core of literary creation.
Hou's expressed emotions are divided into three closely linked categories:
1.  **Father-son Emotion**: The recurring image pattern of "incomplete father" and "runaway son" in his early works:
    - "Incomplete father": Fathers in Hou's films are almost always physically disabled or emotionally absent: the father in *The Boys from Fengkuei* (1983) is hit by a baseball and loses the ability to speak and take care of the family; the father in *Dust in the Wind* (1986) has a leg injury and cannot work; the father in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* (1985) suffers from asthma, rarely communicates with his children, and always sits quietly on a bamboo chair. This pattern is rooted in Hou's personal experience (his father died when he was 12) and the disintegration of traditional patriarchal family structure in Taiwan's industrialization process.
    - "Runaway son": Corresponding to the absent father, sons in the films always leave their rural hometowns for cities to make a living, growing up in the absence of paternal authority, reflecting the drastic social changes in Taiwan in the 1970s-1980s when a large number of rural populations moved to cities.
2.  **Family Emotion**: Female group images as the bearer of family emotion:
    - Early works feature traditional silent, sacrificing female images: grandmother, mother, and sister, who sacrifice their own needs (e.g., the sister in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* gives up the opportunity to study in Taipei to support the family) to maintain the family.
    - Later works gradually shift to independent, self-conscious female images: Vicky in *Millennium Mambo* (2001) and Yoko in *Coffee Jikou* (2004), who actively pursue their own emotional and life autonomy, which is directly influenced by Chu Tien-wen's female perspective.
3.  **Family-nation Emotion**: The "people living downstream of history" in his "Sadness Trilogy" (*A City of Sadness* 1989, *The Puppetmaster* 1993, *Good Men, Good Women* 1995):
    - The trilogy focuses on ordinary families and individuals caught in historical turbulence (Japanese colonial rule, 228 Incident, White Terror in Taiwan). Hou uses a distant, non-interventional gaze, does not take ideological sides, does not create intense dramatic conflict, only quietly presents the suffering and tenacity of ordinary people under the impact of history, expressing a gentle, implicit lament for ordinary people's fate, which aligns with the Chinese poetic tradition of "expressing resentment without anger".
### Step 3: Analyze "Scene" as the visual carrier of emotion
Link to Chinese classical aesthetic theory: The interaction of emotion and scene forms "artistic conception" (yì jìng), the highest aesthetic state in Chinese art, as stated by Qing Dynasty scholar Wang Fuzhi: "Emotion and scene are nominally two, but actually inseparable. A master of poetry blends them seamlessly without boundary."
Hou's scenes are divided into two categories corresponding to Wang Guowei's two states of artistic conception:
1.  **Selfless State: Scenes of trees**:
    Trees are a recurring scene in Hou's films, silent, long-lived, and act as objective witnesses of time and events, with emotion implicitly hidden in the scene, no direct expression of feeling. For example, the banyan tree in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* witnesses the childhood of the protagonist Ah Xiao, the passing of KMT cavalry, and the death of his parents.

### Step 4: Analyze "Object" as the concrete sustenance of emotion
Link to both Chinese classical "gan wu" (emotion stirred by external objects) theory and Japanese mono no aware aesthetic, adapted to Taiwan's special post-colonial cultural context. The three layers of mono no aware are clearly reflected in Hou's use of objects:
1.  **Movement by people**: Objects carrying romantic emotion, e.g., the ring given by Ah Yuan's mother to Ah Yun (Ah Yuan's girlfriend who married another man while Ah Yuan was in military service) in *Dust in the Wind*, which carries the mother's recognition of the two's relationship and the sadness of lost love.
2.  **Movement by social phenomena**: Objects carrying historical meaning, e.g., the bamboo furniture in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*: Ah Xiao's father, who moved to Taiwan from mainland China, only bought cheap bamboo furniture because he thought he would return to the mainland soon, but he never got to go back. The bamboo furniture carries the nostalgia of mainland immigrants in Taiwan for their hometown.
3.  **Movement by natural objects**: The recurring object of trains in almost all of Hou's films, which symbolizes the passage of time, the gap between urban and rural areas, and the act of leaving hometown, carrying the collective memory of Taiwan's rapid industrialization process.

## 5.2. Analytical Methods
Three complementary qualitative research methods are used:
1.  **Textual Close Reading**: Detailed analysis of the narrative content, visual language, character settings, and recurring imagery (trees, trains, bamboo furniture) in each of Hou's films, to extract the common traits related to literariness expression.
2.  **Historical Contextualization**: Placing Hou's creative choices in the specific social, political, and cultural context of Taiwan from the 1970s to the 2010s, to explain the origin and practical significance of his aesthetic choices, rather than interpreting them as purely personal artistic preferences.
3.  **Theoretical Docking**: Connecting the textual features of Hou's films to Chinese classical literary theory (lyrical tradition, artistic conception theory, Wang Guowei's state theory) and related East Asian aesthetic theories (mono no aware), to construct a systematic local interpretation framework, avoiding the forced application of Western theoretical concepts.
## 5.3. Analytical Baselines
The baseline interpretations of Hou's films, which this paper compares with and refutes, are the mainstream prior research conclusions:
1.  Hou's unique cinematic style is mainly derived from formal techniques: extensive use of long shots, fixed shots, non-linear narrative, and weak dramatic conflict.
2.  His widely recognized "lyrical" or "poetic" quality is a by-product of these formal techniques, not rooted in deeper cultural or literary traditions.
3.  Interpretations of his works should be based on Western film theoretical systems, which are considered universal.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The three core results of the research are:
1.  **Conscious Connection to Literary Tradition**: Hou's creative practice has a deep and conscious connection to Chinese literary tradition, not a formal coincidence. This connection comes from both external influences (the Nativist Literature Movement's requirement to reflect real local emotion) and internal influences (collaboration with Chu Tien-wen, influence of Shen Congwen's writing concept). This result breaks the view that Hou's style is a purely personal formal innovation.
2.  **Emotion-Scene-Object as Literariness Carriers**: The three dimensions of emotion, scene, and object are the concrete, interlinked carriers of Hou's film literariness, and their interplay forms the unique lyrical quality of his films: emotion is the core of expression, scene is the visual carrier of implicit emotion, and object is the concrete sustenance of emotion. In actual films, the three are always integrated and inseparable, and together achieve the traditional Chinese aesthetic effect of "emotion hidden in scene, meaning beyond words".
3.  **Refutation of Formalist Baseline**: The paper effectively refutes the mainstream baseline view that Hou's lyrical quality is only derived from formal techniques: formal techniques such as long shots and empty shots are not ends in themselves, but means to realize the interplay of emotion, scene, and object, to construct the artistic conception required by Chinese traditional aesthetics. Compared to prior formalist interpretations, this paper's framework better explains the cultural specificity of Hou's style, and why his films have unique Eastern aesthetic charm that resonates with global audiences.
## 6.2. Sub-analysis of Each Dimension
1.  **Emotion Dimension**: The three types of emotion (father-son, family, family-nation) are rooted in both Hou's personal life experience and the collective historical experience of Taiwanese people, so they have both personal authenticity and collective resonance, which is the core reason why his films can move audiences from different cultural backgrounds.
2.  **Scene Dimension**: The flexible use of "selfless state" and "with self state" scenes perfectly aligns with the Chinese traditional aesthetic pursuit of implicit emotion expression, which is completely different from the direct, intense emotional expression in most Western films, forming the unique gentle, implicit aesthetic style of Hou's works.
3.  **Object Dimension**: The integration of mono no aware aesthetic with Chinese lyrical tradition is adapted to Taiwan's special post-colonial cultural context, making Hou's expression of suffering and impermanence more tolerant and gentle, without intense criticism or emotional venting, reflecting the Eastern aesthetic of "moderation".
## 6.3. Limitations of the Analysis
The author explicitly points out one key limitation of the research: for the convenience of discussion, the paper separates the three interlinked dimensions of emotion, scene, and object into three independent chapters for analysis, which inevitably weakens the analysis of their integrated, inseparable relationship in specific film texts, because in actual films, emotion, scene, and object always appear together, not in isolation.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper breaks through the long-term over-reliance on Western theoretical frameworks in Hou Hsiao-hsien research, uses the three core categories of emotion, scene, and object in Chinese classical literary aesthetics as the analytical framework, and systematically demonstrates that the core of Hou's unique global cinematic style is the literariness rooted in the Chinese lyrical tradition, rather than formal techniques. The research proves that Chinese classical aesthetic theory has strong explanatory power for Chinese-language films, and provides a valuable model for building local interpretive systems for Chinese film studies, avoiding the erasure of cultural specificity caused by forced application of Western theories.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations
1.  As mentioned above, the separate discussion of emotion, scene, and object weakens the analysis of their integrated interaction in specific film texts.
2.  The analysis of the connection between Hou's films and Chinese literary tradition can be further deepened: the paper only uses general traditional aesthetic concepts, and does not carry out more detailed comparative analysis with specific classical literary works (e.g., Tang Dynasty landscape poetry, Song Dynasty lyrical prose).
3.  The paper does not make a detailed comparison between Hou's works and other Eastern directors' works (e.g., Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu), so it does not fully highlight the specificity of Hou's literariness rooted in Chinese tradition, rather than general Eastern aesthetic traits.
### Future Work Suggestions
1.  Expand the "emotion-scene-object" analytical framework to study other Chinese-language directors with strong lyrical traits (e.g., Jia Zhangke, Ang Lee, Bi Gan), to verify the generalizability of this framework for interpreting Chinese-language films.
2.  Compare the Eastern lyrical film tradition represented by Hou with Western poetic film traditions (e.g., European art film, Terrence Malick's works), to highlight the cultural specificity of Eastern cinematic aesthetics.
3.  Further explore the application of Chinese classical aesthetic theory in the study of contemporary Chinese art forms other than film, such as drama, television series, and digital media art, to explore how contemporary Chinese art inherits and transforms traditional aesthetic resources.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
1.  **Theoretical Value**: This paper is a very valuable attempt at decolonizing Chinese film studies. For a long time, Chinese film studies have been using Western theoretical systems to interpret local works, which often reduces local works to "examples" of Western theories, erasing their unique cultural connotations. This paper's use of native classical aesthetic theory to interpret Hou's films provides a successful model for local film studies to build their own independent interpretive systems.
2.  **Framework Appropriateness**: The choice of the three dimensions of emotion, scene, and object is very appropriate, as these three categories run through all traditional Chinese art forms (poetry, painting, calligraphy, opera), so they can well capture the commonality between Hou's films and traditional Chinese artistic pursuit, avoiding the problem of conceptual mismatch caused by applying Western categories.
3.  **Potential Improvement**: The paper could add more analysis of the audience reception dimension: for example, investigate how audiences with different cultural backgrounds perceive the interplay of emotion, scene, and object in Hou's films, to further verify the cross-cultural communicability of this local aesthetic trait.
4.  **Practical Significance**: The conclusion of this paper also has guiding significance for contemporary Chinese-language film creation: to create works with global influence, it is not necessary to blindly imitate Western film forms, but can draw nutrients from Chinese native literary and aesthetic traditions, which can form unique cultural competitiveness.
