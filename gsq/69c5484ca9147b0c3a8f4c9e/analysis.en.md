# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The paper focuses on the relationship between Taiwanese director Hou Hsiao-hsien’s process of `self-discovery` and the formation of his unique cinematic style, tracing the evolution of his creative identity across his entire filmography. The full title is *Hou Hsiao-hsien's Self-discovery and the Formation of His Film Style*.
## 1.2. Authors
The author is Du Yuqiu, a master’s student in Literary Theory (under the Chinese Language and Literature discipline, with a research focus on film aesthetics) at Northwest Normal University, China. Her graduate advisor is Professor Huang Huaipu. Prior to this thesis, she published two related papers on Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film aesthetics: *The Gaze of Life’s Long View: An Analysis of the Narrative Style of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s The Puppetmaster* (2016) and *The Growing Eastern Mirror Image* (2016).
## 1.3. Journal/Conference
This is an unpublished master’s degree thesis, submitted to Northwest Normal University for graduation requirements in May 2017. It is categorized as a graduate research output in the field of Chinese film studies and aesthetic theory.
## 1.4. Publication Year
2017
## 1.5. Abstract
This thesis takes leading Chinese-language director Hou Hsiao-hsien as a case study to explore the mechanism linking a director’s internal self-awareness process to the formation of their personal film style. It combines textual analysis of Hou’s representative works, the director’s personal biographical experience, and his creative context to systematically sort out the evolution of his individual consciousness awakening and stylistic characteristics. The study finds that the uniqueness of Hou’s film style lies in his delicate presentation of Taiwanese local culture and profound excavation of personal emotion, which reflects the director’s continuous exploration of self-cognition and cultural identity. The conclusion emphasizes that understanding the formation of Hou’s film style requires consideration of both the historical and cultural context and the director’s subjective agency.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem & Research Gap
Prior academic research on Hou Hsiao-hsien has almost exclusively focused on describing external features of his work: its oriental aesthetic characteristics, Taiwanese local identity, realist focus on ordinary people, and historical narrative. Very few studies have explored the *internal origin* of his style from the perspective of the director’s personal self-awareness, leaving a gap in understanding the causal link between a director’s individual identity and their creative output. In the broader field of film style research, most work also focuses on describing stylistic features rather than tracing how style emerges from a creator’s internal self-discovery process.
### Significance of the Problem
For film creators, understanding how personal experience and self-awareness shape unique artistic style is critical for developing original, personal creative voices, especially for Chinese-language filmmakers seeking to move beyond copying Western film conventions and develop distinct local cinematic aesthetics.
### Entry Point & Innovative Idea
The paper proposes `self-discovery` as the core mediating variable between a director’s personal life experience, cultural background, and their final film style. It builds a systematic framework to trace how self-discovery emerges, is structured into a coherent worldview, and is expressed formally across different genres via two key mechanisms: `illusion (幻化)` and `experience restructuring (重构)`.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1.  **Theoretical Framework**: It establishes a new explanatory framework for film style formation centered on `self-discovery`, breaking the process into three sequential formative stages (`self-accumulation` → `self-perception` → personal worldview construction) and two extension mechanisms (`illusion` and `experience restructuring`).
2.  **Empirical Application**: It applies this framework to systematically trace Hou Hsiao-hsien’s stylistic evolution across his 40-year career, from his early commercial romantic comedies to his later award-winning historical, urban, and wuxia works, proving the consistent core of his style is rooted in his unique self-discovery process.
3.  **Generalizable Insights**: It extracts generalizable rules for personal film style formation, providing an actionable reference for other filmmakers seeking to develop their own unique creative voices.
### Key Conclusions
1.  `Self-discovery` is the core driver of personal film style formation: all external influences (era, experience, culture) shape style only through their internalization into the director’s self-awareness.
2.  Hou’s unique style (detached long takes, plot-light natural narrative, focus on the desolation of individual fate under natural/historical laws) is a direct externalization of his worldview, formed by his traumatic youth (early loss of parents and grandmother, experience growing up as a marginalized delinquent in rural Taiwan) and deep influence from traditional Chinese Taoist culture and modern Chinese literature (especially Shen Congwen’s writing).
3.  After a core style is formed, it can be extended to entirely different genres via `illusion` (adapting the core style to new topics while retaining its core identity) and `experience restructuring` (reconfiguring personal life experience and learned artistic techniques to fit new creative needs).

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
The following core concepts are required to understand this paper, with beginner-friendly explanations:
1.  **Auteur theory (auteurism)**: A core film studies theory originating from 1950s French film journal *Cahiers du Cinéma*, which argues that the director is the "author" of a film, and their consistent personal creative vision (reflected in repeated thematic concerns and formal stylistic choices across their filmography) is the primary marker of a film’s artistic value. This paper is rooted in this theoretical tradition.
2.  **Film style**: Defined in the paper by film scholar David Bordwell as the characteristic way a filmmaker uses core film techniques (cinematography, editing, sound design, narrative structure) to organize the formal system of their work. It is the unique "signature" that distinguishes one director’s work from another.
3.  `Self-discovery` (as defined in this paper): The active process where an individual (here, a film director) extracts their unique identity, life experiences, and worldview from the influence of generic external social norms, achieving clear self-awareness distinct from mainstream values.
4.  `Self-accumulation`: The sum of external influences (social and historical context, personal life experience, cultural literacy) that an individual internalizes over their lifetime, forming the raw material for self-discovery.
5.  `Self-perception`: The unique, subjective way an individual interprets and feels about external events, directly shaped by their prior self-accumulation. It acts as the emotional trigger for self-discovery.
6.  `Illusion (幻化, Huanhua)`: The mechanism where a director’s consistent core style adapts to different film genres/topics, appearing in different formal forms while retaining its core identity and thematic concerns.
7.  `Experience restructuring (重构, Chonggou)`: The creative process where a director integrates their personal life experiences and learned artistic experiences, reconfiguring them into unique narrative and formal expressions in their films that carry their personal signature.
8.  **Long take**: A continuous film shot that lasts for an extended period (usually longer than 30 seconds) without cutting. It is the most iconic formal feature of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s style.
9.  **Montage**: The technique of assembling separate shots together to create meaning. Hou’s style intentionally avoids rapid, dramatic montage to prioritize objective presentation of reality.
## 3.2. Previous Works
### Film Style Research
1.  **Western foundational work**: David Bordwell’s *Film Art: Form and Style* (2008) provides the core definition of film style used in the paper. Other key prior work includes studies tracing individual directors’ style to personal experience, such as research on Ozu Yasujiro’s silent films showing how his childhood experience shaped his signature static camera style, and studies on Alfred Hitchcock showing how his childhood trauma shaped his focus on suspense and guilt themes.
2.  **Chinese-language research**: Most prior Chinese film style research focuses on describing stylistic features of directors, genre styles, or national film styles, rather than exploring the internal origin of style from the director’s self-awareness.
### Hou Hsiao-hsien Research
Prior research on Hou falls into four main categories:
1.  **Oriental aesthetics analysis**: Studies focusing on Hou’s use of long takes, poetic narrative, and Taoist-influenced view of life, such as Chen Xuguang’s comparative study of Hou and Jia Zhangke’s long lens aesthetics, which argues Hou’s long takes carry unique Eastern cultural connotations of ethical tradition and lyricism that Western long takes lack.
2.  **Local identity analysis**: Studies exploring Hou’s representation of Taiwanese local experience and nostalgia, including James Uden’s *No Man is an Island: The Cinema of Hou Hsiao-hsien* (2014), which incorrectly claims Hou’s work only reflects Taiwanese identity and has no connection to broader Chinese cultural identity (a claim the paper implicitly rejects).
3.  **Realism analysis**: Studies focusing on Hou’s compassionate portrayal of ordinary marginalized people, such as Li Daoxin’s analysis of *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* which notes Hou’s unique ability to naturally integrate themes of life, death, and grief into everyday life depictions.
4.  **Historical narrative analysis**: Studies focusing on Hou’s representation of Taiwanese history in films like *City of Sadness* and *The Puppetmaster*, which note his unique focus on individual experience rather than grand historical narratives.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
In the field of auteur theory, research has evolved in three stages:
1.  Early stage (1950s-1970s): Focused on identifying consistent stylistic markers to prove a director’s authorship of their work.
2.  Middle stage (1980s-2000s): Focused on linking stylistic markers to the director’s personal background and cultural context.
3.  Recent stage (2010s-present): Focused on exploring the internal psychological and cognitive processes that connect a director’s personal experience to their stylistic choices.
    This paper’s focus on `self-discovery` as the core mediating mechanism fits directly into this latest stage of evolution in auteur theory research.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Unlike all prior Hou Hsiao-hsien research, which only describes *what* his stylistic features are, this paper explains *why* those features exist, tracing their origin directly to the director’s internal self-discovery process. It also extends beyond single-case analysis to propose a generalizable framework for style formation applicable to other auteur directors.

# 4. Methodology
This is a qualitative humanities study rooted in film auteur theory and aesthetic analysis. Its core principle is that a director’s personal film style is the external manifestation of their internal self-discovery process, with a clear causal flow from personal experience to self-awareness to formal stylistic expression.
## 4.1. Principles
The core theoretical intuition of the paper is that all external influences (social context, life experience, cultural knowledge) only shape a director’s creative style after they are internalized into the director’s unique self-awareness via the process of self-discovery. This avoids the one-sided determinism of purely social context-focused or purely biographical-focused research, balancing the influence of external context and the director’s subjective agency.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The paper’s analytical framework proceeds in five sequential, interconnected steps, fully integrated with the case study of Hou Hsiao-hsien:
### Step 1: Self-accumulation as the foundational raw material for style
Self-accumulation refers to all external influences an individual internalizes over their lifetime, and is the necessary precondition for self-discovery. It has three mutually reinforcing components:
1.  **Era and social context**: Hou grew up in post-1949 Taiwan, and began his directorial career in the 1980s during the rise of the Taiwan New Cinema movement, which rejected the previous era’s political propaganda films and formulaic commercial films, advocating for artistic freedom, local expression, and personal storytelling. This social context allowed him to experiment with non-commercial, personal creative styles.
2.  **Personal experience**: Hou’s traumatic youth is the core of his self-accumulation: he moved from Guangdong, China to Taiwan as an infant, his father suffered from long-term illness and died when he was 12, his mother attempted suicide twice and died when he was 16, his grandmother died when he was 17. He grew up as a delinquent in the rural town of Fengshan, participating in gang activities, which gave him intimate understanding of marginalized groups.
3.  **Cultural literacy**: Hou was deeply influenced by traditional Chinese culture (Taoist thought, classical poetry, traditional opera like Taiwanese opera and glove puppetry), modern Chinese literature (Shen Congwen’s autobiography was a key trigger for his self-awareness, teaching him the detached, compassionate perspective of viewing life and death as natural events under the sun), and extensive reading of foreign literature and film.
### Step 2: Self-perception as the trigger for self-discovery
Self-perception is the unique subjective interpretation of the world shaped by self-accumulation, and acts as the emotional trigger that activates self-discovery. Hou’s self-perception has three defining features:
1.  **Perception of rural vs urban space**: His rural upbringing makes him portray rural space as warm, nostalgic, and full of human warmth, while urban space is portrayed as confused, alienated, and full of spiritual emptiness.
2.  **Perception of marginalized groups**: His own experience as a delinquent and gang member makes him view marginalized groups (delinquents, gang members, ordinary people affected by historical upheaval) with sympathy and compassion, rather than the negative stereotypes common in mainstream media.
3.  **Perception of life, death, and history**: His early experience of repeatedly losing close family members and Taoist cultural influence makes him view life, death, and historical change with a calm, detached, and compassionate perspective, believing that all human joys and sorrows are natural events under the law of nature, requiring no exaggerated dramatization.
### Step 3: Personal worldview construction as the blueprint for formal style
Self-perception is organized into a coherent, stable personal worldview via rational reflection and cultural accumulation, which directly shapes the formal features of a director’s style. Hou’s worldview is composed of three mutually consistent components:
1.  **View of human affairs**: Life, death, joy, and sorrow are all natural parts of the objective law of nature, so they should be presented objectively, without direct emotional intervention or dramatic exaggeration.
2.  **View of history**: In the context of large historical changes, individual suffering and life/death are unavoidable, and people continue to live on regardless, leaving only the desolation of human existence in the wake of historical change.
3.  **View of time and space**: Film should prioritize presenting the authentic atmosphere and charm of a specific time and space, rather than using time only as a tool to connect plot points. Long takes should be used to preserve the completeness of time and space, allowing the audience to actively experience the scene rather than being told what to feel via editing.
    This worldview directly shapes Hou’s iconic formal stylistic features:
- **Cinematography**: Fixed-position long shot taken from a distance, avoiding close-ups and rapid montage to maintain objectivity.
- **Narrative**: Loose, plot-light narrative that follows the natural flow of everyday life, rather than strict three-act dramatic structure with clear causal links between scenes.
- **Editing**: Two unique editing techniques inspired by traditional Chinese aesthetics:
  1.  `Rhythm editing`: Cuts are based on the emotional tension and underlying rhythm between scenes, rather than plot causality. For example, in *City of Sadness*, a scene of the eldest brother’s funeral is directly cut to a scene of the fourth brother’s wedding, with no transition, to create a tension that reflects the natural coexistence of life and death under historical change.
  2.  `Cloud block editing`: Scenes are connected like floating clouds, with no clear causal links, only following the natural flow of memory and life experience. For example, in *The Puppetmaster*, scenes of the protagonist’s life are presented as disconnected fragments, just like how memory works.
### Step 4: Illusion as the mechanism for style extension
Once a core style is formed, `illusion` allows the director to adapt it to entirely new genres and topics while retaining its core identity. For Hou, this means his core worldview and formal preferences are adapted to four very different genres:
- Rural autobiographical films (*A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, *Dust in the Wind*)
- Historical films (*City of Sadness*, *The Puppetmaster*, *Good Men, Good Women*)
- Urban films (*Millennium Mambo*, *Goodbye South, Goodbye*)
- Wuxia (martial arts) films (*The Assassin*)
  In all these genres, the core features (detached long takes, focus on individual tragic fate, natural narrative) remain consistent, while only the surface formal features adapt to the genre (e.g. handheld long takes are used in urban films to show the chaotic, alienated feeling of modern urban life).
### Step 5: Experience restructuring as the mechanism for style realization
`Experience restructuring` is the concrete creative process that turns internal self-awareness into concrete film content and form, with two components:
1.  **Life experience restructuring**: Hou extracts specific, emotionally charged elements from his personal life experience (billiard halls, trains, absent father figures, gang stories, the visual of wind blowing through leaves) and places them in different film contexts across all genres, infusing all his works with his personal emotional signature. He also restructures his core worldview to apply to characters from very different backgrounds, seeing all marginalized people through the same compassionate lens.
2.  **Art experience restructuring**: Hou reworks imported Western film techniques to fit his Eastern aesthetic needs: for example, Western realist long takes are designed to show factual accuracy, but Hou restructures the long take to prioritize showing the atmospheric charm and poetic feeling of a scene, rather than just factual accuracy. He also develops his unique editing techniques based on traditional Chinese aesthetic principles of charm and blank space, rather than Western narrative editing conventions.

## 5.2. Evaluation Metrics (Analytical Framework)
The paper uses a qualitative analytical framework that verifies the link between Hou’s self-discovery and his style by checking for consistent alignment between three layers:
1.  **Biographical layer**: Consistency between Hou’s documented life experience and the thematic concerns in his films.
2.  **Self-report layer**: Consistency between Hou’s own statements in interviews and lectures about his creative philosophy and the formal features of his films.
3.  **Textual layer**: Consistency of core stylistic and thematic features across his entire filmography, even across very different genres.
## 5.3. Baselines
The paper compares Hou’s work to three sets of baselines to highlight the uniqueness of his style rooted in his self-discovery:
1.  **Intra-author baseline**: Comparing Hou’s early commercial works (before his self-discovery) to his later works (after self-discovery) to show the clear shift in style after he achieved self-awareness.
2.  **Cross-genre baseline**: Comparing Hou’s works across different genres (rural, historical, urban, wuxia) to show the consistent core of his style even when surface formal features change.
3.  **Cross-director baseline**: Comparing Hou’s work to other directors working in the same genres (Jia Zhangke for realist long-take film, Wong Kar-wai for urban film, King Hu and Ang Lee for wuxia film) to show how his unique self-discovery makes his treatment of the genre distinct.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
### Result 1: Staged evolution of Hou’s self-discovery and style formation
The paper identifies four clear stages in Hou’s career that align perfectly with the proposed self-discovery framework:
1.  **Gestation stage (1980-1982)**: Self-discovery is still unconscious. His early commercial romantic comedies have no distinct personal style, but already contain embryonic elements of his later concerns: rural nostalgia, childhood experience, and focus on ordinary people.
2.  **Establishment stage (1983)**: Self-discovery is explicitly triggered by reading Shen Congwen’s autobiography, which helped him recognize his own detached, compassionate perspective on life. *The Boys from Fengkuei* is the turning point work, where he first consciously uses distant long takes and non-dramatic narrative, marking the formation of his core style.
3.  **Refinement stage (1984-1987)**: Self-discovery is refined and consolidated through a series of autobiographical rural films (*A Summer at Grandpa's*, *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, *Dust in the Wind*), where he perfects his formal style and thematic concerns.
4.  **Extension stage (1989-present)**: Self-discovery is stable, so he extends his core style to new genres via `illusion` and `experience restructuring`, creating unique works in historical, urban, and wuxia genres that all carry his personal signature.
### Result 2: Consistent core style across all genres
Across all genres, Hou’s work retains three invariant core features directly derived from his worldview:
1.  **Invariant cinematography**: All his films use long takes from a detached distance, avoiding close-ups and dramatic montage to prioritize objective presentation of the scene’s atmosphere. Even in *Millennium Mambo* (his most formally "unstable" urban film), the handheld long takes still retain the detached perspective, only adapting to show the chaotic feeling of urban life.
2.  **Invariant thematic focus**: All his films focus on marginalized figures and the tragedy of individual fate under impersonal natural or historical laws, regardless of whether the character is a rural delinquent, a historical figure, an urban youth, or a wuxia assassin.
3.  **Invariant narrative logic**: All his films use loose, natural narrative that follows the flow of life rather than strict dramatic causality, with editing based on emotional rhythm rather than plot needs.
### Result 3: Comparative advantage relative to other directors
The paper finds that Hou’s style is clearly distinct from other directors working in the same genre, due to his unique self-discovery:
1.  Compared to Jia Zhangke’s long takes, which focus on the impact of China’s social change on ordinary people, Hou’s long takes are more focused on the universal desolation of individual existence, rooted in his Taoist worldview.
2.  Compared to Wong Kar-wai’s urban films, which focus on the romantic alienation between individuals, Hou’s urban films focus on the broader spiritual confusion and emptiness of modern life, rooted in his detached perspective on human suffering.
3.  Compared to King Hu and Ang Lee’s wuxia films, which focus on martial arts spectacle and chivalric ethics, Hou’s *The Assassin* focuses on the individual’s tragic fate and choice between personal emotion and duty, with almost no exciting action scenes, prioritizing the presentation of the character’s internal state.
## 6.2. Limitation Result
The paper finds that Hou’s urban films are less critically successful and artistically complete than his rural, historical, and wuxia films. This is because his self-accumulation contains very little direct experience of modern urban youth life, so his self-perception of urban themes is less acute than his perception of rural or historical themes. He describes this as using an "old man’s detached perspective" to observe urban youth, rather than having the intuitive, personal feeling he has for other themes. This result confirms the paper’s core claim: style quality is directly linked to the depth of the director’s self-discovery and self-accumulation related to the theme.
## 6.3. Ablation Analysis (Component Verification)
The paper implicitly verifies the necessity of each component of the self-discovery framework via ablation analysis:
1.  When Hou lacked clear self-discovery (early commercial film stage), his works had no distinct personal style, proving self-discovery is necessary for unique style formation.
2.  When he attempted to work on themes where his self-accumulation and self-perception were weak (early urban film *Daughter of the Nile*), the work was artistically unsuccessful, proving self-accumulation and self-perception are necessary foundations for effective style expression.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper systematically demonstrates that Hou Hsiao-hsien’s unique film style is fundamentally the external manifestation of his internal self-discovery process. His traumatic early life experience and deep traditional Chinese cultural background form his self-accumulation, which shapes his unique self-perception of the world, which is then organized into a coherent worldview that directly shapes his formal stylistic choices. After his core style is formed, he extends it to different genres via `illusion`, and realizes concrete stylistic expression via `experience restructuring` of his personal life and artistic experiences.
The paper’s broader conclusion is that `self-discovery` is the core driving force of personal film style formation. For filmmakers, respecting and expressing their unique personal experience and self-perception, rather than copying Western film conventions or commercial genre formulas, is the only way to develop a unique, artistically valuable personal style, especially for Chinese-language filmmakers seeking to develop a distinct local cinematic aesthetic.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The paper explicitly notes its own limitations:
1.  **Single case study limitation**: It only uses Hou Hsiao-hsien as a case study, so the generalizability of the self-discovery framework to other directors (especially directors from different cultural backgrounds) needs further verification.
2.  **Lack of industrial context analysis**: It focuses almost exclusively on the director’s internal psychological process, and does not conduct in-depth analysis of how external factors like the Taiwan film industry’s evolution, market pressure, and audience reception interacted with Hou’s self-discovery process to shape his style.
### Suggested Future Work
1.  Expand the framework to analyze other Chinese auteur directors clearly influenced by Hou (e.g. Jia Zhangke, Bi Gan) to verify the generalizability of the self-discovery framework.
2.  Integrate analysis of industrial and market factors into the framework to explore how external structural factors interact with the director’s internal self-discovery to shape style.
3.  Conduct more fine-grained formal analysis of specific film scenes (e.g. quantitative analysis of shot length, editing rhythm) to more concretely verify the link between the director’s worldview and specific formal choices.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper makes a very valuable contribution to auteur theory by moving beyond descriptive analysis of stylistic features to explain the internal psychological mechanism that links a director’s personal experience to their creative output. The self-discovery framework it proposes is highly applicable to the analysis of many contemporary Chinese directors who draw heavily on autobiographical experience for their work.
One potential area for improvement is that the paper could have further explored how Hou’s self-discovery as a post-1949 migrant to Taiwan shapes the complex negotiation between Taiwanese local identity and broader Chinese cultural identity in his films, a theme that is only briefly touched on despite being a core concern of his historical works. The paper correctly rejects James Uden’s incorrect claim that Hou’s work has no Chinese cultural identity, but it does not fully explain how Hou’s unique self-identity (as a Chinese person raised in Taiwan) shapes this complex thematic tension.
Another strength of the paper is its clear practical value for young filmmakers: it provides a concrete, actionable path for developing a personal style, emphasizing that the core of original creation is exploring one’s own unique experience and self-awareness, rather than mastering fashionable techniques or copying successful formulas. This is an especially important insight in the current Chinese film industry, where commercial genre formulas and Hollywood imitation are increasingly common.
Overall, this paper is a very accessible, systematic analysis of the origin of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s style, and makes a meaningful contribution to both Chinese film studies and broader auteur theory research.
