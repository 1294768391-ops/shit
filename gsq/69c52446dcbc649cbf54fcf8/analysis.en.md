# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The paper's title is *History, Public Memory and Identity: "Waishengren" Writings in Taiwan New Cinema (1982-1987)*. Its core focus is analyzing how the group known as "waishengren" (mainland Chinese migrants to Taiwan post-1945) is represented in films from the 1982-1987 Taiwan New Cinema movement, and how these portrayals reflect the group's historical experience and identity struggles.
## 1.2. Authors
The sole author is Mo Jiani, a Master's candidate in Drama and Film Studies at the School of Communication, East China Normal University, supervised by Professor Nie Xinru, a leading scholar of film theory in China.
## 1.3. Publication Venue
This is a 2020 Master's degree dissertation published in the East China Normal University Master's Thesis Archive, a peer-reviewed repository for graduate research in humanities and social sciences in China.
## 1.4. Publication Year
2020
## 1.5. Abstract
The paper first establishes that "waishengren" (literally "people from other provinces") refers to people who migrated from mainland China to Taiwan between 1945 and 1955, a group long stereotyped as a homogeneous, privileged elite in both popular discourse and prior media portrayals. It focuses on the 1982-1987 Taiwan New Cinema (TNC) movement, a period on the eve of Taiwan's lifting of 40 years of martial law, when discussions of ethnic conflict between waishengren and local Taiwanese first entered the public sphere. The author analyzes 13 accessible TNC films featuring waishengren characters, categorizing their portrayals into three groups: self-enclosed first-generation waishengren with a "return to mainland roots" identity, self-exiled second-generation waishengren with a "take root in Taiwan" identity, and diverse marginal waishengren figures. It cross-references these film portrayals with historical memoirs, oral histories, and official records to confirm their general historical accuracy, and uses spatial symbolic analysis of juancun (military dependents' villages), urban, and campus spaces to unpack how identity is visually constructed in the films. The paper concludes that TNC broke from prior sanitized propaganda portrayals of waishengren, providing a nuanced, humanizing view of the group's diverse experience and intergenerational identity splits.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem Addressed
Waishengren are a highly diverse group of roughly 1 million people who migrated to Taiwan with the KMT (Nationalist Party) after its defeat in the Chinese civil war in 1949. Popular stereotypes frame them as a monolithic, politically privileged elite, ignoring the large share of working-class waishengren (e.g. low-ranking veterans, laborers) who faced extreme poverty, isolation, and displacement. Prior research on Taiwan New Cinema only mentions waishengren in passing, without focused analysis of their representation, and prior media portrayals of the group were either state propaganda or sanitized feel-good stories that erased real ethnic conflict and identity struggles.
### Importance of the Problem
Waishengren identity is a core fault line in Taiwanese politics and cross-strait relations. Misunderstandings about the group's diverse experience and identity splits contribute to polarization both in Taiwan and between Taiwan and mainland China. Film as a mass medium shapes collective memory and public perceptions of marginalized groups, so analyzing realistic portrayals of waishengren in TNC can help reduce stereotypes and improve cross-group understanding.
### Entry Point & Innovation
The paper is the first study to systematically analyze waishengren representation specifically in the 1982-1987 TNC period, a pivotal moment when martial law censorship began to weaken and filmmakers (most of whom were second-generation waishengren themselves) first sought to portray authentic local lived experience. It combines textual film analysis, historical comparative research, and spatial symbolic analysis to link film portrayals to real historical experience and identity formation.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1.  **Character Categorization**: It systematically categorizes waishengren characters in 13 TNC films into three distinct groups, documenting a clear intergenerational identity split: first-generation waishengren overwhelmingly hold a "fallen leaves return to roots" identity (desire to return to mainland China), while second-generation waishengren (born and raised entirely in Taiwan) hold a "take root where you land" identity (identify with Taiwan as their home, though with significant anxiety about belonging).
2.  **Historical Validation**: It confirms that almost all TNC waishengren portrayals align with memoirs, oral histories, and official census data, countering the monolithic privileged stereotype by showing waishengren across all social classes, from homeless veterans to dance hostesses to small business owners.
3.  **Spatial Analysis**: It demonstrates that TNC uses three core symbolic spaces to visually represent identity formation: closed, nostalgic juancun (military dependents' village) spaces associated with first-generation traditional identity, disorienting globalized urban Taipei spaces associated with second-generation identity confusion, and authoritarian campus spaces associated with state efforts to shape collective memory.
4.  **Limitation Identification**: It notes that TNC failed to portray upper-class waishengren elites (e.g. high-ranking KMT officials) due to pre-1987 martial law censorship, a gap that was only addressed in post-TNC films after martial law was lifted (e.g. *A Brighter Summer Day*, *City of Sadness*).

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All specialized terms are defined below for beginner readers:
- **Taiwan New Cinema (TNC, 1982-1987)**: A landmark Taiwanese film movement that began with the 1982 anthology film *In Our Time* and ended in 1987 when 54 filmmakers signed the *Taiwan Film Manifesto*. It rejected commercial escapist and propaganda films in favor of realistic, autobiographical stories of local Taiwanese life, and launched the careers of world-renowned directors like Hou Hsiao-hsien and Edward Yang.
- **Waishengren**: A Taiwan-specific term referring to people who migrated from mainland China to Taiwan between 1945 (end of Japanese colonial rule in Taiwan) and 1955 (end of KMT military retreat from the mainland). They are distinct from benshengren ("local people"), who lived in Taiwan before 1945.
- **Juancun (Military Dependents' Villages)**: Temporary, tightly segregated housing built by the KMT after 1949 for waishengren military families. These closed, insular communities became the core symbol of waishengren collective experience, as residents shared similar displacement trauma and nostalgia for mainland China.
- **Martial Law in Taiwan (1949-1987)**: 38 years of authoritarian KMT rule that included strict film censorship, a ban on travel to mainland China, and suppression of discussion of the 1947 228 Incident (a violent anti-KMT uprising that deepened hostility between benshengren and waishengren).
- **Collective Memory**: A concept first developed by sociologist Maurice Halbwachs, referring to shared memories of a social group that are shaped by culture and media, and form the foundation of group identity.
- **Identity (in this paper context)**: Specifically ethnic and national identity, the sense of belonging to a particular geographic, ethnic, or national group.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The paper organizes prior research into three core areas:
1.  **Taiwan New Cinema Research**: The foundational works on TNC are Jiao Xiongping's 1988 *Taiwan New Cinema* (focused on industrial history and aesthetics) and Chen Ruxiu's 1993 *The Historical and Cultural Experience of Taiwan New Cinema* (focused on cultural context). All prior TNC research only mentions waishengren in passing, without focused analysis of their representation.
2.  **Waishengren Research**: The only full monograph on the group is French scholar Stéphane Corcuff's 2004 *Stately Wind and Warm Sun: Taiwan Waishengren and Shifts in National Identity*. Most other waishengren research is in literary studies, focused on works by waishengren writers like Bai Xianyong (*Taipei People*) and Zhu Tianwen, with no focus on film representation.
3.  **Waishengren in Film Research**: Prior film research only studies juancun-themed films as a genre, focusing on the setting rather than the waishengren people themselves and their identity struggles. No prior work focuses specifically on waishengren representation in 1982-1987 TNC.

    The paper also notes the three stages of pre-TNC waishengren film portrayals:
- 1950s: Anti-communist propaganda films that framed benshengren-waishengren conflict as a CCP plot, with no realistic portrayal of lived experience.
- 1960s-1970s: "Healthy Realism" films that only showed harmonious, conflict-free relations between benshengren and waishengren, erasing real ethnic tensions.
## 3.3. Technological/Field Evolution
Film portrayals of waishengren evolved in three distinct stages:
1.  **1949-1979: Propaganda and Sanitized Portrayals**: Film was used as a state propaganda tool, with no room for authentic portrayals of waishengren displacement, poverty, or intergroup conflict.
2.  **1982-1987: Taiwan New Cinema**: Pre-lifting of martial law, censorship weakened, and second-generation waishengren filmmakers (who had grown up in Taiwan and experienced juancun life firsthand) began making realist films that centered marginalized local experiences, including waishengren lived experience.
3.  **Post-1987: Post-TNC Critical Realism**: After martial law was lifted, filmmakers could address previously taboo topics (e.g. 228 Incident, upper-class waishengren privilege) and produce even more critical, nuanced portrayals of the group.

    This paper's work is the first focused analysis of the pivotal 1982-1987 transition stage, when authentic waishengren portrayals first appeared on screen.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Unlike prior works that either ignore waishengren in TNC or only study juancun as a setting, this paper:
- Centers the waishengren people themselves, their identity struggles, and intergenerational differences, rather than treating them as a background detail.
- Cross-references film portrayals with primary historical sources (memoirs, oral histories) to validate their accuracy, rather than only analyzing the film text in isolation.
- Uses spatial symbolic analysis to unpack how physical spaces in the films shape and reflect identity formation, a framework absent from prior related research.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The paper uses a mixed-methods cultural studies approach, grounded in three core theoretical frameworks:
1.  **Textual Film Analysis**: Close reading of film content, characters, and visual language to identify patterns in waishengren representation.
2.  **Historical Comparative Analysis**: Matching film portrayals to primary historical sources to verify their authenticity, and contextualize them within the broader 1980s Taiwanese social context.
3.  **Spatial Symbolic Analysis**: Drawing on Henri Lefebvre's theory of spatial production (which argues that space is not neutral, it is shaped by social power relations and actively shapes the identity of people who occupy it) and Roland Barthes' theory of myth (which argues that cultural objects like film carry hidden symbolic meanings that naturalize ideological narratives), to analyze how different spaces in the films correspond to different identity orientations.

    The core intuition of the study is that film as a mass medium constructs collective memory and shapes public perceptions of marginalized groups, so the realist TNC portrayals of waishengren provide a unique window into the group's actual lived experience and identity struggles in the 1980s.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The study proceeds in four sequential steps:
### Step 1: Sample Selection
The author first compiles a full list of 15 TNC films that feature waishengren characters, from official TNC filmographies and prior research. 3 films (*Taipei Myth*, *Sunny Dad*, *Crystal Boys*) are excluded because no accessible prints exist, leaving a final sample of 13 films that represent the full accessible corpus of TNC waishengren content.
### Step 2: Character Categorization and Textual Analysis
The author conducts a close, frame-by-frame analysis of all waishengren characters in the 13 sample films, and categorizes them into three distinct groups based on their traits and identity orientation:
1.  **Self-enclosed first-generation waishengren (elders/fathers)**:
    - Core traits: Face language barriers (cannot speak local Minnan dialect, only speak their home mainland dialect or broken Mandarin), are often "silenced" (either physically mute, as the veteran protagonist of *Papa, Can You Hear Me Sing*, or socially marginalized and unable to have their experiences recognized), self-isolate from local Taiwanese society, and are consumed by nostalgia for their mainland home.
    - Identity orientation: *Luò yè guī gēn* ("fallen leaves return to their roots"): They view mainland China as their true home, and see themselves as temporary residents of Taiwan waiting to return.
    - Subcategories: Impoverished disabled veterans, men in difficult inter-ethnic marriages with local women, isolated elders suffering from intergenerational conflict with their Taiwan-born children.
2.  **Self-exiled second-generation waishengren (children/youth)**:
    - Core traits: Born or raised entirely in Taiwan, speak multiple languages (Minnan, Mandarin, their parents' home dialect), rebel against parental authority and traditional values, experience persistent confusion about their sense of belonging, often drift in urban spaces or join youth gangs.
    - Identity orientation: *Luò dì shēng gēn* ("take root where you land"): They view Taiwan as their home, but face significant anxiety about their identity, as they are often treated as "outsiders" by local benshengren, and do not share their parents' nostalgia for mainland China.
    - Subcategories: Rebellious gang-affiliated young men, disoriented teen girls navigating consumerist urban Taipei.
3.  **Diverse marginal waishengren characters**:
    Beyond the intergenerational core, the author identifies a range of other waishengren characters to demonstrate the group's diversity: kind, gentle juancun fathers, violent, distant authoritarian fathers, hardworking traditional mothers who hold families together, sharp but kind Shanghai-born dance hostesses, and powerless waishengren police officers who cannot communicate with local residents due to language barriers. These characters counter the stereotype of all waishengren as privileged elites.
### Step 3: Historical Validation
The author cross-references every character type and plot point from the films with primary historical sources to verify their accuracy:
- Oral history collections (e.g. *Taiwan Veteran Oral History*, which records first-hand accounts of 50+ waishengren veterans)
- Memoirs written by waishengren and their children (e.g. *Colonel's Son*, *Wandering Family: The Father-Son Tragedy from Mainland to Taiwan*)
- Official government data (e.g. 1967 Taiwan Population Research Center survey showing 31.5% of waishengren men worked in military, police, or security roles)
- Peer-reviewed sociological studies of waishengren experience (e.g. Corcuff 2004)
### Step 4: Spatial Symbolic Analysis
The author analyzes three core recurring spaces in the sample films, and their symbolic links to identity formation, drawing on Lefebvre's spatial production framework:
1.  **Juancun (Military Dependents' Village) Space**:
    - Community public spaces: Tight-knit, closed communities with strong social bonds, shared mainland nostalgia, and minimal contact with local benshengren, symbolizing stable traditional first-generation "return to roots" identity.
    - Closed home spaces: Private domestic spaces where elders only speak their home mainland dialect, reminisce about their life in China, and isolate themselves from local Taiwanese life, symbolizing the first generation's rejection of integration into Taiwanese society.
2.  **Urban Space (1980s Taipei)**:
    Portrayed as a disorienting, globalized space filled with US/Japanese cultural symbols (KFC restaurants, Japanese manga, discos, American military personnel), symbolizing the breakdown of traditional family structures, the erosion of traditional identity, and the hybrid, fragmented identity formation of second-generation waishengren.
3.  **Campus Space**:
    Portrayed as a site of KMT state assimilation policy: Mandatory Mandarin language rules, bans on local dialects, and textbooks that push KMT propaganda about "recovering the mainland" and erase the 228 Incident. It symbolizes authoritarian state control over collective memory and identity, and is often the site of second-generation youth rebellion against both state and parental authority.

# 5. Experimental Setup
This is a humanities cultural studies paper, so the following sections adapt the experimental setup framework to the study's qualitative research design:
## 5.1. Datasets
The study uses two complementary datasets:
1.  **Film Sample Dataset**: The 13 accessible TNC waishengren films listed in Table 1 below. These films are selected because they represent the full corpus of surviving TNC content featuring waishengren characters, and are directed by core TNC filmmakers (Hou Hsiao-hsien, Wan Ren, Li Youning, etc.) who are representative of the movement's realist ethos.
2.  **Historical Validation Dataset**: A collection of 9 memoirs, 2 oral history collections, 3 official government datasets, and 12 peer-reviewed academic studies of waishengren experience, all listed in the paper's references. These sources are chosen because they are either primary first-hand accounts of waishengren lived experience, or authoritative peer-reviewed studies, making them valid for verifying the accuracy of film portrayals.
## 5.2. Evaluation Metrics
The study uses three qualitative evaluation metrics, defined below:
1.  **Representational Validity**: The degree to which film characters, plot points, and lived experiences match accounts from primary historical sources.
2.  **Symbolic Coherence**: The degree to which spatial representations in the films consistently align with the identity orientations of the characters who occupy them (e.g. juancun spaces align with first-generation "return to roots" identity).
3.  **Stereotype Subversion**: The degree to which the films counter the mainstream monolithic stereotype of waishengren as a homogeneous, politically privileged elite, by showing diverse, working-class, marginalized waishengren characters.
## 5.3. Baselines
The study compares its findings to two core baselines:
1.  **Pre-TNC Film Portrayals**: 1950s propaganda films and 1960s-70s Healthy Realism films, which only showed sanitized, conflict-free portrayals of waishengren.
2.  **Mainstream Public Stereotypes**: The widespread belief that all waishengren are privileged, pro-KMT, and share a uniform desire to reunify with mainland China.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
### Character Representation Findings
1.  **First-generation waishengren portrayals are largely historically accurate**: 92% of first-generation character traits and plot points align with historical sources. For example, portrayals of impoverished veterans working as manual laborers, struggling with language barriers, and entering difficult inter-ethnic marriages with local women match first-hand accounts in *Taiwan Veteran Oral History* and memoirs. The only exception is the waishengren police officer in *Apple's Taste* who cannot speak any Minnan, which the author confirms is a deliberate satirical exaggeration by the director, as most working-age waishengren learned basic Minnan after decades in Taiwan.
2.  **Second-generation waishengren portrayals align with memoir accounts**: 88% of second-generation character traits (multi-lingualism, gang involvement, identity confusion, rebellion against parental authority) match accounts in *Colonel's Son* and *Wandering Family*, memoirs written by second-generation waishengren who grew up in juancun in the 1960s and 1970s.
3.  **Diversity of waishengren experience**: The sample films show waishengren across all social classes: 46% of characters are working-class (veterans, laborers, dance hostesses), 38% are middle-class (small business owners, police officers, low-ranking civil servants), and 0% are upper-class elite (high-ranking KMT officials, politicians, business tycoons). This directly counters the stereotype of all waishengren being privileged, but also reveals a limitation: TNC did not portray upper-class waishengren due to pre-1987 martial law censorship.
### Spatial Analysis Findings
1.  **Juancun space**: 100% of juancun scenes in the sample are associated with first-generation characters and "return to roots" identity, while 91% of scenes showing second-generation youth in juancun frame them as wanting to escape to the city, matching historical accounts of juancun youth's desire to leave closed, insular community life.
2.  **Urban Taipei space**: 87% of urban scenes are associated with second-generation characters, identity confusion, and exposure to global US/Japanese culture, matching the 1980s Taiwanese context of rapid economic growth and globalization.
3.  **Campus space**: 100% of campus scenes include elements of state assimilation policy (Mandarin-only rules, "recover mainland" propaganda), and 79% include second-generation youth rebelling against authority (cheating on exams, fighting, skipping class), matching historical accounts of 1980s Taiwanese student unrest before martial law was lifted.
### Identity Findings
There is a clear, statistically significant intergenerational split in identity orientation: 100% of first-generation waishengren characters in the sample hold a "return to mainland roots" identity, while 80% of second-generation characters hold a "take root in Taiwan" identity, with the remaining 20% expressing no clear sense of belonging. This aligns directly with Corcuff's 2004 sociological study of waishengren identity shifts across generations.

## 6.3. Robustness Checks (Equivalent to Ablation Studies for Qualitative Research)
The author verifies the robustness of her findings by cross-referencing every key finding with at least three independent historical sources. For example, the finding that most waishengren veterans struggled with marriage is validated by three separate memoirs, one oral history collection, and one official census study, confirming it is not an isolated or exaggerated portrayal. The only exaggerated portrayal (the monolingual waishengren police officer) is explicitly identified as satirical by the film's director, so it does not undermine the overall validity of the findings.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper is the first focused systematic study of waishengren representation in 1982-1987 Taiwan New Cinema. It finds that TNC broke radically from prior sanitized propaganda portrayals of the group, presenting a diverse, realistic picture of waishengren lived experience that aligns closely with historical records. The study documents a clear intergenerational identity split: first-generation waishengren overwhelmingly held a "return to mainland roots" identity, while second-generation waishengren identified with Taiwan as their home, though with significant identity anxiety. It also demonstrates that TNC used spatial symbolism (juancun, urban, and campus spaces) to visually represent identity struggles, and that the films largely counter the mainstream stereotype of waishengren as a homogeneous, privileged elite. The paper also notes that TNC's failure to portray upper-class waishengren elites was a limitation imposed by pre-1987 martial law censorship, a gap that was only addressed in post-TNC films after martial law was lifted.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations Identified by the Author
1.  **Incomplete Sample**: Three TNC waishengren films were unavailable for analysis, so the sample does not cover 100% of TNC waishengren content.
2.  **Limited Time Frame**: The study only covers the 1982-1987 TNC period, and does not analyze pre-TNC or post-TNC waishengren portrayals in depth.
3.  **No Audience Reception Analysis**: The study only analyzes the text of the films themselves, and does not examine how audiences received these films, or how they shaped public perceptions of waishengren.
### Suggested Future Work
1.  Expand the study to cover waishengren representation across the entire history of Taiwanese cinema, from 1949 to the present, to trace long-term shifts in portrayal.
2.  Conduct audience reception studies, including interviews with Taiwanese viewers who watched these films in the 1980s, to understand how they shaped public perceptions of waishengren and cross-group understanding.
3.  Compare waishengren representation in Taiwanese film to representation in Hong Kong and mainland Chinese films of the same period, to provide a broader regional context.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper makes a highly valuable contribution to cross-strait cultural understanding, as it provides a nuanced, humanizing picture of waishengren experience that counters stereotypes on both sides of the Taiwan Strait: it pushes back against stereotypes in Taiwan that frame all waishengren as privileged KMT elites, and against stereotypes in mainland China that frame all waishengren as eager to reunify. The intergenerational identity split finding is particularly important, as it explains much of the political divergence in Taiwan across age groups.

One unaddressed limitation of the study is that almost all the TNC directors analyzed are second-generation waishengren themselves, so their portrayals may be biased towards their own lived experience (primarily juancun military family experience). They may underrepresent the experience of non-military waishengren, or the perspectives of local benshengren on waishengren. Another potential improvement would be to include analysis of how these films were received in mainland China, and how they shaped mainland perceptions of waishengren experience in Taiwan.

Overall, the paper's focus on film as a site of collective memory construction is highly relevant to contemporary cross-strait relations, as media continues to be a core force shaping perceptions of identity and history on both sides of the strait. The study demonstrates that realistic, humanizing media portrayals of marginalized groups can be a powerful tool for reducing polarization and improving cross-group understanding.
