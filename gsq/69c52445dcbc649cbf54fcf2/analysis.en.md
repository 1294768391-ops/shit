# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The paper is titled *Study of Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films* (Chinese: 侯孝贤电影研究). Its central topic is a systematic, holistic analysis of the full body of artistic films by leading Taiwanese director Hou Hsiao-hsien, covering both his thematic concerns and unique aesthetic style, and situating his work within the context of Taiwanese social/historical transformation and global art cinema.
## 1.2. Authors
The sole author is Zhao Qianlin, a Master’s student in the Department of Art, Wuhan University, majoring in Drama and Traditional Chinese Opera (research focus: Chinese Film). The thesis was supervised by Associate Professor Huang Xianwen, a scholar of 20th-century Chinese film history.
## 1.3. Publication Venue
This is an unpublished Master’s degree thesis submitted to Wuhan University in 2004, not formally published in a journal or conference. It is a foundational early Chinese-language systematic study of Hou Hsiao-hsien, widely referenced in subsequent Chinese scholarship on Taiwanese cinema.
## 1.4. Publication Year
2004
## 1.5. Abstract
The thesis has two core research objectives: 1) To analyze the evolution of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s film themes from a cultural perspective, linking his personal creative expression to Taiwan’s collective social and historical transformation; 2) To deconstruct Hou’s unique formal aesthetic style from a film art perspective, and define the characteristics of "Hou-style aesthetics". Its core methodology is dual dimensional analysis: it divides Hou’s 11 artistic films into three thematic phases (early youth memory, mid-term historical reflection, late urban experience), and breaks down his style into six core formal elements (non-dramatic narrative, long take, tonal lighting design, life-like performance with recurring cast, recurring symbolic motifs, and layered sound design). Its key conclusion is that Hou is a globally recognized master of art cinema, who fused Western auteur film and realist traditions with traditional Chinese aesthetic principles, created a uniquely Chinese auteur film style, and was the leading figure of the Taiwan New Cinema Movement who first brought Taiwanese film to global attention.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
Prior to this 2004 thesis, research on Hou Hsiao-hsien was almost exclusively fragmented: Taiwanese critics (such as Jiao Xiongping and Zhu Tianwen, Hou’s long-time screenwriter) focused on single-film critiques of his most popular works (e.g., *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, *City of Sadness*), while early mainland Chinese scholars focused only on surface-level comparisons of his long take style to other realist directors. No existing work provided a systematic, holistic analysis of Hou’s entire artistic oeuvre, integrated both thematic/cultural and formal/aesthetic dimensions, or linked his work to the full timeline of Taiwan’s post-WWII social transformation. This gap was the core motivation for the thesis: to provide a comprehensive framework for understanding Hou’s work as a coherent, evolving body of "auteur" (authorial) film, rather than isolated individual works.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
The thesis makes three key contributions:
1.  It provides the first phase-based categorization of Hou’s artistic film themes, showing that his three thematic phases closely mirror Taiwan’s social transformation: early youth-focused films reflect 1960s-70s rural-to-urban transition, mid historical trilogy reflects post-colonial identity and ethnic conflict after 1945, and late urban films reflect 1990s-2000s rapid urbanization and value collapse.
2.  It systematically deconstructs "Hou-style aesthetics" as a unique fusion of Western realist film traditions and classical Chinese aesthetic principles (such as poetic lyricism, blank space, and harmony between human and nature), breaking down six core formal elements of his style with concrete film examples.
3.  It positions Hou in the broader context of Chinese-language and global cinema, comparing him to contemporaneous peers (mainland Fifth Generation director Zhang Yimou, fellow Taiwan New Cinema director Edward Yang, post-New Cinema director Tsai Ming-liang) to highlight his unique focus on local Taiwanese grassroots experience and warm humanistic observation.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
To understand this thesis, beginners need to master the following core film studies and cultural concepts:
### 3.1.1. Auteur (Film Author) Theory
Originating in 1950s French film criticism (advocated by André Bazin and Alexandre Astruc), this theory argues that a director is the "author" of a film, with consistent, recognizable thematic and stylistic signatures across their entire body of work, even within the constraints of a studio film system. Astruc’s "camera-pen" concept states that directors should use the camera as personally as a writer uses a pen, to express their unique worldview. This is the core theoretical foundation of the thesis, which treats Hou as a model "film author".
### 3.1.2. Taiwan New Cinema Movement
A wave of innovative, realistic Taiwanese film production active from 1982 to 1987, led by young directors including Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, and Te-sheng Wei. It rejected the previous dominant modes of escapist commercial romance, kung fu, and government propaganda film, instead focusing on personal, semi-autobiographical stories, realistic depictions of Taiwanese daily life, and critical reflection on social issues. Hou is widely considered the movement’s leading figure.
### 3.1.3. Long Take (Long Shot)
A continuous, unedited shot that lasts far longer than the average 2-3 second shot in mainstream Hollywood film, typically 30 seconds or more. It preserves the full temporal and spatial continuity of an action or scene, and is associated with realist film aesthetics, as it avoids manipulating the audience’s perception via editing. It is the most famous formal element of Hou’s style.
### 3.1.4. Non-dramatic Narrative
A narrative style that rejects the classic Hollywood narrative model: the linear cause-effect plot chain, clear central conflict, and closed, satisfying resolution. Instead, non-dramatic narrative uses fragmented, slice-of-life segments, downplays external conflict, focuses on character emotion and atmosphere, and often uses open endings. This is the core narrative feature of Hou’s films.
### 3.1.5. Key Classical Chinese Aesthetic Concepts
The thesis links Hou’s style to these traditional Chinese art principles:
- *Qiyun shengdong* (rhythmic vitality): The core goal of Chinese art, to convey the living, breathing spirit of the subject, not just its external form.
- *Liubai* (blank space): Leaving empty space in a painting or literary work to allow the audience’s imagination to fill in gaps, rather than explicitly showing every detail.
- *Fa xian nong yu jian gu, ji zhi wei yu dan bo*: Conveying rich, complex meaning and emotion through simple, plain, understated expression, rather than explicit, exaggerated performance.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The thesis identifies two main strands of prior Hou Hsiao-hsien research:
1.  **Taiwanese critical work (1980s onwards):** Almost exclusively single-film essays, focused on Hou’s most popular works. Representative work includes Jiao Xiongping’s analyses of *A Summer at Grandpa's* and *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, and Zhu Tianwen’s *Thirteen Questions about City of Sadness*, which provides behind-the-scenes context for the film’s production. These works focus on individual film content, not the evolution of Hou’s oeuvre.
2.  **Mainland Chinese research (mid-1990s onwards):** Early work by scholars such as Guo Xiaolu focused only on comparing Hou’s long take style to Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu, without analyzing his thematic concerns or connection to Taiwanese history. No work prior to this thesis covered his full filmography.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Taiwanese film development leading to Hou’s career:
1.  1950s-1960s: Dominated by Taiwanese-dialect commercial films and government-supported "Healthy Realism" films, which promoted traditional family values and avoided social criticism.
2.  1970s: Commercial film quality declined sharply, with escapist romance and kung fu films dominating the market, losing audiences to Hong Kong New Wave films.
3.  1982-1987: Taiwan New Cinema Movement, with Hou as its leading figure, bringing realistic, socially conscious film to Taiwanese audiences.
4.  Post-1987: After the New Cinema Movement dissolved, Hou, Edward Yang, Ang Lee, and Tsai Ming-liang led the next wave of Taiwanese art film, which gained global critical recognition.
    This thesis covers Hou’s artistic film output from the start of the New Cinema Movement (1983) to 2004, when the thesis was written.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior fragmented studies, this thesis has three core innovations:
1.  It is the first work to analyze Hou’s entire artistic film oeuvre as a coherent, evolving body of work, rather than isolated individual films.
2.  It integrates both cultural thematic analysis (linking Hou’s themes to Taiwan’s social transformation) and formal aesthetic analysis (deconstructing his unique style), rather than focusing only on one dimension.
3.  It connects Hou’s style to traditional Chinese aesthetic principles, rather than only interpreting his work through Western film theory frameworks, providing a culturally rooted analysis of his unique style.

# 4. Methodology
This is a qualitative film studies thesis, using two core analytical methods: historical-cultural thematic analysis, and formal film style analysis.
## 4.1. Principles
The core analytical principle is dual dimensional, integrated analysis:
1.  **Cultural thematic analysis:** Trace the evolution of Hou’s film themes across his career, and map these themes to parallel shifts in Taiwan’s post-WWII social, historical, and cultural transformation, showing how Hou’s personal semi-autobiographical expression reflects broader collective Taiwanese experience.
2.  **Formal stylistic analysis:** Deconstruct the unique formal elements of Hou’s film language, connecting each element to both Western realist film traditions (Italian Neorealism, Bazinian long take theory) and traditional Chinese aesthetic principles, to define the unique characteristics of "Hou-style aesthetics".
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
### 4.2.1. Thematic Phase Categorization
The thesis divides Hou’s 11 artistic films (excluding his three early 1980-1982 commercial romance films) into three coherent thematic phases, each with three sub-themes:
1.  **Phase 1: Personalized youth complex (1983-1986, 4 films: *The Boys from Fengkuei*, *A Summer at Grandpa's*, *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*, *Dust in the Wind*)
    These semi-autobiographical films draw from Hou’s own childhood and youth, as well as the life experiences of his collaborators Zhu Tianwen and Wu Nien-jen, set in 1960s-70s rural Taiwan. Their sub-themes are:
    - Youth rebellion and growing disorientation: Young rural migrants to cities face culture shock, lost ideals, and the pain of first love.
    - Family failure and emotional redemption: Traditional family structures break down under economic pressure, and young people learn to reconcile with their families after experiencing hardship.
    - Value of life and revelation of death: The unexpected death of family members becomes the core catalyst for young protagonists’ maturation.
2.  **Phase 2: Localized historical vision (1989-1995, 3 films: *City of Sadness*, *The Puppetmaster*, *Good Men, Good Women*, called the "Sadness Trilogy")
    These films cover 100 years of Taiwanese history, from Japanese colonization (1895-1945) to the 1950s White Terror period. Their sub-themes are:
    - Reflection on national positioning: Examination of the identity confusion of Taiwanese people after 50 years of Japanese colonization, who were forced to shift between Japanese and Chinese national identities.
    - Introspection on ethnic integration: Reflection on the conflict between local Taiwanese people and mainland Chinese migrants who relocated to Taiwan with the KMT government in 1949, particularly the violence of the 1947 228 Incident.
    - Wandering personal identity: Depiction of how ordinary individuals’ lives are uprooted and destroyed by large-scale historical and political changes, with no control over their own fate.
3.  **Phase 3: Alienated urban experience (1987, 1996-2001, 3 films: *Daughter of the Nile*, *Goodbye South, Goodbye*, *Millennium Mambo*)
    These films focus on 1990s-2000s urban Taiwan, after rapid industrialization and urbanization have eroded traditional rural values. Their sub-themes are:
    - Loss of rural Taiwan and evolution of female consciousness: Depiction of the disappearance of traditional rural life, and the rise of independent, rebellious modern urban female characters, a break from the traditional obedient female characters in Hou’s early films.
    - Degeneration of family, dissolute life and lonely fate: Traditional extended family structures break down entirely, and urban youth live rootless, dissolute lives with no stable family or social support, facing profound alienation.
    - Entanglement of past, present and future: Use of non-linear, overlapping timelines to show how the past haunts the present, and modern urban people have no clear sense of future direction.
### 4.2.2. Stylistic Element Deconstruction
The thesis breaks down "Hou-style aesthetics" into six interrelated core formal elements, with concrete film examples:
1.  **Non-dramatic narrative: Poetic lyricism**
    Hou rejects the classic Hollywood linear cause-effect plot, instead using fragmented, isolated "slice of life" segments, like Chinese ink wash plum painting that only shows one branch of a plum tree, leaving blank space for the audience’s imagination. He deliberately downplays external dramatic conflict (e.g., the death of a family member in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* is shown in a single, understated long take with no emotional dialogue, no close-ups of crying characters), focusing instead on internal emotional tension and atmospheric tone, leading many Western critics to describe him as a "film poet" rather than a storyteller.
2.  **Long take: Calm observation rooted in Chinese aesthetics**
    Hou uses three types of long take, all designed to preserve temporal and spatial continuity, and avoid manipulating the audience’s emotion via editing:
    - Deep-focus long take: Uses deep depth of field to show multiple layers of action in a single frame, with foreground, mid-ground, and background actions all visible at the same time, increasing the amount of realistic detail in the frame. For example, in *The Puppetmaster*, a single long take shows the protagonist building a coffin for his dead son in the foreground, his young son watching confusedly in the mid-ground, and his elderly father sitting silently smoking in the background, showing different age groups’ attitudes to death without any dialogue.
    - Fixed extreme long shot: Places human characters as small figures within vast natural landscapes (mountains, fields, ocean), conveying the harmony between human and nature, and the insignificance of individual suffering against the larger natural world. For example, the ending of *Dust in the Wind* uses an extreme long shot of the protagonist standing in a green sweet potato field talking to his grandfather, emphasizing his calm acceptance of his failed first love.
    - Moving long take: Uses slow horizontal panning, similar to viewing a traditional Chinese handscroll painting, which unfolds gradually over time, preserving the flow of time. For example, the opening of *Dust in the Wind* uses a slow horizontal pan of a mountain forest, establishing the film’s nostalgic rural tone.
3.  **Tone: Emotionalized light processing**
    Hou uses light and color tone to convey emotion, rather than for purely visual appeal:
    - Plain natural light for early rural films: Uses bright, natural summer sunlight for childhood scenes, and dim, gray rainy light for scenes of loss and grief, matching the real lighting conditions of rural Taiwan.
    - Contrast of cold and warm tones for mid historical and late urban films: Uses cold blue tones for scenes of gang violence, drug use, and urban alienation, and warm yellow tones for intimate family and emotional scenes, creating visual contrast to reflect internal emotional conflict.
    - Ambiguous warm yellow tones for period films: Uses dim, yellow, oil-lamp-like lighting for *The Puppetmaster* and *Flowers of Shanghai*, creating a dreamlike, nostalgic, old-fashioned atmosphere that feels like a faded memory.
4.  **Recurring cast and life-like performance: Nostalgic authentic recording**
    Hou deliberately uses non-professional actors and a small set of recurring "regular" cast members, who play similar roles across multiple films, to achieve natural, unscripted, life-like performance:
    - Li Tianlu (a famous Taiwanese glove puppetry artist) plays the grandfather figure in almost all of Hou’s early and mid films, with almost no scripted lines, relying on his own natural life experience for performance.
    - Mei Fang plays the traditional mother figure in multiple films, embodying the virtue and resilience of traditional Taiwanese women.
    - Tony Leung Chiu-wai plays introverted, quiet male leads, and Gao Jie plays rebellious, roguish male leads, for Hou’s mid and late films.
      Hou also extracts performance details directly from real life: he spent months observing young people in Taipei pubs before filming *Millennium Mambo*, to ensure the actors’ behavior matched real urban youth life.
5.  **Recurring motifs: Personalized authorial signature**
    Hou uses the same symbolic objects and events across his films, as recognizable authorial signatures, similar to recurring themes in a writer’s literary work:
    - Train: The most common motif, representing transition between rural and urban areas, the passage of time, nostalgia for the past, and the inevitability of life changes.
    - Power outage: Sudden, unplanned power cuts appear in multiple films, representing unexpected, irreversible life changes, and the instability of Taiwan’s social development in the 1960s-70s, when power supply was inconsistent.
    - Movie-watching: Characters watching films in theaters or at open-air screenings appear in multiple films, a self-reflexive nod to Hou’s own love of film, and a marker of youth coming of age.
    - Military enlistment notice: All young male protagonists receive a military enlistment notice, which is a mandatory coming-of-age threshold for Taiwanese men, and a turning point that disrupts their relationships and life plans.
    - Window framing: Hou often frames indoor scenes through doorways or windows, making the scene look like a stage, where characters are trapped in their own life situations, and the audience observes them from a calm, distanced perspective, without interfering.
6.  **Sound: A factor that increases the charm of the film**
    Hou’s films have very little dialogue, so sound design plays an unusually important role in conveying emotion and context:
    - Multi-layered synchronous ambient sound: Prioritizes natural ambient sound (train noise, bird calls, wind, market chatter) over dialogue, to create a realistic, immersive sense of place. For example, the opening of *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* uses bird calls, distant market chatter, and the grandmother calling the protagonist’s name to establish the rural small-town setting immediately, without any exposition.
    - Voiceover to externalize psychology: Uses calm, understated voiceover to convey characters’ internal thoughts and memories, rather than using explicit dialogue. For *Millennium Mambo*, he uses a voiceover from a narrator 10 years in the future, describing the protagonist’s youth in the third person, creating a sense of nostalgic distance from the events shown on screen.
    - Music: Uses simple, understated original scores (e.g., Chen Mingzhang’s solo guitar score for *Dust in the Wind*) and diegetic period songs (Japanese folk songs in *City of Sadness*, pop songs in *Millennium Mambo*) to enhance emotion, without using manipulative background music to force the audience to feel a certain way.

## 5.2. Evaluation Criteria (Adapted from "Evaluation Metrics")
The thesis uses four qualitative evaluation criteria to analyze Hou’s work:
1.  **Thematic coherence:** The consistency of thematic concerns across Hou’s oeuvre, and the degree to which his themes align with broader Taiwanese social and historical experience.
2.  **Stylistic distinctiveness:** The uniqueness of Hou’s formal film language, compared to mainstream Hollywood film and other Taiwanese directors of the same era.
3.  **Aesthetic continuity:** The degree to which Hou’s style aligns with core principles of traditional Chinese art and aesthetics, rather than only borrowing from Western film traditions.
4.  **Historical contribution:** The impact of Hou’s work on the development of Taiwanese cinema, and its reception in global art film circuits.
## 5.3. Comparative Baselines (Adapted from "Baselines")
The thesis compares Hou’s work to four representative comparative baselines to highlight his unique characteristics:
1.  **Classic Hollywood narrative film:** To highlight the innovation of Hou’s non-dramatic, open narrative style, which rejects the classic Hollywood cause-effect plot and closed resolution.
2.  **Pre-1980s Taiwanese commercial/propaganda film:** To show how Hou broke from the escapist, non-critical tradition of earlier Taiwanese film, and introduced realistic, socially conscious content.
3.  **Contemporaneous Chinese-language directors:**
    - Mainland Fifth Generation director Zhang Yimou: To contrast Zhang’s focus on grand national narratives and symbolic visual metaphors, with Hou’s focus on small daily details of ordinary people’s lives.
    - Fellow Taiwan New Cinema director Edward Yang: To contrast Yang’s rational, critical analysis of urban social structures, with Hou’s emotional, nostalgic focus on rural and grassroots Taiwanese experience.
    - Post-New Cinema Taiwanese director Tsai Ming-liang: To contrast Tsai’s extremely minimalist, alienated style, with Hou’s warmer humanistic care for his characters.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Thematic Findings Analysis
The thesis finds that Hou’s three thematic phases are not arbitrary creative choices, but closely mirror Taiwan’s social transformation:
1.  Early youth films correspond to Taiwan’s 1960s-70s rapid industrialization, when large numbers of young people migrated from rural areas to cities, traditional rural family structures broke down, and an entire generation experienced lost childhood and disorientation during the transition to urban life. Hou’s semi-autobiographical depictions of this experience resonated widely with Taiwanese audiences who lived through this period.
2.  Mid historical trilogy corresponds to Taiwan’s 1980s lifting of martial law, which allowed for the first public discussion of previously taboo historical topics, such as Japanese colonization, the 228 Incident, and the White Terror period. Hou’s films were the first popular cultural works to address these topics, sparking widespread public discussion about Taiwanese post-colonial identity and ethnic relations between local Taiwanese and mainland migrants.
3.  Late urban films correspond to Taiwan’s 1990s-2000s full urbanization, when traditional rural values were almost entirely eroded, young urban people faced rootlessness and alienation, and women gained greater social and economic independence. Hou’s depiction of these changes was one of the first realistic portrayals of modern urban Taiwanese youth life in film.
## 6.2. Stylistic Findings Analysis
The thesis finds that "Hou-style aesthetics" is a unique fusion of Western and Chinese aesthetic traditions:
- Hou’s use of long take and non-professional actors draws directly from Italian Neorealism and Bazinian realist film theory, which prioritize authenticity and temporal/spatial continuity.
- However, his use of long take is not identical to Western realist directors: he uses fixed extreme long shots to place characters within natural landscapes, and slow horizontal panning that mimics Chinese handscroll painting, to create a sense of poetic harmony between human and nature, which is rooted in traditional Chinese aesthetic principles, not Western realism.
- His non-dramatic narrative, use of blank space, and understated emotion all align with core traditional Chinese literary and artistic principles, rather than Western narrative traditions.
## 6.3. Comparative Analysis Results
The thesis’s comparative analysis finds that Hou has a unique position among Chinese-language directors:
- Compared to Zhang Yimou, who focuses on abstract cultural metaphors and dramatic conflict to appeal to global audiences, Hou focuses on specific, authentic details of daily life, which makes his work more deeply rooted in local Taiwanese experience, even as it gains global recognition.
- Compared to Edward Yang and Tsai Ming-liang, whose work is often critical and alienated, Hou’s work retains a warm, compassionate attitude towards his characters, even when depicting their suffering and hardship, which makes his work more emotionally resonant for general audiences.
## 6.4. Historical Contribution Findings
The thesis confirms Hou’s status as a foundational figure in Taiwanese and global Chinese-language cinema:
- He was the leader of the Taiwan New Cinema Movement, which transformed Taiwanese film from escapist commercial fare to a serious art form that addresses social and historical issues.
- He was the first Chinese-language director to win the Venice Film Festival Golden Lion (for *City of Sadness* in 1989), bringing Taiwanese film to global art cinema attention for the first time, and laying the foundation for subsequent Taiwanese directors’ success in global film circuits.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This 2004 thesis provides the first systematic, holistic analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s entire artistic film oeuvre, filling a major gap in prior fragmented research. Its core conclusions are:
1.  Hou’s film themes evolve in parallel with Taiwan’s post-WWII social transformation, from early personal youth memory, to mid historical reflection on post-colonial identity and ethnic conflict, to late depiction of urban alienation, making his work a valuable cinematic record of Taiwan’s collective memory.
2.  Hou’s unique "Hou-style aesthetics" is a fusion of Western realist film traditions and traditional Chinese aesthetic principles, creating a uniquely Chinese auteur film style that prioritizes calm, compassionate observation of ordinary people’s daily lives, understated emotion, and poetic atmosphere.
3.  Hou is a globally recognized master of art cinema, whose work not only transformed Taiwanese film, but also created a universal, humanistic film language that transcends local cultural context, and has influenced generations of Chinese-language directors.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The thesis has two core limitations, which point to directions for future research:
1.  **Temporal limitation:** It was written in 2004, so it does not cover Hou’s later works produced after 2001: *Three Times* (2005), *Flight of the Red Balloon* (2007), and *The Assassin* (2015, which won the Best Director award at the Cannes Film Festival). These later works further expand Hou’s style, including transnational production and wuxia (martial arts) genre experimentation, which are not addressed in this thesis.
2.  **Scope limitation:** It focuses almost exclusively on thematic and formal analysis, with very little discussion of Hou’s core creative team (long-time screenwriter Zhu Tianwen, cinematographer Mark Lee Ping-bing, editor Liao Ching-sung, sound designer Tu Duu-chih), whose collaboration was critical to the creation of his style, as Hou himself has repeatedly emphasized in interviews. It also does not address the industrial context of Hou’s film production, audience reception, or transnational circulation.
    Future research could:
- Integrate Hou’s post-2001 works into the phase-based thematic framework.
- Analyze the collaborative creative process of Hou’s core production team, to better understand the origins of his style.
- Conduct comparative studies of Hou’s work with other East Asian art film directors (such as Japanese director Hirokazu Kore-eda, South Korean director Hong Sang-soo), who share his focus on daily life and family dynamics.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This thesis is a foundational work for Chinese-language Hou Hsiao-hsien studies, and its phase-based thematic and stylistic framework is still widely used in subsequent research. Its greatest strength is its culturally rooted analysis, which avoids the common pitfall of interpreting Hou’s work only through Western film theory, and instead links his style to traditional Chinese aesthetics, providing a more accurate understanding of his unique artistic identity.
However, the thesis has two notable weaknesses:
1.  It overstates the coherence of Hou’s oeuvre, understating the significant breaks and experiments between phases: for example, *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998) is a period film set in 19th-century Shanghai brothels, with no connection to Taiwanese history or contemporary urban life, which the thesis mentions only briefly in the stylistic analysis, and does not integrate into its thematic phase framework.
2.  It idealizes Hou’s authorial status, following the auteur theory’s focus on the director as the sole creator of the film, and ignoring the major contributions of his core creative team, particularly screenwriter Zhu Tianwen, who co-wrote almost all of Hou’s artistic films, and shaped both his thematic concerns and narrative style.
    Despite these limitations, this thesis remains an excellent entry point for beginners to understand Hou’s work, the development of the Taiwan New Cinema Movement, and the connection between film and social/historical transformation. It provides a clear, comprehensive framework that makes Hou’s often slow, understated films accessible to new audiences.
