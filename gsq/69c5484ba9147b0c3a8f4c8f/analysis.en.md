## 1.5. Abstract
This paper takes Hou Hsiao-hsien's complete film works as its research object, uses classic film narratology as its core theoretical framework, and adopts a combination of text close reading and audio-visual analysis methods. # 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Be Solved
Hou Hsiao-hsien is a flagship figure of the Taiwan New Cinema Movement and one of the most internationally influential Chinese-language film directors. His works have unique narrative techniques and aesthetic styles, but prior academic research on his works mostly focuses on overall style interpretation, cultural value analysis, and comparative studies with other directors. There is a clear research gap: few studies systematically deconstruct his narrative techniques from a professional film narratology perspective, and reveal the internal logic between his narrative choices and aesthetic pursuit.
### Significance of the Problem
Studying Hou's narrative art is of great value for understanding the development path of Chinese-language art films, exploring the construction of oriental film language that is different from the Hollywood narrative system, and summarizing the creative experience of art film directors.
### Research Entry Point
This paper takes the classic film narratology research system (narrative style, narrative time-space, narrative strategy) as its entry point, combines the analysis of traditional Chinese aesthetic thought (artistic conception, imagery theory), and conducts a full coverage analysis of Hou's 19 representative works across his 40-year creative career.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1. It systematically summarizes the poetic narrative style of Hou's films for the first time, including the poetic application of long lenses and depth of field lenses, and the construction mechanism of oriental artistic conception and symbolic imagery.
2. It reveals the rules of Hou's narrative time and space construction: he breaks the linear time logic of real life through anachronism and time ellipsis, and builds narrative space closely combined with theme expression and character personality, so that space itself becomes a carrier of narrative meaning rather than just a background.
3. It sorts out Hou's unique narrative strategy system: he innovatively uses prose fragment structure and aggregated block narrative structure to replace the classic Hollywood causal linear structure, and flexibly switches between internal focalization and zero focalization to balance subjective emotional expression and objective realistic presentation.
### Key Conclusions
Hou's narrative art is a perfect integration of modern film narrative techniques and traditional Chinese aesthetic thought. It forms an oriental poetic narrative system that is both realistic and lyrical, which not only has unique aesthetic value, but also provides important practical reference for the creation of Chinese-language art films.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
This section provides all the basic concepts and background knowledge required for beginners to understand the paper.
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
### Film Narratology
Film narratology is a branch of narratology that studies the narrative rules and techniques of film works. It evolved from literary narratology in the 1980s, combining the characteristics of film as an audio-visual medium. It focuses on two core questions: 1) What story does the film tell? 2) How does the film tell this story through audio-visual language?
### Narrative Style
The overall stable aesthetic characteristic of narrative presentation formed by a director's consistent use of specific narrative techniques in his works, which is an important symbol of a director's personal style.
### Narrative Time-Space
The time and space constructed in the film, which is different from the linear, irreversible real time and three-dimensional real space. It is the time and space reorganized by the director according to the needs of storytelling:
- Narrative time: The time sequence, duration, and frequency of events presented in the film, which can be freely adjusted by the director (e.g., flashback, slow motion, time ellipsis).
- Narrative space: The scene space where the events in the film take place, which is carefully selected and designed by the director to serve the theme expression.
### Narrative Strategy
The specific techniques and methods used by the director to arrange the story, including narrative structure, narrative focalization, plot arrangement, etc.
### Core Lens Concepts
- **Long lens**: A shot that lasts for a relatively long time (usually more than 10 seconds) without cutting. It can present the complete process of events, has a strong sense of realism, and can avoid the subjective guidance of the director caused by frequent cutting.
- **Depth of field lens**: A lens that controls the clear range of the picture:
  - Large depth of field: The foreground, middle ground, and background of the picture are all clear, which can enhance the sense of spatial depth and present the environment and characters at the same time.
  - Small depth of field: Only the main subject is clear, and the foreground or background is blurred, which can highlight the main subject and create a framing effect.
### Narrative Focalization
Proposed by French literary theorist Gerard Genette, it refers to the perspective from which the story is told, divided into three categories:
1. **Zero focalization (omniscient perspective)**: The narrator knows all information, including the inner thoughts of all characters, and can freely switch between different spaces and times, like a "God's eye".
2. **Internal focalization**: The narrator only knows the information that a specific character knows, and the story is told from the perspective of this character.
3. **External focalization**: The narrator only knows the external behavior of the characters, and cannot access their inner thoughts.
### Taiwan New Cinema Movement
A film innovation movement that broke out in Taiwan in the early 1980s, represented by Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, etc. Its core characteristics are: focusing on the realistic presentation of Taiwan's local history and the daily life of ordinary people, reflecting the social changes and people's survival state in Taiwan's modernization process, with strong humanistic care and realistic spirit.
## 3.2. Previous Works
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien can be divided into three categories:
1. **Film style research**: Representative works include Li Xiang's *Confucian Dream Life: The Authorial Characteristics of Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films* (2006), which analyzes Hou's authorial characteristics and national aesthetic attributes from the perspective of his growth experience and audio-visual language; Meng Hongfeng's *On Hou Hsiao-hsien's Style* (1993), which summarizes his style as having oriental artistic conception and strong psychological tension.
2. **Cultural value research**: Representative works include Ni Zhen's *The Asian Significance of Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films* (2006), which discusses his Asian vision and nostalgia complex reflected in his historical narrative; Zhou Xing's research on the "localness" of his films, which points out that his works have a life sense rooted in Taiwan's local culture.
3. **Comparative research**: Most studies compare Hou with Japanese director Yasujiro Ozu and Chinese mainland director Jia Zhangke. For example, Chen Xuguang's *Comparison of Hou Hsiao-hsien and Jia Zhangke's Long Lenses* (2013) reveals the different aesthetic concepts behind their long lens use under different cultural backgrounds.
   In addition, there are authoritative monographs on Hou's works, including James Udden's *No Man is an Island: The Cinema of Hou Hsiao-hsien* (2014) and Michael Berry's *Boiling the Sea: Hou Hsiao-hsien's Memories of Cinema* (2015).
The core gap in prior research is that few studies systematically analyze Hou's works from the perspective of professional film narratology, which is exactly the problem this paper solves.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Film narratology developed from literary narratology in the 1980s, represented by scholars such as Francois Jost (France) and David Bordwell (USA), who introduced literary narrative theory into film research and established a complete research system for film narrative techniques.
Research on Hou Hsiao-hsien started in the early 1990s after he won the Golden Lion Award at the Venice Film Festival for *A City of Sadness*. Early research focused on overall style and cultural value interpretation, and gradually developed to detailed technical analysis of his audio-visual language and narrative techniques after 2000. This paper is a representative output of this trend of refined technical research.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior research, the core innovations of this paper are:
1. It adopts a systematic narratology framework for the first time, deconstructs Hou's narrative art from three interrelated dimensions (narrative style, narrative time-space, narrative strategy), making the analysis more systematic and logical.
2. It combines the analysis of traditional Chinese aesthetic thought, reveals the deep integration of Chinese traditional aesthetics (artistic conception, imagery) and modern film techniques in Hou's works, rather than just analyzing narrative techniques in isolation.
3. It covers all stages of Hou's 40-year creative career, rather than only analyzing his representative works in a certain period, so the conclusion is more universal and persuasive.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The core research idea of this paper is: take the classic film narratology research system as the basic framework, combine the close reading of the audio-visual text of Hou's representative works and the theoretical analysis of traditional Chinese aesthetics, sort out the composition of his unique narrative system, reveal the internal logic between his narrative technique choices and his aesthetic pursuit of oriental poetic expression.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The paper's research is divided into three core modules, which are analyzed layer by layer:
### Module 1: Analysis of Poetic Narrative Style
This module focuses on how Hou constructs a poetic narrative style through lens language and atmosphere construction, which is the most prominent external characteristic of his works.
#### Poetic Exploration of Lenses
1. **Slow long lens**
   Unlike the long lenses used in other films for realistic recording, Hou's long lenses are endowed with poetic expression functions:
- They slow down the narrative rhythm, guide the audience to pay attention to the details of daily life and the subtle emotional changes of characters, rather than being attracted by fast-paced plot conflicts.
- Typical case: In *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998), there are only 39 shots in the 110-minute film, almost one shot per scene. The opening 8-minute long lens completely presents the scene of guests and courtesans drinking and chatting in the brothel, creating a slow, nostalgic and closed atmosphere of the late Qing concession period.
- Typical case: In *The Assassin* (2015), Hou uses long lenses to shoot martial arts scenes, abandoning the fast cutting and special effects commonly used in traditional martial arts films. He focuses on presenting the inner conflict of the protagonist Nie Yinniang between "killing" and "not killing", creating a poetic martial arts aesthetic.
2. **Aesthetic depth of field lens**
   Hou's use of depth of field lenses is closely combined with traditional Chinese garden and painting aesthetics:
- **Large depth of field**: He makes the foreground, middle ground and background of the picture clear at the same time, integrates the natural environment and character fate, and creates a sense of spatial depth like Chinese landscape painting.

#### Poetic Construction of Atmosphere
Hou's atmosphere construction follows the traditional Chinese aesthetic principle of "integration of emotion and scenery":
1. **Artistic conception construction**
   Artistic conception is a core category of Chinese traditional aesthetics, referring to the aesthetic realm formed by the integration of the objective scenery described in the work and the subjective emotion of the creator. Hou uses a large number of natural empty shots (shots without characters, only natural scenery) to integrate emotion into scenery and create a corresponding atmosphere:
- Typical case: In *A City of Sadness*, after the progressive youth sing *Songhua River* (a song reflecting the pain of people in the northeast occupied by Japan), the camera cuts to the empty shot of continuous mountains covered by sea fog, which sets off the tragic and helpless mood of the characters, and creates a bleak artistic conception of the era under the "February 28 Incident".
- Typical case: In *Dust in the Wind*, after A Yuan's motorcycle for delivery is stolen, he squats alone by the sea in the rain, the dark sky and rolling waves set off his frustration and helplessness in urban life.
2. **Imagery presentation**
   Imagery refers to the artistic image formed by the integration of the objective object and the creator's subjective emotion. Hou uses specific recurring objects as imagery to carry implicit emotional connotations:
- **Train imagery**: It is a symbol of the connection between rural and urban areas, and the carrier of youth's dream pursuit and spiritual return. For example, in *Dust in the Wind*, the train takes A Yuan and A Yun from the mining town to Taipei to pursue their urban dreams, and takes A Yuan back to his hometown after he is frustrated in the city and his girlfriend leaves him.
- **Letter imagery**: It is a carrier of emotional communication between characters in different time and space, reflecting the implicit expression of emotion in oriental culture. For example, in *Summer at Grandpa's*, Dongdong's weekly letters to his hospitalized mother not only convey his daily life in the countryside, but also implicitly show the strict but deep love of his grandfather.
- **Jade Jue imagery**: In *The Assassin*, jade Jue (a jade ornament with a gap) has the symbolic meaning of "resolution" and "separation": it represents Princess Jiacheng's resolution to guard Weibo and maintain the stability between the imperial court and the vassal state, and also represents the broken engagement and separate life paths of Nie Yinniang and Tian Ji'an.

### Module 2: Analysis of Narrative Time and Space
This module focuses on how Hou breaks the limitations of real time and space, and reconstructs narrative time and space that serve the theme expression.
#### Reshaping of Narrative Time
Hou breaks the linear, irreversible characteristics of real time through two techniques:
1. **Anachronism**
   He uses flashback (inserting past events into the current narrative) and flashforward to break the linear narrative logic, which is more in line with the real state of human memory (fragmented, non-linear):
- Typical case: In *The Boys from Fengkuei*, there are three flashbacks of the protagonist A Qing's memories of his father, which are inserted into the current narrative of his life in Kaohsiung. These flashbacks do not promote the plot, but show A Qing's hidden nostalgia and respect for his father.
- Typical case: In *A City of Sadness*, the story of the third son Lin Wenliang is told in flashback: the film first shows his insane, violent state after being released from prison, then traces back to his experience of being framed as a traitor, tortured in prison, and mentally broken. This narrative method creates suspense and highlights the destruction of ordinary people by the chaotic historical era.
2. **Time ellipsis**
   He compresses the long actual story time (the time that the story actually spans in the setting) into the limited film screening time by omitting trivial, non-critical life fragments:
- Typical case: *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* (Hou's autobiographical work) spans nearly 20 years of the protagonist's growth from childhood to youth. Hou takes the death of the protagonist's father, mother and grandmother as the transition nodes, omits all trivial daily life fragments between these events, and completes the presentation of the protagonist's growth process through simple scene transitions.
- Typical case: *The Puppetmaster* (the biographical film of Taiwanese glove puppetry master Li Tianlu) spans 35 years from 1910 to 1945. Hou only selects key life events (his miserable childhood, entry into the puppetry troupe, being a son-in-law, performing for the Japanese army, the death of his father-in-law and son) to construct the story, omitting all irrelevant trivial events.
#### Construction of Narrative Space
Hou's narrative space is not just a passive background for character activities, but a carrier of narrative meaning, which is closely combined with the theme and character personality. He mainly constructs two types of space:
1. **Family space**
   The form of family space changes with the theme of the film:
- In his early youth growth-themed works, the family space is bright, open, and uses natural light, creating a warm and casual atmosphere. For example, the house by the sea in *The Boys from Fengkuei*, the traditional Japanese-style wooden house in *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*.
- In the "Taiwan Trilogy" (focusing on the tragic historical theme), the family space is closed, narrow, and uses dim, cold light, setting off the tragic and depressed atmosphere of the era. For example, the closed, dark Lin family residence in *A City of Sadness*.
- In *Flowers of Shanghai*, the color tone of each courtesan's room (a quasi-family space in the brothel) corresponds to her personality and fate: Shen Xiaohong's room is dark red, corresponding to her passionate, impulsive and dangerous character; Zhou Shuangzhu's room is dark and stable, corresponding to her calm, sophisticated and mature character; Huang Cuifeng's room is bright yellow and luxuriously decorated, corresponding to her shrewd, capable and arrogant character.
2. **Market space**
   Market space refers to the public street and market space where ordinary people live. Hou uses different market space characteristics to show the contradiction between urban and rural areas and the survival state of characters:
- Rural market space: The streets are long and narrow, the buildings are low and simple, with strong idyllic life atmosphere, reflecting the slow, peaceful life rhythm of the countryside. For example, the tree-lined country streets in *Summer at Grandpa's*, the winding mountain roads in the mining town in *Dust in the Wind*.
- Urban market space: The streets are crowded, narrow, and full of neon signs and fast-moving vehicles, reflecting the confusion, pressure and blurred atmosphere of urban life. For example, the crowded, neon-lit streets of Taipei in *Millennium Mambo*, the noisy, chaotic urban streets in *Goodbye South, Goodbye*.

### Module 3: Analysis of Narrative Strategies
This module focuses on Hou's unique narrative structure and narrative focalization choices, which are the core of his narrative system that is different from mainstream commercial films.
#### Innovative Narrative Structure
Hou abandons the classic Hollywood causal linear narrative structure (with clear beginning, development, climax, ending, and strong dramatic conflict), and innovatively uses two non-linear narrative structures:
1. **Prose fragment structure**
   He selects a series of representative, meaningful daily life fragments to connect into the film, there is no strong dramatic conflict between the fragments, and the connection between them relies on the internal emotional logic, similar to the structure of prose. This structure focuses on presenting the real texture of daily life, leaving sufficient space for the audience to think and feel.
- Typical case: *Summer at Grandpa's* selects fragments of Dongdong and his sister playing in the countryside, their acquaintance with the crazy woman Han Zi, the conflict between the grandfather and the uncle, etc. There is no intense plot conflict, but it shows the innocence of children, the kindness of human nature, and the helplessness of life in plain daily fragments.
- Typical case: *A Time to Live, A Time to Die* selects fragments of the protagonist's childhood mischief, youth rebellion, and the death of his father, mother and grandmother. It shows the protagonist's growth process and the sadness of life and death in plain life details, without deliberate sensationalism.
2. **Aggregated block structure**
   The whole film has no central plot running through, it consists of several independent story blocks, these blocks are not connected by causal logic, but are connected by a common theme or emotional tone.
- Typical case: *Three Times* consists of three independent love stories set in 1966 ("Love Dream"), 1911 ("Freedom Dream") and 2005 ("Youth Dream"). The three stories have no plot connection, but are connected by the common theme of "the best time in love", showing the different appearances of love in different eras.
- Typical case: *Flowers of Shanghai* consists of three independent story blocks of three groups of courtesans and their guests: Shen Xiaohong and Wang Liansheng, Zhou Shuangzhu and Master Hong, Huang Cuifeng and Master Luo. The three stories are parallel and independent, but together show the warmth and coldness of human relations in the late Qing brothel.
#### Flexible Narrative Focalization
Hou flexibly uses two types of narrative focalization according to the needs of narrative expression:
1. **Limited internal focalization**
   The narrative is limited to the perspective of a specific character, only showing what this character sees, hears and feels. It is divided into two types:
- **Off-screen "I" focalization**: The narrator appears as an off-screen voiceover, and does not appear in the picture. Typical case: *The Puppetmaster* uses the off-screen voiceover of the old Li Tianlu (the prototype of the protagonist) to tell his own life experience, leading the audience to understand his bumpy life and inner feelings.
- **On-screen "I" focalization**: The narrator is both the teller of the story and a character in the film, and the narrative is carried out from his/her perspective. Typical case: *Good Men, Good Women* uses the monologue of the protagonist Liang Jing to show her inner pain and longing for her dead lover, allowing the audience to directly enter her inner world.
2. **Unlimited zero focalization**
   Also known as the omniscient perspective, the camera acts as an implicit "God's eye" narrator, which can freely switch between different characters, spaces and times, and master all information. It is divided into two types:
- **Presentation type zero focalization**: The film only presents the picture objectively, without any narration or subtitle intervention. Typical case: *Goodbye South, Goodbye* uses this focalization, the camera objectively records the life of marginalized people in southern Taiwan, without subjective evaluation, leaving the right of judgment to the audience.
- **Image-subtitle type zero focalization**: In addition to the objective picture, the director adds subtitles to supplement information that the characters do not know, such as historical background, the inner thoughts of mute characters. Typical case: *A City of Sadness* adds historical background subtitles at the beginning and end to introduce the historical background of Japan's surrender and the Nationalist government's relocation to Taiwan; it also uses subtitles to present the content of the written notes used by the deaf and dumb protagonist Lin Wenqing to communicate, making up for the limitation that he cannot speak.

# 5. Research Design
Since this is a humanities film studies paper, there are no natural science-style experiments, datasets or evaluation metrics. This section introduces the research corpus, methods and comparative reference framework used in the paper.
## 5.1. Research Corpus (Film Selection)
The paper covers all 19 films directed by Hou Hsiao-hsien from his official debut in 1980 to 2015, covering his five creative stages:

| Creative Stage | Time Range | Core Theme | Representative Works |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Early commercial period | 1980-1982 | Urban romantic comedy | *Cute Girl*, *Cheerful Wind*, *Green, Green Grass of Home* |

The paper selects 12 most representative works from these stages for in-depth text close reading, to ensure the universality of the conclusion.
## 5.2. Research Methods
The paper adopts two core research methods:
1. **Text close reading**: The author conducts frame-by-frame analysis of the selected representative works, focusing on the details of lens language, scene scheduling, plot arrangement, and character setting, to extract the common rules of Hou's narrative techniques.
2. **Theoretical analysis**: The author combines three theoretical systems for analysis: classic film narratology theory (Gerard Genette's narrative time and focalization theory), traditional Chinese aesthetic theory (artistic conception, imagery theory), and Taiwan New Cinema research theory, to explain the internal logic of Hou's narrative choices.
## 5.3. Comparative Reference Framework
The paper takes the classic Hollywood narrative system as the reference baseline for comparative analysis:
- The classic Hollywood narrative uses causal linear structure, strong dramatic conflict, fast editing rhythm, and zero focalization to guide the audience's attention and emotion, with the core goal of improving viewing pleasure.
- Hou's narrative system abandons strong dramatic conflict, uses non-linear structure, slow rhythm, and flexible focalization, with the core goal of presenting the real texture of life and expressing oriental poetic aesthetics.
  Through this comparison, the paper highlights the innovation and uniqueness of Hou's narrative art.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
Through systematic analysis of Hou's works, the paper verifies three core conclusions:
1. **The poetic nature of Hou's narrative style is not accidental, but the result of deliberate technical choice**: His use of long lenses, depth of field lenses, artistic conception construction and imagery presentation is not only for formal beauty, but also to integrate traditional Chinese aesthetic thought into film narrative, creating an oriental film aesthetic that is different from the western realistic aesthetic.
2. **Hou's time and space construction rules serve his core aesthetic pursuit**: His anachronism and time ellipsis break the linear narrative logic of commercial films, creating a fragmented feeling that is consistent with the state of human memory, making the film have a stronger sense of authenticity of personal experience. His narrative space is closely combined with theme and character, so that space itself becomes a carrier of narrative meaning, rather than a passive background.
3. **Hou's narrative strategy achieves a balance between realism and lyricism**: His prose fragment structure and aggregated block structure abandon the artificial dramatic conflict of commercial films, focusing on the presentation of real daily life, which has a strong realistic attribute. His flexible use of internal focalization and zero focalization balances the subjective emotional expression of the director and the objective presentation of reality, making the film both lyrical and realistic.

## 6.3. Comparative Advantage Analysis
Compared with the classic Hollywood narrative system, Hou's narrative system has three unique advantages:
1. It has a stronger sense of life reality: It abandons the artificial plot design of commercial films, and presents the real texture of daily life, which is easier to resonate with the audience's real life experience.
2. It has a more unique oriental aesthetic charm: It integrates traditional Chinese aesthetic thought into film narrative, creating a unique poetic aesthetic that cannot be replicated by western narrative systems.
3. It leaves more space for audience thinking: It does not guide the audience's emotion through strong dramatic conflict and subjective editing, leaving sufficient space for the audience to understand and interpret the work independently.

## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The paper does not explicitly state its limitations, but we can summarize the deficiencies and possible future research directions based on its content:
### Limitations
1. The paper focuses on the analysis of narrative techniques, and the discussion of the interaction between Hou's narrative art and the specific social and cultural context of Taiwan in different periods is not sufficient.
