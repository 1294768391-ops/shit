# 1. Bibliographic Information
## 1.1. Title
The full title is *History · Landscape · Character: Hou Hsiao-hsien's Film Review*. Its central topic is a comprehensive, systematic analysis of all 19 feature films directed by Hou Hsiao-hsien (core figure of Taiwan New Cinema) through three interlocking analytical lenses: landscape presentation, character portrayal, and historical writing.
## 1.2. Authors
- Primary author: Lu Jiaxin, master's graduate in Drama and Film Studies, Hunan Normal University, China.
- Supervisor: Professor Yue Kaihua, a leading scholar in Chinese film studies and modern Chinese cultural studies at Hunan Normal University.
## 1.3. Publication Venue
This is an unpublished master's degree thesis submitted to Hunan Normal University, a key provincial public university in central China with a top-tier humanities and film studies program.
## 1.4. Publication Year
June 2019.
## 1.5. Abstract
This thesis takes Hou Hsiao-hsien, one of the most influential filmmakers in contemporary Chinese and global art cinema, as its research object. It systematically deconstructs his films from three dimensions: 1) landscape presentation (including long-take aesthetics, overlapping natural and human landscapes, and integration of realism and lyricism); 2) character portrayal (including autobiographical figures, traditional cultural symbols represented by puppet master Li Tianlu, and evolving female characters); 3) historical writing (including local consciousness, daily narrative, and restoration of major historical events from a grassroots perspective). It concludes that Hou's works challenge top-down grand historical narratives, record half a century of social changes in Taiwan, and express consistent humanistic care for ordinary people in turbulent times.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
Existing research on Hou Hsiao-hsien tends to focus on single dimensions of his work (e.g., only his long-take aesthetic, only his historical themes, or only his character construction), and lacks a unified, integrated framework that connects his formal aesthetic choices, character design, and underlying historical consciousness. Prior studies also often limit their scope to his works during the Taiwan New Cinema period (1982-1987), ignoring the evolution of his style in his later 20+ year career.
### Importance of the Problem
Hou Hsiao-hsien is not only the leading figure of Taiwan New Cinema, but also one of the few Chinese directors to win top awards at all three major European international film festivals (Venice Golden Lion, Cannes Best Director, Locarno Lifetime Achievement Award). His works are core texts for understanding Taiwan's social history, local cultural identity, and the development of Chinese-language art cinema. The lack of a comprehensive, multi-dimensional study creates gaps in understanding the internal logic of his creative system.
### Innovative Entry Point
The thesis proposes a tripartite analytical framework that treats landscape, character, and history as interconnected, mutually influencing components of Hou's film system, rather than isolated research objects. It covers all 19 feature films he directed between 1980 and 2015, spanning his entire creative career.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1. **Framework innovation**: It constructs the first unified analytical framework for Hou's films that integrates aesthetic form, character expression, and historical narrative, clarifying the internal logical connection between the three dimensions: landscape is the carrier of narrative and historical memory, characters are the perspective for observing history, and historical consciousness is the core connotation running through all his works.
2. **Aesthetic interpretation innovation**: It reveals that Hou's use of landscape (long takes, overlapping natural and human landscapes) is not merely a formal aesthetic choice, but a conscious tool to present grassroots folk memories and challenge official grand historical narratives. It also corrects the prior misconception that Hou's long-take aesthetic is entirely derived from Western realist film theory, pointing out it comes more from his practical filming constraints and traditional Chinese aesthetic influence.
3. **Character research expansion**: It finds that Hou's character system provides alternative perspectives for historical narration: autobiographical characters connect personal growth with collective generational memory; puppet master Li Tianlu serves as a living carrier of Taiwan's traditional culture and 20th century history; female characters represented by Kuanmei in *A City of Sadness* provide a non-linear, cyclical view of history that differs from male-dominated linear historical narratives.
4. **Historical writing clarification**: It summarizes that Hou's focus on historical themes comes from two core drivers: the influence of Chinese writer Shen Congwen's "cold, compassionate view of life and death" narrative perspective, and the collective pursuit of Taiwan New Cinema filmmakers to record local Taiwanese history and reality. His non-dramatic, daily life-centered historical writing restores the real life experience of ordinary people ignored by grand historical narratives.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core technical terms are explained below for beginners:
- **Taiwan New Cinema (1982-1987)**: A landmark film movement in Taiwan led by a new generation of directors including Hou Hsiao-hsien and Edward Yang. It abandoned the previous dominant commercial film model (Qiong Yao romantic dramas, formulaic kung fu films, official propaganda films) and turned to realistic presentation of Taiwan's local life, social changes, and personal memories, with strong humanistic care and experimental aesthetic styles.
- **Long take**: A continuous, unedited shot that lasts for an extended period (usually 30 seconds or longer, sometimes up to 10+ minutes in Hou's works). It preserves the real space-time continuity of events, reduces the director's excessive intervention in the narrative, and gives the audience more space for independent interpretation.
- **Deep focus cinematography**: A shooting technique that keeps objects in the foreground, middle ground, and background of the frame all in clear focus, allowing the audience to observe multiple layers of simultaneous action in the same shot.
- **Grand historical narrative**: A top-down narrative of history constructed by official authorities, which usually focuses on major political events, elite figures, and linear "progress" of history, and often erases or ignores the life experiences and memories of ordinary grassroots people.
- **Feminist film theory's "male gaze"**: A concept proposed by scholar Laura Mulvey, which points out that in most mainstream films, female characters are presented as objects of male viewing and desire, lacking independent subjectivity.
- **Julia Kristeva's "women's time"**: A theory that distinguishes between two types of time consciousness: dominant male/historical time is linear, goal-oriented, and focused on major events; while women's time is cyclical, commemorative, and connected to daily life, reproduction, and eternity.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The thesis summarizes four main strands of existing research on Hou Hsiao-hsien:
1. **Research on his Taiwan New Cinema period works**: Chen Qing points out that Hou's creation in this period returned to real life and the times, breaking away from the previous escapist romantic drama model. Liu Lixing and Shen Wenying note that Hou's unique contribution is integrating personal life experience with the fate of the larger era, which is the core feature of Taiwan New Cinema.
2. **Research on his formal and aesthetic style**: Yuan Yuqin argues that Hou's films integrate Chinese and foreign cultural influences, form a unique poetic image style with long takes, and create a unique artistic conception similar to traditional Chinese landscape painting. Cai Xiao points out that Hou's realism is different from Western realist film aesthetics, as it combines Taiwan's local lyric tradition and traditional Chinese philosophical concepts of "unity of man and nature".
3. **Research on landscape in his films**: Zhang Aizhu analyzes the dialectical relationship between sound and image, and the overlapping of natural and human landscapes in Hou's films, pointing out that these landscapes are carriers of the lived experience of Taiwan's multiple colonial periods (Japanese colonial rule, Kuomintang rule). Yan Ling finds that Hou maintains a delicate balance between narrative and landscape presentation in early works such as *A Summer at Grandpa's*.
4. **Research on his character images**: Zhang Qiong points out that Hou regards marginality as the true state of human life, and his marginal character images question and deconstruct the established social identity framework. Zhou Zhongmou finds that Hou gradually began to focus on marginal female characters as protagonists in the mid-1990s, presenting their independent personality and unique life experience.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
The development of Taiwan cinema can be divided into three phases, and this thesis's work is positioned at the end of this evolutionary timeline:
1. **Pre-1980s**: Dominated by commercial genre films (Qiong Yao romantic dramas, kung fu films) and official propaganda films, which rarely touched on real social issues or local Taiwanese history, and adopted a highly dramatic, audience-pleasing narrative model.
2. **1982-1987 (Taiwan New Cinema period)**: Young directors absorbed influences from European film movements (French New Wave, Italian Neorealism) and began to explore local realistic themes and innovative aesthetic forms, focusing on personal memories and social reality. Hou became the core representative of this movement.
3. **Post-1987**: After the end of the New Cinema movement, Hou's works further expanded to large-scale historical themes, and gradually integrated more experimental narrative techniques, becoming a world-renowned art film director with a unique personal style.
   This thesis is the first comprehensive study covering all phases of Hou's 40-year career, connecting the previously isolated dimensions of aesthetic, character, and historical research.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior research, the core innovations of this thesis are:
1. **Integrated framework**: It does not separate Hou's aesthetic techniques, character portrayal, and historical narrative, but treats them as an interconnected whole, clarifying how formal choices serve character and historical expression.
2. **Comprehensive coverage**: It analyzes all 19 of Hou's feature films from 1980 to 2015, including his early commercial works, New Cinema works, historical trilogy, and later cross-cultural/costume works, which is more comprehensive than previous studies that only focus on his works before 1995.
3. **Local perspective**: It avoids the eurocentric bias of some Western research on Hou, and explains his style from the perspective of Chinese traditional aesthetics and Taiwan's local social and historical context.

# 4. Methodology
This is a humanities film studies paper, so its methodology is based on text analysis and contextual interpretation, as detailed below:
## 4.1. Principles
The core research principle is "text + context": starting from close reading of Hou's film texts, combining relevant film theories and the specific historical background of Taiwan's social development, to systematically interpret the internal relationship between landscape, character, and history in his works. Its theoretical foundations include:
1. Guy Debord's *Society of the Spectacle* theory: Landscape in images is not only a visual presentation, but also a carrier of social consciousness and historical memory, which reflects the power relations of a specific society.
2. Andre Bazin's realist film theory: Long takes and deep focus cinematography can preserve the authenticity of reality to the greatest extent, avoiding the director's subjective manipulation of the narrative through editing.
3. Julia Kristeva's "women's time" theory: Female time consciousness provides an alternative perspective to the dominant linear male historical narrative.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
The thesis adopts three interlocking research methods, implemented in sequence:
### Step 1: Close text reading of all film works
The author conducts a frame-by-frame close reading of all 19 feature films directed by Hou Hsiao-hsien, sorting out:
- The length, composition, and narrative function of all long takes in each film;
- The types, presentation frequency, and symbolic meaning of natural and human landscapes;
- The personality traits, identity attributes, and narrative functions of all core characters;
- The historical events, narrative perspective, and presentation methods of historical themes in each film.
  For example, the author compares the average shot length of Hou's early commercial work *Cute Girl* (1980, average shot length ~8 seconds) and his later historical work *The Puppetmaster* (1993, average shot length ~72 seconds), finding that his editing became increasingly minimalist, which served his goal of presenting unfiltered real historical experience.
### Step 2: Literature and archival research
The author sorts out all existing research results on Hou Hsiao-hsien, including:
- Academic monographs, journal papers, and degree papers on Hou's works;
- First-hand interview materials with Hou and his core creative team (screenwriter Chu Tien-wen, editor Liao Ching-song, cinematographer Mark Lee Ping-bing, etc.);
- Historical documents related to the Taiwan New Cinema movement and major historical events in Taiwan (Japanese colonial rule, February 28 Incident, White Terror period, etc.).
  For example, the author cites Hou's own interview about the origin of his use of long takes: he initially used long takes because of limited filming equipment, low budgets, and the use of non-professional actors (who perform more naturally in unbroken long takes), and later gradually turned this practical choice into a conscious aesthetic style. This corrects the prior misconception that his long-take aesthetic was directly derived from Bazin's theoretical teachings.
### Step 3: Historical contextual analysis
The author places each of Hou's works in its specific historical background of Taiwan's social development, connecting the content of the film with the social reality and collective psychological state of the time, to reveal how Hou's films reflect and reflect on historical events from the perspective of ordinary people.
For example, when analyzing *A City of Sadness* (1989), the author combines the historical background of the February 28 Incident (1947, an anti-government uprising suppressed by the Kuomintang authorities, resulting in tens of thousands of deaths, and a long-term taboo topic in Taiwanese society until the late 1980s), pointing out that Hou's choice of a deaf-mute protagonist Lin Wenqing is not only an artistic choice, but also a metaphor for the long-term unspeakable trauma of this historical event in Taiwanese society.

# 5. Experimental Setup
As a humanities research paper, it does not have traditional scientific experiments, so its research materials, evaluation criteria, and analytical baselines are presented below:
## 5.1. Research Materials (Equivalent to Datasets)
The core "datasets" used in the thesis are:
1. **Complete filmography of Hou Hsiao-hsien**: All 19 feature films he directed between 1980 and 2015, covering all phases of his creative career (early commercial, New Cinema, historical trilogy, late cross-cultural/costume). This is the most complete research sample of Hou's works to date.
2. **First-hand archival materials**: Including original film scripts, production notes, interviews with Hou and his creative team, memoirs of Taiwan New Cinema filmmakers, and official historical documents of 20th century Taiwan.
## 5.2. Evaluation Criteria (Equivalent to Evaluation Metrics)
The thesis uses three core evaluation dimensions to analyze Hou's films, each with clear judgment standards:
### Dimension 1: Landscape presentation
1. **Long take application effectiveness**: Measured by whether the long take can preserve the real space-time continuity of events, provide sufficient narrative information, and give the audience independent interpretation space.
2. **Natural-human landscape integration**: Measured by whether the landscape in the film combines natural scenery and human life scenes, and reflects the specific social and historical background of the story.
3. **Realism-lyricism balance**: Measured by whether the film can maintain objective presentation of real life while conveying the director's subjective humanistic care and emotional expression, without excessive sensationalism or excessive coldness.
### Dimension 2: Character portrayal
1. **Authenticity**: Measured by whether the character's behavior and personality conform to the logic of real life, without being shaped as a symbolic tool for the director to express ideas.
2. **Marginality**: Measured by whether the character belongs to the group ignored by the grand historical narrative, and can present the real life experience of grassroots people.
3. **Historical bearing**: Measured by whether the character's fate is closely linked to the changes of the times, and can reflect the impact of major historical events on ordinary people.
### Dimension 3: Historical writing
1. **Local consciousness**: Measured by whether the film presents the real local life and cultural characteristics of Taiwan, rather than a generalized, imagined "Chinese society".
2. **Grassroots perspective**: Measured by whether the historical narrative is from the perspective of ordinary people, rather than the perspective of official authorities or elite figures.
3. **Historical authenticity**: Measured by whether the film can restore the real appearance of major historical events, rather than repeating the official grand narrative.
## 5.3. Analytical Baselines (Equivalent to Baselines)
The main baselines for comparison in the thesis are:
1. **Pre-1980s Taiwan commercial films**: Represented by Qiong Yao romantic dramas and formulaic kung fu films, which are divorced from real life, to highlight the innovation of Hou's realistic creative style.
2. **Other core Taiwan New Cinema directors**: Represented by Edward Yang, who focuses on urban middle-class life and rational, logical narrative, to highlight Hou's focus on grassroots rural life and lyrical, poetic realist style.
3. **Prior research conclusions on Hou**: Compare the author's analytical conclusions with existing research viewpoints, to correct misconceptions and fill research gaps.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The thesis draws three core conclusions through systematic analysis:
### Conclusion 1: Landscape in Hou's films is a carrier of historical memory, not just an aesthetic element
- **Long take aesthetic**: Hou's distant, static long takes create a "cold, compassionate observer" perspective, which not only respects the natural performance of non-professional actors, but also avoids guiding the audience's judgment through editing. For example, in *A City of Sadness*, the 7-minute long take in the "Little Shanghai" restaurant scene presents multiple layers of simultaneous action (diners eating, gangsters negotiating, staff working) in the same frame, intuitively reflecting the chaotic social state after Taiwan's retrocession. His long take aesthetic also influenced later Asian directors: Edward Yang's average shot length increased from 15 seconds in the 1980s to 50 seconds in the 1990s; Tsai Ming-liang's average shot length increased from 19 seconds in 1992 to over 1 minute after 2000.
- **Overlapping natural and human landscapes**: In *A City of Sadness*, 13 empty shots of Jiufen's mountain and sea landscapes appear repeatedly, which not only serve as narrative transitions, but also contrast the fragility of personal fate with the eternity of nature. The indoor landscape of the Lin family's dining table appears three times with the exact same composition, but the number of family members gradually decreases (from a full table of people to only the elderly, disabled, and children in the final shot), intuitively presenting the decline of the family in turbulent times.
- **Integration of realism and lyricism**: Hou's films adhere to objective presentation of real life, while integrating subjective lyricism through "blank space" techniques similar to traditional Chinese painting. For example, in *Dust in the Wind*, he never directly presents the plot of A-yun falling in love with the postman, only showing the postman appearing at the village occasionally, and then A-yun's wedding announcement arriving. This avoids excessive dramatic conflict, and gives the audience space to feel the helplessness of fate.
### Conclusion 2: Hou's character system provides an alternative perspective for historical narration
- **Autobiographical characters**: Most of Hou's works from the 1980s to 1990s are autobiographical, drawn from his own or his friends' life experiences. The "bad boy" image in *The Boys from Fengkuei* is derived from Hou's own teenage experience, reflecting the spiritual wandering of Taiwanese youth in the process of rapid urbanization. His "Taiwan Trilogy" (*A City of Sadness*, *The Puppetmaster*, *Good Men, Good Women*) integrates personal biography with the 20th century history of Taiwan, making abstract grand history concrete and perceptible through the fate of individual characters.
- **Puppet master image**: Li Tianlu, the real-life national treasure-level puppet master who appears in multiple Hou films, is not only a character, but also a symbol of traditional Taiwanese culture. His life experience spanning the Qing Dynasty, Japanese colonial rule, and Taiwan's retrocession is a living record of Taiwan's 20th century history. The puppet shows he performs also form a mutual reflection with the theme of "life is like a play" in the films.
- **Evolving female characters**: Hou's portrayal of female characters gradually shifted from supporting roles serving male characters to independent subjects. In *A City of Sadness*, Kuanmei's diary voice-over runs through the whole film, providing a cyclical, life-oriented view of history different from the linear male historical narrative of wars and political events. In his later works *Millennium Mambo* and *The Assassin*, female characters have complete independent subject consciousness, and their choices drive the development of the plot.
### Conclusion 3: Hou's historical writing breaks grand narratives and restores grassroots experience
- **Drivers of historical focus**: Hou's focus on history comes from two sources: 1) The influence of Chinese writer Shen Congwen, whose narrative perspective of observing life and death from a distance, without excessive joy or sorrow, allowed Hou to adopt an objective and tolerant attitude towards history. 2) The collective pursuit of Taiwan New Cinema filmmakers, who wanted to break the official control of historical narration and record the real local history of Taiwan.
- **Daily narrative method**: Hou avoids direct depiction of dramatic historical conflicts, and instead presents history through daily trivialities. For example, in *Good Men, Good Women*, he presents the history of the White Terror period through the daily life of the protagonists (cooking, eating, writing letters), rather than intense political conflicts or violent scenes. He also uses multi-layered flashback techniques to connect personal memory and collective history, making the presentation of history more three-dimensional.
- **Restoration of major historical events**: In restoring major events such as the February 28 Incident, Hou uses visual metaphors, audio-visual interleaving, and repeated images to present historical trauma from the grassroots perspective. For example, in *A City of Sadness*, the voice of the Japanese emperor's surrender announcement is interleaved with the cry of Lin Wenxiong's wife giving birth; the official speech of Chen Yi claiming to "maintain social stability" after the February 28 Incident is interleaved with scenes of people hiding in fear, forming a sharp contrast between official narrative and people's real experience.

## 6.3. Component Analysis
The thesis verifies that the three dimensions of landscape, character, and history are indispensable and mutually reinforcing:
- If the landscape dimension is analyzed in isolation without connecting to characters and history, Hou's long-take aesthetic is reduced to a purely formal technical choice, ignoring its historical and cultural connotations.
- If the character dimension is analyzed in isolation without connecting to landscape and historical background, the characters are reduced to simple personal images, ignoring their value as carriers of collective memory and historical reflection.
- If the historical writing dimension is analyzed in isolation without connecting to landscape and character, Hou's films are reduced to simple historical texts, ignoring their unique artistic value and aesthetic charm.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This thesis provides the first comprehensive, multi-dimensional analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien's entire 40-year creative career through the tripartite framework of landscape, character, and history. It clarifies that Hou's films are not only the most important artistic achievements of Taiwan New Cinema, but also a continuous visual record of Taiwan's social changes over half a century. His unique aesthetic system breaks the official grand historical narrative, presents the real life experience of ordinary grassroots people ignored by mainstream historical records, and expresses consistent humanistic care for people in turbulent times. His works have important artistic value, historical value, and cultural value, and have had a profound impact on the development of Asian and even global art cinema.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations of the thesis
1. **Imbalance in case selection**: Most of the analytical cases are concentrated on Hou's works before 2000, and the analysis of his later works (especially *The Assassin* 2015) is relatively superficial, lacking in-depth discussion of the evolution of his creative style in his later period.
2. **Lack of comparative research**: The thesis does not conduct comparative research between Hou and other East Asian art film directors (such as Yasujiro Ozu, Hirokazu Kore-eda, Jia Zhangke), which could further highlight the uniqueness of his style in the context of East Asian film culture.
3. **Insufficient research on cross-cultural acceptance**: The thesis rarely discusses how Hou's works with strong Chinese cultural characteristics are interpreted and accepted by Western audiences, and what misinterpretations may exist in cross-cultural communication.
### Future research directions
1. Expand the research scope to include Hou's works as a producer and his influence on young Taiwanese directors.
2. Conduct in-depth comparative research between Hou and other Chinese and international art film directors, to clarify his position in global film history.
3. Explore the cross-cultural communication mechanism of Hou's works, and analyze the differences in their interpretation in different cultural contexts.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
1. **Applicability of the framework**: The tripartite analytical framework of landscape, character, and history proposed in this thesis has strong universality, and can be used to analyze other art film directors who focus on realistic themes and historical reflection, such as Jia Zhangke (mainland China), Hirokazu Kore-eda (Japan), and Apichatpong Weerasethakul (Thailand).
2. **Correction of eurocentric bias**: The thesis's conclusion that Hou's long-take aesthetic is not entirely derived from Western film theory, but more from his own practical experience and traditional Chinese aesthetic influence, corrects the long-standing eurocentric bias in Western research on Hou, and provides a more accurate local perspective for understanding Chinese film aesthetics.
3. **Unaddressed controversial issues**: The thesis does not deeply discuss the controversy over Hou's historical views, especially the different evaluations of his historical narrative in Taiwan and mainland China, which involves the complex issue of cross-strait cultural identity. Future research can further explore this aspect to understand the political and cultural implications of his works more comprehensively.
4. **Shallow gender analysis**: The analysis of female characters in the thesis is relatively superficial, and can further combine more feminist film theories to conduct in-depth research on the gender politics in Hou's works, especially the changes in his portrayal of female characters in different creative periods.
