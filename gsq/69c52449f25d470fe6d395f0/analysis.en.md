# 1. Bibliographic Information

The full title of the paper is *Research on Artistic Style of Hou Hsiao-hsien's Movies* (Chinese title: 《侯孝贤电影的美学风格研究》). Its core topic is to conduct a systematic, full-oeuvre analysis of the aesthetic characteristics, underlying core, formative origins, and industry influence of the film works of Hou Hsiao-hsien, the leading representative of Taiwan New Cinema.

## 1.4. Publication Year
The thesis was submitted in May 2014 and finalized in June 2014.
## 1.5. Abstract
This paper takes 18 of Hou Hsiao-hsien's film works as its core research object. It first deconstructs Hou's unique aesthetic characteristics from four core film language dimensions: long take, empty shot, visual tone, and fixed camera position. It then identifies the constant aesthetic core of Hou's works as a pursuit of documentary aesthetics, which records the essence of individual life and Taiwan's social and historical changes through the medium of the lens. Next, it explores the triple origins of Hou's style: the internalization of traditional Eastern culture, his personal poetic temperament, and the profound influence of Bazin's documentary aesthetics. Finally, it systematically summarizes the multi-dimensional influence of Hou's aesthetic style on the development of Chinese cinema.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
Prior to 2014, mainland Chinese academic research on Hou Hsiao-hsien (a world-renowned director and core figure of Taiwan New Cinema) was highly fragmented. Most studies focused on single representative works (such as *A City of Sadness* or *The Time to Live and the Time to Die*) or isolated traits such as narrative structure or poetic expression, and lacked systematic, full-oeuvre analysis of his aesthetic system. This gap meant that the unique value of Hou's integration of traditional Chinese aesthetics and Western documentary thought was not fully explored from a mainland academic perspective.
### Importance of the Problem
Hou Hsiao-hsien is one of the few Chinese directors who has won widespread international recognition while retaining distinct local cultural characteristics. His exploration of the integration of Eastern aesthetics and modern film language provides an important reference for the development of Chinese local cinema that avoids blind imitation of Hollywood commercial models.
### Entry Point & Innovation
This paper takes aesthetic style as its core entry point, and conducts the first systematic, full-oeuvre combing of all 18 of Hou's works from an aesthetic perspective, filling the gap in mainland academic research on Hou's complete aesthetic system.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
1.  **Aesthetic feature system construction**: It systematically summarizes four core aesthetic characteristics of Hou's films, each corresponding to a unique traditional Chinese aesthetic connotation:
    - Long takes embody the harmonious beauty of "unity of man and nature"
    - Empty shots embody the poetic beauty of the interaction between `substance (shi)` and `void (xu)`
    - Natural, low-saturation visual tones embody the freehand beauty of Chinese ink wash painting
    - Fixed camera positions embody the simple beauty of unmodified real life
2.  **Constant core identification**: It identifies that the consistent aesthetic core of Hou's works across his entire career is the pursuit of documentary aesthetics, which records the essence of individual life, the trajectory of social change, and the suppressed collective memory of Taiwan from a civilian perspective.
3.  **Style origin clarification**: It clarifies the triple origins of Hou's unique aesthetic system:
    - Internalization and condensation of traditional Eastern culture (including traditional Chinese aesthetic thought, Hakka local culture, and folk art)
    - Personal poetic temperament, expressed through poetic design of light and sound
    - Deep influence of André Bazin's documentary aesthetics (long take and deep focus lens theory)
4.  **Influence evaluation**: It summarizes the four-fold influence of Hou's style on Chinese cinema:
    - Brings a "defect beauty" narrative style through ellipsis techniques
    - Enriches the diversified aesthetic expression of Chinese films
    - Introduces a grand aesthetic of historical narrative and collective memory
    - Establishes a creative orientation focused on discovering humanity and the beauty of human nature

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
For beginners to understand this paper, the following core concepts are explained:
- **Taiwan New Cinema**: A film movement that emerged in Taiwan in the early 1980s, represented by directors such as Hou Hsiao-hsien and Edward Yang. It broke away from the previous dominant commercial melodrama model, focused on realistic presentation of Taiwan's local society, people's daily lives, and suppressed historical memory, used a large number of non-professional actors, on-location shooting, long takes, and restrained narrative techniques, with strong humanistic care.
- **Long take (Long-take)**: A film shooting technique where a single shot lasts for an extended period (usually more than 30 seconds, often several minutes) without editing. It is used to present the complete process of an event, the real interaction between characters and their environment, maintain the integrity of time and space, and bring a strong sense of reality to the audience.
- **Empty shot**: A shot with no human characters in the frame, usually shooting natural scenery, objects, or the environment. It is used to render atmosphere, express unspoken emotions, create artistic conception, and transition between scenes. It is functionally similar to the "blank leaving" (`liubai`) technique in Chinese traditional painting.
- **Fixed camera position**: A shooting technique where the camera is placed in a fixed position without movement (no panning, tilting, tracking, etc.) during the entire shot. It can present the natural flow of life objectively and calmly, maintain the integrity of the space, and create a sense of distance that encourages the audience to think independently.
- **Bazin's documentary aesthetics**: Founded by French film theorist André Bazin (1918-1958), its core view is that the essence of film is to reproduce objective reality as it is. It advocates the use of long takes and deep focus lenses to maintain the integrity of the time and space of events, opposes the subjective editing of montage that artificially cuts reality, and gives the audience the right to actively interpret the content of the film.
## 3.2. Previous Works
### Taiwan Research
Taiwan academic circles began to study Hou Hsiao-hsien as early as the 1980s:
- Jiao Xiongping, a leading Taiwan film scholar, compiled *Taiwan New Cinema* in 1988, the first systematic collection of reviews of Hou's works, which linked Hou's films to Taiwan's social and historical context.
- In 2000, scholars Lin Wenqi, Shen Xiaoyin, and Li Zhenya compiled *Drama and Life: Hou Hsiao-hsien Film Research*, the first dedicated monograph on Hou's works, which remains a core reference in the field.
### Mainland Research Prior to This Paper
Mainland research on Hou began in the mid-1990s, and was divided into two main categories:
1.  **Humanistic research**: Scholars such as Li Daoxin (Peking University) pointed out that Hou's films are an identity symbol of Taiwan's internationalization, recording Taiwan's social transformation from agricultural to industrial civilization. Hu Ke (Communication University of China) regarded his films as representative of local cinema, recording historically sensitive events such as the February 28 Incident from a civilian perspective.
2.  **Technical research**: Scholar Chen Xuguang (Peking University) studied the use of long takes in Hou's films, comparing them with mainland director Jia Zhangke's long takes, pointing out that Hou's long takes have a unique sense of depth of Eastern aesthetics. Fan Zhizhong (Zhejiang University) analyzed the reasons for Hou's use of long takes, including the active choice of documentary style and passive adaptation to the limitations of non-professional actors.
    All prior studies lacked systematic aesthetic combing of Hou's entire oeuvre, focusing instead on single works or isolated traits.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Research on Hou Hsiao-hsien's films has gone through three clear stages:
1.  1980s-1990s: Taiwan-led initial research stage, focusing on the connection between Hou's works and the Taiwan New Cinema movement, and their social context.
2.  1990s-2000s: Mainland research began, focusing on single work analysis, and isolated studies of narrative or technical characteristics.
3.  After 2000: Research gradually deepened, but still lacked systematic, full-oeuvre aesthetic analysis.
    This paper is located at the third stage, filling the gap of systematic aesthetic research from the mainland perspective.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared with prior research, the core innovations of this paper are:
1.  It is the first mainland study to systematically comb all 18 of Hou's works from an aesthetic perspective, rather than analyzing single works or isolated technical characteristics.
2.  It explicitly connects Hou's film techniques with traditional Chinese aesthetic connotations, rather than treating techniques as isolated formal features.
3.  It systematically sorts out the triple origins of Hou's style, integrating the influence of traditional Eastern culture, personal temperament, and Western documentary aesthetics.
4.  It systematically summarizes the influence of Hou's style on Chinese cinema, providing actionable reference for the development of Chinese local films.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
This paper adopts a qualitative research design, combining **literature analysis** and **comprehensive comparative research** with close text analysis of specific films. Its core idea is to take aesthetic style as the entry point, systematically sort out the aesthetic characteristics, constant core, formative origins, and industry influence of Hou's films. Its theoretical basis includes traditional Chinese aesthetic theory, Bazin's documentary aesthetics, and prior research results on Hou's works.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The research is carried out in four progressive steps:
### Step 1: Deconstruction of Aesthetic Feature Elements
The paper selects the four most representative film language elements of Hou's works, and analyzes their aesthetic connotations combined with specific film cases:
1.  **Long take analysis**: Taking *The Boys from Fengkuei* (1983) as a core case, it analyzes how Hou uses long takes to capture the interaction between characters and their environment, express the integrity of emotions and artistic conception, and reflect the harmonious beauty of "unity of man and nature" influenced by Shen Congwen's objective, tolerant literary viewpoint. For example, the alley fight scene in *The Boys from Fengkuei* uses a long take shot from a distance: when the characters run out of the frame, the camera remains still, and passers-by glancing sideways hint that the fight is continuing, maintaining the completeness of the event and a sense of unmodified reality.

4.  **Fixed camera position analysis**: Taking *Dust in the Wind* (with only 5 moving shots in the entire film) and *A City of Sadness* as core cases, it analyzes how fixed camera positions objectively and calmly restore the real flow of life, showing the simple beauty of unmodified daily reality. For example, the scene in *A City of Sadness* where Lin Wenqing's prison mates are taken away to be shot uses a fixed camera: the calm, unmoving lens contrasts sharply with the turbulent political environment, highlighting the insignificance of ordinary people in the tide of the times.
### Step 2: Summarization of Constant Aesthetic Core
On the basis of feature analysis, the paper summarizes that the consistent core of Hou's aesthetic pursuit across his entire career is documentary aesthetics: he focuses on recording the essence of life, real existing marginalized individuals, and Taiwan's social and historical changes from a non-official civilian perspective. Taking *The Puppetmaster* (1993) and *A City of Sadness* as core cases, it analyzes how Hou uses the lens as a medium to record the real history of Taiwan under Japanese rule, the February 28 Incident, and other historically sensitive periods, breaking the single official historical narrative, and presenting the real life of ordinary people.

The paper explores the three sources of Hou's aesthetic style from multiple dimensions:
1.  **Traditional Eastern culture influence**: Hou internalizes traditional Chinese aesthetics (blank leaving, unity of man and nature) and Taiwan local culture (Hakka culture, folk opera) into his films. For example, the "braid cutting" scene in *The Puppetmaster* reflects the cultural changes in Taiwan under Japanese rule; clothing statistics in *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* show the simple, practical characteristics of traditional Hakka culture. 2.  **Poetic temperament and objective cultural expression**: Hou expresses cultural connotations through poetic design of light and sound. For example, in *The Puppetmaster*, he uses dim natural light from patios and roof glass to present the ancient, profound, and dense state of traditional Chinese culture. In sound design, he uses on-site synchronous recording, adjusts the volume according to the distance of the sound source, and uses asynchronous sound and picture to make the audience maintain a thinking distance from the film, and objectively interpret the historical content.
3.  **Bazin's documentary aesthetics influence**: Hou is a faithful practitioner of Bazin's theory. He uses long takes to maintain the integrity of time, deep focus lenses to maintain the integrity of space, gives the audience the right to interpret independently, and avoids the subjective cutting of reality by montage. The use of long takes is also adapted to his preference for non-professional actors, allowing them to perform naturally in a complete time and space, presenting a real, unmodified life state.
### Step 4: Influence Evaluation
The paper summarizes the influence of Hou's aesthetic style on Chinese cinema from four aspects, combined with the current situation of Chinese film creation, and puts forward the reference value of Hou's style for local Chinese film creation.

# 5. Experimental Setup
As this is a qualitative humanities research paper, the following framework corresponds to the natural science experimental setup:
## 5.1. Research Objects (Corresponding to Datasets)
The research objects are all 18 feature films directed by Hou Hsiao-hsien from 1980 to 2013, covering all stages of his creative career:
- Early commercial works: *Cute Girls* (1980), *Play While You Play* (1981), *The Green, Green Grass Of Home* (1982)
- Taiwan New Cinema representative works: *The Boys from Fengkuei* (1983), *The Sandwich Man* (1983), *A Summer At Grandpa's* (1984), *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* (1985), *Dust in the Wind* (1986), *Daughter Of The Nile* (1987)
- Taiwan Trilogy (historical works): *A City of Sadness* (1989), *The Puppetmaster* (1993), *Good Men Good Women* (1995)
- Later works: *Goodbye South Goodbye* (1996), *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998), *Millennium Mambo* (2001), *Coffee time* (2004), *The best time* (2005), etc.
  These works fully reflect the evolution and constant core of Hou's aesthetic style, and are highly representative for studying his complete aesthetic system.
## 5.2. Analytical Framework (Corresponding to Evaluation Metrics)
The paper adopts a multi-dimensional analytical framework constructed based on traditional Chinese aesthetic theory, film aesthetic theory, and Bazin's documentary aesthetics:
- 4-dimensional framework for aesthetic feature analysis: long take, empty shot, visual tone, fixed camera position
- 2-dimensional framework for constant aesthetic core analysis: documentary aesthetic pursuit, lens as documentary medium
- 3-dimensional framework for style origin analysis: traditional Eastern culture, poetic temperament, Bazin's documentary aesthetics
- 4-dimensional framework for influence evaluation
  This framework can systematically and comprehensively analyze Hou's aesthetic style.
## 5.3. Comparative Baselines (Corresponding to Baselines)
The comparative baselines for this study include:
1.  Traditional commercial film aesthetic system (focus on dramatic conflict, fast montage editing, high-key lighting, clear cause-effect narrative), used to highlight the uniqueness of Hou's aesthetic style
2.  Previous research results on Hou's films, used to highlight the innovation of this paper's systematic aesthetic combing
3.  Aesthetic styles of other Taiwan New Cinema directors (such as Edward Yang), used to highlight Hou's unique aesthetic characteristics

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
1.  The four aesthetic characteristics of Hou's films are not independent, but unified under the core of documentary aesthetics, and all integrated with traditional Chinese aesthetic thought: long takes, empty shots, natural tones, and fixed camera positions all serve to present the real state of life and history, and convey unique Eastern artistic conception.
2.  Compared with traditional commercial films, Hou's style abandons the dramatic narrative logic of "background-exposition-development-climax-ending", uses ellipsis techniques similar to Chinese painting blank leaving, gives the audience more space for imagination and independent interpretation, which is the biggest difference from traditional commercial film aesthetics.
3.  Compared with previous studies on Hou, this paper systematically connects his technical techniques with Eastern aesthetic connotations, rather than only analyzing technical features or narrative content, which deepens the understanding of the cultural core of Hou's films.
## 6.2. Case Validation Results
The paper validates all conclusions through a large number of specific film cases:
- The analysis of *The Puppetmaster* verifies that Hou's documentary aesthetic can present the real life of Taiwan under Japanese rule from the perspective of ordinary people, breaking the official single historical narrative
- The analysis of *Dust in the Wind* verifies that the combination of empty shots, fixed camera positions, and long takes can create a strong poetic atmosphere and express the subtle, unspoken emotions of characters
- The clothing statistics of *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* verify the reflection of traditional Hakka culture in Hou's films
## 6.3. Supplementary Discussion
The paper also discusses the sense of alienation between Hou's films and ordinary audiences caused by his unique techniques (such as ellipsis, fixed long takes, asynchronous sound and picture). It points out that this sense of alienation is deliberately created by Hou, which allows the audience to maintain an objective thinking distance, rather than being immersed in the plot like watching commercial films, so as to independently interpret the historical and humanistic connotations of the films.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper systematically sorts out the aesthetic system of Hou Hsiao-hsien's films for the first time from the perspective of mainland academic circles, summarizes four core aesthetic characteristics with Eastern cultural connotations, clarifies that the constant core of his creation is the pursuit of documentary aesthetics, explores the triple origins of his style combining Eastern and Western cultural influences, and evaluates his fourfold influence on Chinese cinema. The paper fills the gap of systematic aesthetic research on Hou's works in mainland China, and provides important reference for Chinese local film creation to integrate traditional Chinese aesthetics and documentary thought.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
### Limitations Pointed Out by the Author
1.  The paper focuses on the analysis of aesthetic characteristics and origins, and does not conduct in-depth comparative research with other Asian directors (such as Yasujiro Ozu, who also uses fixed camera positions and long takes) to further highlight the uniqueness of Hou's aesthetic style.
2.  The analysis of the influence on later young directors is relatively general, and lacks specific case studies of specific works of later directors.
### Suggested Future Work
1.  Conduct comparative research between Hou's aesthetic style and other Chinese and foreign directors with similar styles to explore the commonality and uniqueness of Eastern film aesthetics.
2.  Conduct in-depth case studies of the influence of Hou's style on later young directors (such as Jia Zhangke, Wei Te-sheng) to clarify the specific transmission path of his aesthetic thought.
3.  Expand the research to Hou's later works after 2014 (such as *The Assassin* released in 2015) to improve the completeness of the research.
## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
### Inspiration
The paper reveals that the unique value of Chinese local films lies in the integration of traditional Chinese aesthetic thought and modern film techniques, rather than blind imitation of Hollywood commercial film models. Hou's successful experience of integrating Eastern aesthetics such as blank leaving, unity of man and nature, and poetic artistic conception with Western documentary aesthetics provides an important reference for Chinese films to go global and form their own independent aesthetic system.
### Potential Areas for Improvement
1.  The paper can add more comparative analysis with other directors of the same period, such as comparing Hou's long takes with Edward Yang's long takes, to better highlight Hou's unique aesthetic characteristics.
2.  The paper can conduct more in-depth analysis of the audience acceptance of Hou's films, exploring how to balance the expression of artistic style and the audience's viewing experience, which has reference value for the development of current Chinese art films.
3.  The paper was completed in 2014, and does not include the analysis of *The Assassin* (2015), which is an important work of Hou's later style transformation, and subsequent research can supplement this part.
### Transferability
The research method of linking film language techniques with traditional national aesthetic connotations used in this paper can be transferred to the aesthetic style research of other Chinese local directors, such as Ang Lee, Wong Kar-wai, etc., which helps to systematically sort out the complete aesthetic system of Chinese films.
