## 1.5. Abstract
This paper argues that Hou Hsiao-hsien’s widely recognized "realist" film style is not a derivative of Western cinematic realism, but a uniquely Chinese form of realism shaped by his personal life experience, influence from Taiwanese nostalgia and lyrical literature, and traditional Taoist natural philosophy. It systematically deconstructs Hou’s realism at both the conceptual level (natural view, anti-dramatized narrative, free indirect discourse viewpoint construction) and formal technical level (natural performance, deep focus long takes, poetic editing for artistic conception), using case studies of Hou’s representative works including *The Time to Live and the Time to Die*, *A City of Sadness*, and *Flowers of Shanghai*. Finally, it applies Hou’s realist creative principles to the production of the author’s original short film *Farewell*, which depicts the life of an elderly man caring for his wife with Alzheimer’s disease.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
Hou Hsiao-hsien is a foundational figure of the 1980s Taiwan New Cinema movement, and his work is globally celebrated for its distinct realist aesthetic. However, prior academic research on Hou’s realism has two key gaps:
1. Most studies either frame Hou’s style as a derivative of Western realist film theory (such as Bazin’s long take theory, or Italian Neorealism) or focus only on formal technical features, neglecting the deep influence of traditional Chinese philosophical thought (especially Taoist natural views) on his creative logic.
2. There is very little research that translates Hou’s theoretical realist principles into actionable guidance for contemporary small-scale short film production.
   This paper addresses these gaps by centering traditional Chinese cultural roots in its analysis of Hou’s realism, and combining theoretical analysis with practical creative application.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
The paper’s primary contributions are:
1. It establishes the distinctly Chinese identity of Hou’s realism, demonstrating that it is rooted in Taoist philosophy of "Tao follows nature" (Dao fa zi ran), which prioritizes unforced, spontaneous depiction of life’s natural flow over the Western realist focus on social critique and strict objective documentation.
2. It provides a systematic, integrated deconstruction of Hou’s realist system, linking his core conceptual ideas to specific formal techniques, with detailed shot-by-shot analysis of representative scenes from his filmography.
3. It develops a practical framework for applying Hou’s realist principles to low-budget short film production, demonstrated via the author’s original short film *Farewell*, which adapts Hou’s anti-dramatized narrative, natural performance, and objective cinematography to a contemporary Chinese elderly care story.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
All core technical terms are defined below for beginner readers:
1. **Film Realism**: An approach to filmmaking that prioritizes authentic, unembellished depiction of everyday life, rejecting exaggerated dramatic effect, romanticized representation, and manipulative narrative or editing choices. Key Western realist traditions include Italian Neorealism (1940s-1950s, using location shooting, non-professional actors, and working-class stories) and André Bazin’s realist theory (advocating for long takes and deep focus to preserve the spatial and temporal integrity of reality, rather than fragmenting it via montage).
2. **Deep Focus Long Take**: A shot combination where:
   - A *long take* refers to a shot that runs for an extended duration (in Hou’s work, average shot length ranges from 43 seconds in *A City of Sadness* to over 100 seconds in later works) without cutting.
   - *Deep focus* is a cinematographic technique where all planes of the frame (foreground, midground, background) are in sharp focus simultaneously, allowing viewers to choose which part of the scene to attend to, rather than being guided by selective focus.
3. **Free Indirect Discourse (Film)**: Adapted from literary theory, this is a narrative technique where the perspective shifts seamlessly between the director’s objective, omniscient viewpoint and a character’s subjective viewpoint, without explicit markers (such as a clear point-of-view shot setup). It allows audiences to experience the character’s emotional state while maintaining the work’s overall objective tone.
4. **Anti-Dramatization (Narrative)**: A narrative strategy that rejects the classic Hollywood three-act structure (setup, central conflict, climax, resolution) and clear causal links between plot events. It instead prioritizes the depiction of mundane daily life, emotional atmosphere, and the natural flow of time.
## 3.2. Previous Works
Prior research on Hou Hsiao-hsien’s work referenced in the paper includes:
- **Taiwanese scholarship**: Jiao Xiongping’s early analysis of Hou’s work through the lens of traditional Chinese aesthetics; Lin Wenqi’s research on Hou’s "film poetics" (focused on sound, editing, and everyday object details); Shen Xiaoyin’s analysis of Hou’s long takes, landscape shots and distanciation narrative; Yang Yajun’s 2012 master’s thesis documenting the influence of writer Shen Congwen’s objective, detached narrative perspective on Hou’s creative style.
- **Mainland Chinese scholarship**: Meng Hongfeng’s analysis of Hou’s fixed long takes as a device to integrate human, scene and object in line with Chinese aesthetic principles; Li Xun’s deconstruction of Hou’s unique editing techniques (including eye-line matching, intentional axis jumps); Sun Weichuan’s comparison of Hou’s realism with Bazin’s photographic ontology theory; Dai Jinhua’s analysis of Hou’s "Taiwan Trilogy" as national allegories in the Cold War historical context.
- **International scholarship**: David Bordwell’s formal analysis of Hou’s staging style, comparing his long take technique to Japanese director Mizoguchi Kenji and Greek director Theodoros Angelopoulos; James Udden’s *No Man is an Island: The Cinema of Hou Hsiao-hsien*, a comprehensive analysis of Hou’s work across political, cultural and economic dimensions; Olivier Assayas’ 1997 documentary *HHH: A Portrait of Hou Hsiao-hsien*, which includes extensive interviews with Hou about his creative process.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Cinematic realism evolved from the Lumière brothers’ late 19th century actualités (short documentary clips of everyday life), to 1940s Italian Neorealism, to various national new wave movements of the 1960s-1980s. The 1980s Taiwan New Cinema movement (of which Hou is a leading figure) developed its own realist tradition focused on depicting local Taiwanese life and history, departing from both Hollywood commercial narrative conventions and Western realist frameworks. Prior to this paper, most research on Hou’s realism framed it either as a derivative of Western theory or as a purely formal aesthetic innovation, without sufficient focus on its roots in traditional Chinese philosophy.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
The core innovation of this paper compared to prior work is its rejection of Western-centric analytical frameworks for Hou’s work. Instead, it positions Hou’s realism as an independent, locally rooted cinematic system shaped by Taoist natural philosophy, which differs fundamentally from Western realism in its focus on emotional atmosphere and the natural flow of life, rather than social critique or strict objective documentation. Its combination of theoretical analysis and practical creative application also distinguishes it from prior purely academic studies of Hou’s work.

# 4. Methodology
The paper uses a two-part integrated methodology: theoretical deconstruction of Hou’s realist system, and practical application of its principles to short film creation.
## 4.1. Principles
The core underlying principle of the paper is that Hou’s realism cannot be fully understood through Western film theory alone. Its essence lies in the fusion of three elements: Hou’s personal life experience growing up in Fengshan, Taiwan; influence from Chinese literary traditions (Shen Congwen’s writing, Taiwanese nostalgia and lyrical literature); and traditional Taoist views of nature, which frame life, death and change as natural, spontaneous processes that should be depicted without forced dramatic intervention.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth
### 4.2.1. Theoretical Deconstruction Framework
The paper divides Hou’s realist system into two interconnected layers: conceptual core principles, and formal technical implementation.
1. **Conceptual Core of Hou’s Realism**
   a. **Natural View and Realism**
      The paper first explains the Taoist concept of "nature" (ziran), which refers to an unforced, spontaneous state free of human intervention, rather than the physical natural world. This is derived from Laozi’s core principle "Man follows the earth, earth follows heaven, heaven follows Tao, Tao follows nature". Hou’s narrative directly reflects this view: he prioritizes the natural logic of life over the dramatic logic of narrative. For example, in *Coffee Time* (2003), the protagonist’s pregnancy is never framed as a central dramatic conflict point, but is embedded in mundane, everyday activities (making coffee, taking trains, visiting friends) without explicit emphasis.
   b. **Anti-Dramatized Story Construction**
      Hou’s narrative explicitly rejects the classic Hollywood narrative model, which centers on character desire as the core causal driver, builds tension via conflict, and resolves with a clear ending. Instead, Hou uses a fragmentary, diary-like structure, omitting clear causal links between scenes, and focusing on emotional atmosphere and character demeanor rather than plot progression. For example, in *Flowers of Shanghai* (1998), all dramatic events (relationship conflicts, breakups, acts of betrayal) occur entirely off-screen. The film only depicts the mundane, repetitive daily scenes of characters drinking, chatting, and smoking in the brothel, focusing on subtle changes in their expressions and demeanor. Hou explicitly states: "I am not interested in dramatic action. What attracts me is the trace of time and space in the present moment, and people moving within that trace."
   c. **Free Indirect Discourse and Viewpoint Construction**
      Hou’s viewpoint construction was influenced by two sources: Shen Congwen’s objective, detached narrative perspective (which frames all life events, including suffering and death, as natural parts of life’s flow), and Pier Paolo Pasolini’s theory of poetic cinema and free indirect discourse. Hou alternates between three narrative modes: omniscient objective narration, limited character perspective, and free indirect discourse that blends the two. For example, in *A City of Sadness* (1989), after a group of Taiwanese intellectuals sing the patriotic song "September 18" in a restaurant to express their grief and confusion over their post-war national identity, the film cuts to a long shot of the bright, open sky over the town of Jiufen. This shot is neither a pure objective landscape shot nor an explicit character point-of-view shot: it blends the characters’ melancholy, uncertain mood with the natural landscape, allowing the audience to feel their emotional state while maintaining an objective distance.
2. **Formal Technical Implementation of Hou’s Realism**
   The paper links Hou’s conceptual principles to three specific formal techniques:
   a. **Natural, Unforced Performance**
      Hou rejects the rigid, dramatized performance style of professional actors, which he sees as incompatible with the natural, everyday atmosphere he aims to create. He often casts non-professional actors (friends, crew members, local people), only gives actors a general scene framework rather than exact lines, and places the camera far away from actors during long takes to help them relax and behave naturally. For example, while filming *Flowers of Shanghai*, he asked actors to practice using water pipes and opium pipes for weeks until they became a natural, unconscious part of their movement, so that their performance in the film felt like authentic, unscripted life in a 19th century brothel.
   b. **Objective Presentation via Deep Space and Deep Focus Long Takes**
      Hou uses deep space (arranging action in the foreground, midground, and background of the frame simultaneously) and deep focus long takes to preserve the full spatial and temporal integrity of a scene, avoiding manipulative cutting that guides the audience’s emotional response. For example, in *A City of Sadness*, when the character Wenliang returns home from prison after being framed by gangsters, the camera is placed far from the action, and initially shows the family playing cards casually in the midground. A servant enters from the foreground right to announce Wenliang’s return, then runs to the background left. Family members move between the midground and background to check on Wenliang, creating an authentic, unforced home atmosphere, and allowing viewers to observe all parts of the scene freely without being directed by cuts.
   c. **Poetic Editing for Lasting Artistic Conception**
      Hou’s editing (described in prior research as "cloud block editing" or "qipo editing") uses long fixed shots, inserts empty landscape shots to convey emotion and skip time, and maintains a calm, objective tone even when depicting highly dramatic events like death. For example, in *The Time to Live and the Time to Die* (1985), the three death scenes (of the protagonist Ah Xiao’s father, mother, and grandmother) use a mix of objective wide shots and limited subjective shots of Ah Xiao’s reaction, with empty shots of swaying tree leaves inserted after each death. These shots convey the Taoist view of life and death as natural, inevitable processes, rather than exaggerating the sadness of the events for dramatic effect.
### 4.2.2. Practical Application Framework
The paper translates Hou’s realist principles into a actionable framework for the author’s short film *Farewell*, which tells the story of an elderly retired teacher Zhang Rongsheng caring for his wife Meiping who has Alzheimer’s disease:
1. **Narrative design**: Adopt an anti-dramatized structure, focusing on mundane daily care routines (feeding, bathing, taking walks) rather than exaggerated conflict, to depict the protagonist’s gradual emotional breakdown under long-term care pressure.
2. **Performance guidance**: Cast a non-professional actor as Meiping to ensure authentic, unforced behavior; only provide actors with general scene outlines rather than exact lines, allowing them to improvise within the character’s logical behavior.
3. **Cinematography**: Use mostly medium and long focal lengths, objective fixed camera angles, and deep space to show the characters and their living environment; use natural light as much as possible, with minimal artificial lighting modification.
4. **Lighting design**: Use warm light in Meiping’s bedroom to highlight traces of the couple’s decades-long marriage; use cold light for Zhang Rongsheng’s alone scenes to convey his suppressed, exhausted emotional state.

# 6. Results & Analysis
## 6.1. Core Theoretical Findings Analysis
The paper’s theoretical analysis yields three key findings:
1. Hou’s realism is a distinctly Chinese cinematic system, fundamentally different from Western realism. While Western realism prioritizes objective documentation of social reality and social critique, Hou’s realism focuses on depicting the natural flow of life, subtle emotional states, and harmony between humans and their environment, in line with Taoist principles of non-intervention.
2. Hou’s conceptual principles are fully integrated with his formal techniques: his Taoist natural view leads to his anti-dramatized narrative strategy, which is supported by formal techniques including natural performance, deep focus long takes, and poetic editing.
3. Hou’s editing technique explicitly reflects the Taoist view of life and death as a natural, inevitable process. This is clearly demonstrated via the shot-by-shot analysis of the three death scenes in *The Time to Live and the Time to Die*, which are presented in the tables below.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper provides a systematic, culturally rooted analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien’s realist film system, demonstrating that it is a uniquely Chinese cinematic tradition shaped by Taoist natural philosophy, rather than a derivative of Western realist theory. It deconstructs the links between Hou’s core conceptual principles (natural view, anti-dramatization, free indirect discourse) and his formal techniques (natural performance, deep focus long takes, poetic editing), with detailed case studies of his representative works. It also provides a practical framework for applying Hou’s realist principles to contemporary short film production, demonstrated via the author’s original short film *Farewell*. The paper fills a key gap in prior Hou Hsiao-hsien scholarship, which has long neglected the traditional Chinese ideological roots of his work.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The author explicitly notes two key limitations of the work:
1. The paper does not conduct in-depth analysis of the influence of the Chinese lyrical tradition on Hou’s work. Hou’s films are also deeply shaped by Chinese traditional poetic aesthetics and Taiwan’s post-war nostalgia literary tradition, which are not fully explored in this paper.
2. The practical application section does not include audience reception data for the short film *Farewell*, which could further verify the effectiveness of the applied realist techniques.

## 7.3. Personal Insights & Critique
This paper’s most significant contribution is its rejection of Western-centric analytical frameworks for non-Western cinema. By centering traditional Chinese Taoist philosophy in its analysis of Hou’s work, it provides a more accurate, culturally sensitive understanding of his unique cinematic style, which has important implications for Chinese film studies more broadly. Its combination of theoretical analysis and practical creative application also makes it highly grounded, avoiding the abstraction of many purely academic film studies papers.
One potential area for improvement is that the paper could include more comparison between Hou’s realism and other Chinese realist cinematic traditions (such as sixth-generation directors’ focus on marginalized social groups) to further highlight the uniqueness of Hou’s style, which is less focused on social critique and more focused on universal emotional experiences and the natural flow of life. Additionally, adding audience feedback data for the short film *Farewell* would help strengthen the practical component of the work, by demonstrating whether Hou’s realist aesthetic resonates with contemporary audiences.
