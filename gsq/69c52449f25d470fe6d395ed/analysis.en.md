## 1.5. Abstract
### Research Objective
This paper takes Hou Hsiao-hsien's spatiotemporal narrative strategies in the *Tragic Trilogy* as the entry point, to explore the historical concepts and aesthetic spirit Hou expressed when recounting Taiwan's history in the post-martial law era, and clarify the relationship between fictional historical representation and real history in film.
### Core Methodology
The paper adopts an integrated research framework combining formal narrative analysis (based on Gérard Genette's time narrative theory and Henri Lefebvre's triune spatial dialectics), film text close reading, historical contextual analysis, and literature analysis. It treats the *Tragic Trilogy* as a thematically and stylistically continuous unified work, rather than three separate films.
### Main Results & Conclusions
1.  The paper coins "condensation" as the core of Hou Hsiao-hsien's unique spatiotemporal view, which integrates temporal spatialization and spatial temporalization.
2.  The trilogy's time narrative features intertwined non-linear chronology aligned with memory and emotional logic, and heavy ellipsis creating a "disconnected events but continuous meaning" characteristic, using personal memory to reconstruct collective historical memory and resist official forced forgetting.
3.  The trilogy's space narrative adopts a "suspension" strategy for spatial symbols, and constructs three interpenetrating spatial narrative layers (everyday space, social space, natural space) to carry multi-layered historical and emotional meaning.
4.  Behind the spatiotemporal narrative, Hou shows a historical attitude combining resolute rebellion against official discourse power and a transcendent view of life, adhering to a valuable civilian historical view and realist aesthetic spirit. The *Tragic Trilogy* is Hou's "root-seeking" work tracing the historical origin of Taiwan's modern social and identity issues.
5.  The essence of historical reproduction in film is the fictionalization of past reality; film cannot fully restore real history, but can act as the asymptote of history, infinitely approaching historical truth and alerting audiences to the danger of historical amnesia.

# 2. Executive Summary
## 2.1. Background & Motivation
### Core Problem to Solve
Hou Hsiao-hsien is a world-class film master and flag bearer of the Taiwan New Cinema movement. His *Tragic Trilogy* is closely intertwined with Taiwan's century-long history from the First Sino-Japanese War (1895) to the late 20th century, and explores the complex relationship between memory and truth, official narrative and obscured real history. However, prior research on Hou's works mostly focuses on individual films (especially *City of Sadness*), very few studies recognize the *Tragic Trilogy* as an integrated, thematically continuous whole, and even fewer take spatiotemporal narrative as the core entry point to connect formal narrative features with Hou's underlying historical view and aesthetic spirit.
### Importance of the Problem
After Taiwan lifted martial law in 1987, public discussion of previously taboo historical events (such as the 228 Incident and White Terror period) became possible. The *Tragic Trilogy*, as the first group of films to publicly confront these traumatic historical events, are not only important artistic achievements, but also significant cultural events for Taiwan's collective historical memory construction. Understanding their narrative strategies and historical attitude is crucial for understanding Taiwan New Cinema, Taiwan's modern historical memory, and Chinese-language historical film creation.
### Research Gap
Prior research on the *Tragic Trilogy* falls into three main limitations:
1.  Most studies focus on single films, ignoring the thematic and stylistic continuity across the three films as a unified series.
2.  Most studies focus on aesthetic analysis (e.g. long take, poetic style) or cultural analysis (e.g. Taiwan identity), lacking systematic formal analysis of spatiotemporal narrative as the core organizing logic of the films.
3.  Few studies connect the formal narrative features of the films to the historical context of post-martial law Taiwan and Hou's overall creative career trajectory.
### Innovative Entry Point
This paper takes spatiotemporal narrative as the only core entry point, treats the *Tragic Trilogy* as a fully integrated work, and combines formal narrative analysis with historical contextual analysis to reveal the unified spatiotemporal view, historical attitude and aesthetic spirit behind the three films.
## 2.2. Main Contributions / Findings
### Primary Contributions
1.  **Theoretical Innovation**: Proposes "condensation" as the core concept to summarize Hou's unique spatiotemporal view, which effectively unifies the formal spatiotemporal features of all three films, and connects to Hou's own stated creative experience.
2.  **Systematic Narrative Analysis**: For the first time, systematically summarizes the unified time narrative features (intertwined chronology, disconnected events but continuous meaning, memory as representation of duration) and space narrative features (suspension of spatial signifiers, three-layer scaled spatial narrative structure) across the *Tragic Trilogy*.
3.  **Ideological Interpretation**: Reveals the contradictory but unified historical attitude of Hou: coexistence of resolute resistance to official cultural hegemony and historical whitewashing, and a detached transcendent view of life based on respect for human nature, proving that the *Tragic Trilogy* is Hou's "root-seeking" work connecting to his earlier film themes of "fatherless youth growth dilemma".
4.  **Practical Guidance**: Puts forward the argument that "film is the asymptote of history", clarifying the relationship between historical film fiction and historical truth, which has important guiding significance for future Chinese historical film creation.
### Key Conclusions
1.  Hou's spatiotemporal narrative in the *Tragic Trilogy* abandons strict linear time order and causal logic, arranging time according to the logic of memory and emotion, and using space as the main carrier of historical memory and meaning production.
2.  Hou adheres to a civilian historical view, focusing on the fate and dignity of ordinary people in historical turmoil, rather than grand historical events or official ideological narratives.
3.  The *Tragic Trilogy* does not attempt to provide a definitive version of Taiwan's history, but presents the complexity and trauma of history in an objective, restrained way, leaving space for the audience to interpret and reflect, resisting the official monolithic narrative of history.

# 3. Prerequisite Knowledge & Related Work
This section provides all foundational knowledge required for beginners to understand the paper.
## 3.1. Foundational Concepts
### 3.1.1. Core Film & Narrative Concepts
- **Temporal (Time) Narrative**: A narrative strategy that uses the manipulation of time (including order of event presentation, screen time length relative to real event length, flashbacks, flashforwards, etc.) as a core tool to construct story logic, express emotion, and convey thematic meaning.
- **Spatial (Space) Narrative**: A narrative strategy that uses the design, selection, and arrangement of space (including setting, location, spatial symbols, spatial layers, etc.) as a core tool to carry meaning, advance plot, and shape the story world.
- **Chronology (Order)**: From Gérard Genette's classic narrative theory, refers to the order in which story events are presented in the narrative, relative to their actual chronological order in the fictional story world. Includes:
  - Linear order: Events are presented in the same order as they occur in the story world.
  - Reverse order: Events are presented out of linear order, including flashbacks (presenting past events after later events) and flashforwards (presenting future events before earlier events).
  - Non-order: Multiple completely independent timelines are interleaved without clear logical connection, usually aligned with emotional or thematic parallels.
- **Duration (Time Distance)**: From Genette's narrative theory, refers to the relationship between the actual length of time an event takes in the story world, and the length of screen time used to present it. Common treatments include:
  - Ellipsis: A long event in the story world is skipped completely or presented in extremely short screen time.
  - Scene: An event is presented in screen time equal to its real story world duration (e.g. a real-time long take).
  - Stretch: A short event in the story world is presented in longer screen time to emphasize its importance.
### 3.1.2. Key Theories Used in the Paper
- **Henri Lefebvre's Triune Dialectics of Space**: A core theory of spatial studies, dividing space into three interconnected, equal dimensions:
  1.  **Spatial Practice (Perceived Space)**: Tangible, material space experienced through everyday bodily activities and sensory perception, e.g. a family's home, a street, a restaurant.
  2.  **Representations of Space (Conceived Space)**: Conceptual space shaped by power, knowledge, and dominant social discourse, e.g. a government-defined "historical site", a "low-income neighborhood" labeled by urban planning.
  3.  **Representational Space (Lived Space)**: Symbolic, emotionally charged space shaped by individual experience and collective memory, e.g. a home that carries a family's memories of life and death, a street where a historical traumatic event occurred.
- **Henri Bergson's Theory of Duration**: A philosophical theory of time, arguing that real time (duration) is a continuous, indivisible flow of consciousness and experience, rather than a discrete, measurable physical quantity. Memory is the best manifestation of duration, as it integrates past experience into the present.
- **Husserl's Phenomenological Epoché (Suspension)**: A philosophical method that requires suspending all pre-existing assumptions, judgments, and interpretations of an object, to observe it as it is, without imposing external meaning.
### 3.1.3. Hou Hsiao-hsien's *Tragic Trilogy* Introduction
The trilogy is Hou's most important series of historical films, covering 100 years of Taiwan's modern history:
1.  **City of Sadness (1989)**: Set between 1945 (Taiwan's retrocession to China after Japanese colonial rule) and 1949 (KMT retreat to Taiwan), centered on the fate of the Lin family during the 228 Incident and the start of the White Terror period. It was the first Taiwanese film to win the Venice Film Festival Golden Lion Award, and the first public cultural work to confront the previously taboo 228 Incident after martial law was lifted.
2.  **The Puppetmaster (1993)**: A semi-documentary, semi-fiction biopic of legendary Taiwanese glove puppetry master Li Tianlu, covering his life from 1895 (start of Japanese colonial rule) to 1945 (end of WWII). It won the Cannes Film Festival Jury Prize.
3.  **Good Men, Good Women (1995)**: Uses a nested "play-within-a-play" structure, parallelly telling the story of 1940s-1950s political victim Chiang Pi-yu (based on a real historical figure) and contemporary 1990s actress Liang Jing who plays Chiang in a film. It covers the White Terror period in the 1950s and Taiwan's contemporary social transformation in the 1990s.
### 3.1.4. Taiwan Modern History Background (Required to Understand the Films)
- 1895: China loses the First Sino-Japanese War, cedes Taiwan to Japan via the Treaty of Shimonoseki, beginning 50 years of Japanese colonial rule.
- 1945: Japan surrenders in WWII, Taiwan is returned to the Republic of China (ROC) government led by the KMT.
- 1947: The 228 Incident: Mass anti-government protests against KMT authoritarian rule, corruption, and discrimination against local Taiwanese break out, and are violently suppressed by KMT authorities, with an estimated 10,000-28,000 civilians killed. This incident marks the start of the 38-year White Terror period.
- 1949: The KMT loses the Chinese Civil War to the CCP, retreats to Taiwan, and imposes martial law, banning all political opposition, freedom of speech, and public discussion of sensitive historical events like the 228 Incident.
- 1987: The KMT lifts martial law, allowing public discussion of previously taboo historical events, creating the context for Hou to make the *Tragic Trilogy*.
## 3.2. Previous Works
The paper divides prior research into two main categories:
### 3.2.1. Research on Hou Hsiao-hsien and the *Tragic Trilogy*
As of October 2023, there are 966 CNKI (China National Knowledge Infrastructure) papers on Hou Hsiao-hsien, falling into four categories:
1.  **Aesthetic Studies**: Focus on Hou's unique film language, including his signature long take, fixed wide shot, poetic Eastern aesthetic, and restrained narrative style. Representative works include Meng Hongfeng's *On Hou Hsiao-hsien's Style*, and Rao Shuguang's edited volume *Film Comes from Non-Film: Research on Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films*.
2.  **Text & Narrative Studies**: Focus on plot structure, character construction, and narrative features of individual films. Representative works include Huang Zhongjun's *Re-discussing Taiwan New Cinema: Text, Form and Influence*, and Wu Huiying's *Research on the Scene Design of "Dining Scenes" in Hou Hsiao-hsien's Films*.
3.  **Authorship Studies**: Compare Hou with other directors with similar stylistic or thematic features, including Ozu Yasujiro, Yang Dechang, Jia Zhangke, and Kim Ki-duk, to explore his unique authorial features.
4.  **Cultural Studies**: Focus on issues of Taiwan identity, local consciousness, historical context, and national identity in Hou's films. Representative works include Sun Weichuan's *Uphold the Spirit of Realism, Seek the Unity of Beauty and Truth: Reading Hou Hsiao-hsien's "Tragic Trilogy"*, and Jiao Xiongping's *Searching for Taiwan's Identity: The Local Consciousness of Taiwan New Cinema and Hou Hsiao-hsien's <City of Sadness>*.
    Prior research specifically focusing on the entire *Tragic Trilogy* is extremely scarce, with only 3 relevant CNKI papers, most focusing on individual films, especially *City of Sadness* (46 dedicated papers), while *The Puppetmaster* and *Good Men, Good Women* have far less dedicated research.
### 3.2.2. Research on Spatiotemporal Narrative
- **Time Narrative Research**: Classic narrative theory since the 1960s has focused on time as the core of narrative. Gérard Genette's *Narrative Discourse* established the core concepts of chronology and duration for time narrative analysis. In film studies, Gilles Deleuze's *Time-Image* and *Movement-Image* elevated the study of film time to a philosophical level, arguing that film can present pure time experience beyond linear causality. Chinese representative research includes You Fei's *Research on Film Time Narrative*, which summarizes the basic forms of film time manipulation.
- **Spatial Narrative Research**: The "spatial turn" in humanities and social sciences since the 1970s, led by Joseph Frank, Henri Lefebvre, and Michel Foucault, brought space to the forefront of narrative studies. Lefebvre's *The Production of Space* proposed that space is not a passive static container, but a dynamic social product shaped by power and production relations. In Chinese film studies, Li Xianjie's *Film Narrative Theory: Theory and Examples* is a foundational work on film spatial narrative.
## 3.3. Technological Evolution
Film narrative studies have evolved through three main stages:
1.  Early stage (1910s-1960s): Focused on plot structure and formal techniques of film language, with narrative seen as a tool to tell a linear, causal story.
2.  Time narrative stage (1960s-1990s): Influenced by structuralist narrative theory and Deleuze's time-image theory, research focused on how film manipulates time as a core narrative and expressive tool, exploring non-linear time, subjective time, and time as a theme in itself.
3.  Integrated spatiotemporal narrative stage (1990s-present): Influenced by the spatial turn, research began to integrate time and space as interconnected core elements of narrative, exploring how time and space interact to construct meaning, express themes, and shape audience experience.
    This paper fits directly into the latest integrated spatiotemporal narrative research trend, applying this framework to a director's representative historical film series for the first time.
## 3.4. Differentiation Analysis
Compared to prior research, this paper has three core innovations and differences:
1.  **Integrated Research Object**: For the first time, it treats the *Tragic Trilogy* as a thematically, stylistically, and ideologically continuous unified whole, rather than three separate films, revealing the internal logic of evolution and continuity across the three films.
2.  **Unique Entry Point**: It takes spatiotemporal narrative as the only core entry point, connecting formal narrative features to deeper ideological, historical, and aesthetic expression, avoiding the one-sidedness of pure aesthetic or pure cultural analysis.
3.  **New Theoretical Concepts**: It proposes original concepts including "condensation" spatiotemporal view, "suspension" of spatial signifiers, and "film as the asymptote of history", providing a new interpretive framework for Hou's works and Chinese-language historical films in general.

# 4. Methodology
## 4.1. Principles
The core principle of the paper's methodology is the integration of formal spatiotemporal narrative analysis and historical contextual analysis:
1.  **Formal Analysis Principle**: Use classic narrative theory (Genette's time narrative, Lefebvre's spatial theory) to systematically deconstruct the formal features of time and space arrangement in the trilogy, avoiding subjective interpretation without formal evidence.
2.  **Historical Context Principle**: Ground all formal analysis in the specific historical context of the period covered by the films (1895-1990s Taiwan) and the production context of the films (post-martial law 1990s Taiwan), linking formal narrative choices to their historical, social, and cultural meaning.
3.  **Text Close Reading Principle**: All analysis is based on detailed shot-by-shot close reading of the three films, using specific scene and shot examples to support every conclusion.
    The theoretical intuition behind the methodology is that spatiotemporal arrangement is not just a formal technical choice for historical films, but directly reflects the director's view of history, memory, and the relationship between the past and present.
## 4.2. Core Methodology In-depth (Layer by Layer)
The paper's research follows a five-step logical flow, integrating theoretical framework, text analysis, and contextual interpretation:
---
### Step 1: Contextualize the *Tragic Trilogy* and its narrative strategy
First, the paper establishes the historical background of the century-long Taiwanese history covered by the trilogy, from 1895 Japanese colonial rule to 1990s post-martial law Taiwan, to clarify the historical events referenced in the films.
Next, it analyzes Hou's historical storytelling strategy, which balances two competing demands of historical films:
1.  **Balance between historical materials and historical imagination**: Historical films require a basis in real historical materials to maintain historical credibility, but inevitably include the director's subjective imagination, selection, and interpretation of events. The paper cites Hayden White's argument that "history is essentially the fictionalization of facts and the fictionalization of past reality", arguing that Hou achieves a balance between objective historical facts and subjective artistic expression, constructing a self-consistent historical order in the films.
2.  **Linkage between individual fate and historical change**: Hou avoids direct depiction of grand historical events, instead embedding historical change in the fate of ordinary individual characters, using the rise and fall of small families and individuals to reflect the upheaval of the entire era. For example, the destruction of the Lin family in *City of Sadness* directly reflects the trauma of the 228 Incident and White Terror period for the entire Taiwanese population.
    ---
### Step 2: Propose the core concept of Hou's spatiotemporal view: "Condensation"
The paper extracts "condensation" as the core of Hou's unique spatiotemporal view, based on Hou's own stated creative experience (his childhood memory of hiding in a mango tree stealing mangoes, feeling time freeze and space expand as he focused on eating, hearing only the wind and cicadas). The concept has two interconnected layers:
1.  **Temporal Spatialization**: The continuous, flowing duration of time is frozen into discrete, tangible historical moments captured in space, so that space becomes the "container of memory" that carries the traces of past time.
2.  **Spatial Temporalization**: Static, tangible space is embedded with continuous historical time and collective memory, so that every space in the films carries the weight of past events and emotional meaning, becoming a portal to past time.
    The paper argues that this "condensation" view directly shapes all spatiotemporal narrative choices in the trilogy, and is the source of Hou's signature poetic, detached, and vast aesthetic style.
---
### Step 3: Systematically analyze the time narrative features of the trilogy
The paper divides the time narrative analysis into three parts:
#### Part A: Intertwined chronology: Memory inversion and life parallelism
The trilogy abandons strict linear chronology, using two types of non-linear chronology aligned with the logic of memory and emotion:
1.  **Reverse Chronology**: Used in *City of Sadness* and *The Puppetmaster*, with frequent flashbacks triggered by character memory and emotion, breaking linear time order. For example, in *City of Sadness*, the scene where Japanese woman Shizuko gives her late brother's wooden sword, poem, and her own kimono as gifts to Kuanmei and Kuanjung triggers a series of flashbacks to past interactions between Kuanjung, Shizuko, and her brother during the Japanese colonial period. The sequence of these flashbacks is arranged according to the emotional logic of nostalgia and separation, rather than the causal logic of events.
    This reverse chronology aligns with the natural way human memory works, as people often recall past events when triggered by related objects or experiences, making the historical narrative feel more authentic and personal.
2.  **Non-Chronology**: Used in *Good Men, Good Women*, which interleaves three completely independent time modules without clear transition markers:
    - 1990s contemporary Taipei: Actress Liang Jing's daily life after her boyfriend Ah Wei is killed in a gang fight.
    - 1980s Taipei: Liang Jing's memories of her life with Ah Wei before his death.
    - 1940s-1950s historical time: The life of political victim Chiang Pi-yu, who Liang Jing plays in a film.
      The three timelines are interleaved based on the thematic and emotional parallelism between Liang Jing and Chiang Pi-yu, both of whom lose their partners to violent historical/social forces, creating a cross-temporal "life parallelism" that connects the trauma of the past to the confusion of the present.
#### Part B: Disconnected events but continuous meaning: Time axis fracture and continuous time imagery
Hou uses extreme ellipsis in duration treatment, only selecting the most meaningful fragments of events, cutting off the causal links between events, creating a "disconnected events but continuous meaning" feature:
1.  **Ellipsis and discrete fragments**: Most events in the films are presented as discrete fragments with no clear setup or explanation of prior context. For example, in *The Puppetmaster*, the scene where child character Ta-yen Tsai is taken away by her birth mother is presented in a single long take, with no prior introduction of the character or explanation of the backstory of why she is being taken away. The audience only learns the context through the dialogue in the single scene, making the event feel like a random, fragmented memory fragment, consistent with how an elderly person recalls minor events from their childhood.
    This ellipsis strategy intentionally hides 60% of the event context, leaving the audience to fill in the gaps through their own interpretation, making the narrative feel more open and authentic to the fragmented nature of memory.
2.  **Time imagery as entry points to historical time**: The gaps between fragmented event fragments are filled by continuous time imagery, including empty landscape shots, glove puppetry performances, folk ballads, and everyday life details. These imagery act as portals to historical time, allowing the audience to enter the emotional atmosphere of the era without explicit explanation. For example, the recurring empty shots of misty mountains and coastal landscapes in *City of Sadness* carry the sense of historical uncertainty and collective trauma of the era, without any explicit commentary.
#### Part C: Representation of time: Memory as the best manifestation of duration
Drawing on Bergson's theory of duration, the paper argues that memory is the core way the trilogy constructs and presents historical time:
1.  **Using narration to resist forgetting**: The trilogy focuses on everyday moments of ordinary people, rather than grand historical scenes, to reconstruct collective memory of Taiwan's traumatic modern history, resisting the official KMT narrative that tried to erase or whitewash the 228 Incident and White Terror period. For example, *City of Sadness* never directly shows the violent scenes of the 228 Incident, but presents its impact on the daily life of the Lin family: the arrest of family members, the fear of midnight raids, the breakdown of family structure, making the historical trauma more tangible and personal for the audience.
2.  **Memory time as the basis of real life**: The paper argues that for historical events that have passed and can never be fully restored, collective memory carried by individual stories is the only proof that the events really happened. For example, *The Puppetmaster* uses Li Tianlu's personal oral memory as the sole narrative source, even when his oral account contradicts the script based on his written biography, Hou includes both versions in the film, emphasizing that individual memory is a more authentic manifestation of historical experience than official written records.
    ---
### Step 4: Systematically analyze the space narrative features of the trilogy
The paper divides the space narrative analysis into three parts:
#### Part A: Spatial cognition: Historical space with meaning-producing function
First, the paper redefines the nature of narrative space in the trilogy, drawing on Lefebvre's triune spatial dialectics:
1.  **Space as a verb**: Space is not a passive static container for events, but a dynamic productive force shaped by social relations, power, and memory, which actively shapes the fate of characters and the meaning of events.
2.  **"Neverland of history": Representational historical space**: The historical space constructed in the films is a hybrid of real historical space reconstruction and fictional symbolic space, a "Neverland" that carries Hou's historical view and emotional attitude, rather than a 100% accurate restoration of real historical space. For example, *The Puppetmaster* intentionally uses low-light, hazy cinematography for historical scenes, creating a dream-like feeling of distant memory, emphasizing that the past can never be fully restored.
3.  **Formation of three-dimensional space: Meaning-producing function of sound and cinematography**: The space is made three-dimensional and multi-layered by cinematography and sound design:
    - **Cinematography**: Hou uses mostly long takes, wide and medium shots, very rare close-ups, leaving large amounts of off-screen space for the audience to imagine, creating a detached, observational perspective. For example, the scene where Lin Wenxiong is shot dead in *City of Sadness* is presented in a single fixed long take, with no close-up of his death, emphasizing the insignificance of individual fate in front of historical forces.
    - **Sound design**: The use of multiple dialects (Minnan, Japanese, Mandarin, Shanghai dialect), folk operas, ballads, and political radio broadcasts adds layers of meaning to the space. For example, the use of Shanghai dialect by the KMT-connected gang in *City of Sadness* directly reflects the power imbalance between mainland immigrants and local Taiwanese after 1945.
#### Part B: Suspended signifiers: Construction of memory space semiotic domain
The paper identifies "suspension" (phenomenological epoché) as Hou's core method of constructing memory space:
1.  **Memory space as a semiotic domain**: The historical space in the films is a semiotic domain composed of various memory symbols (objects, locations, sounds) that can trigger collective memory of the era for Taiwanese audiences.
2.  **Suspension of spatial signifiers**: Hou suspends all pre-existing official interpretations and judgments of historical events, presenting space and events objectively without explicit commentary, directed metaphors, or emotional guidance, leaving all meaning to be interpreted by the audience. For example, the air raid scene in *The Puppetmaster* is presented in complete darkness, with only a swaying piece of clothing visible and the sound of air raid sirens, with no close-ups of character expressions or explicit indication of how the characters feel, allowing the audience to experience the fear, numbness, and helplessness of the time themselves, without being told what to feel.
    This suspension strategy reflects Hou's rejection of official monolithic historical narrative, as he refuses to impose a single definitive interpretation of history on the audience.
#### Part C: Scaling of space: Construction of multiple spatial narrative layers
The trilogy constructs three interpenetrating spatial narrative layers by scaling the observational distance, which correspond to Lefebvre's three spatial dimensions:
1.  **Everyday space (marginal space)**: The private domestic space of ordinary people (homes, small restaurants, local shops), corresponding to perceived space, carrying the everyday life experience of civilians. It is the layer most impacted by historical forces, but also the layer that shows the resilience of ordinary people. For example, Kuanmei continues to feed her child after receiving news of her brother's death, showing how ordinary people must continue their daily lives even in the face of historical trauma.
2.  **Social space**: Public space shaped by power (wine tables, negotiation venues, prisons, nightclubs), corresponding to conceived space, where power struggles and historical decisions take place. For example, the wine tables where KMT officials and gang leaders negotiate in *City of Sadness* are the space where the fate of local Taiwanese is decided without their input.
3.  **Natural space**: Landscape space (mountains, rivers, sky, fields), corresponding to representational space, acting as a non-human, transcendent "eye of heaven" overlooking all human tragedies, carrying the sense of fate and historical inevitability. For example, after Lin Wenxiong is shot dead by the Shanghai gang in *City of Sadness*, the next shot is a natural landscape of misty mountains with a lone bird circling, emphasizing that individual tragedy is just a tiny part of the larger flow of history.
    These three layers interpenetrate even within a single scene. For example, in the wine table scene where intellectuals discuss the political situation after the 228 Incident, the high-spirited intellectuals occupy the social space, while the deaf-mute Lin Wenqing and Kuanmei, who cannot participate in the discussion, communicate via written notes in their own marginal everyday space within the same physical location.
---
### Step 5: Integrated spatiotemporal analysis and exploration of historical consciousness
Finally, the paper integrates the time and space narrative analysis to explore the underlying historical consciousness and aesthetic spirit:
1.  **Spatiotemporal narrative feature: Condensation**: The core spatiotemporal feature of the trilogy is "condensation", achieved through two methods:
    - **Overlap of present space and past time**: Hou films the trilogy in the actual locations where the historical events took place, using the "present here" space to represent the "past then" time, blurring the boundary between personal memory and collective history, creating an integrated historical time-space.
    - **Psychological-emotional spatiotemporal combination**: All spatiotemporal arrangement is based on character emotion and psychological logic rather than causal logic, creating a poetic narrative rhythm that aligns with the experience of memory.
      This condensation creates a Chan Buddhist aesthetic feeling of "empty mountain with no one, water flowing and flowers blooming", where the narrative perspective is detached from the events, presenting history in a calm, objective, and transcendent way.
2.  **Historical attitude and view of life: Coexistence of decisiveness and detachment**:
    - **Detachment**: Influenced by Chinese writer Shen Congwen's worldview, which sees all human tragedies as normal, inevitable occurrences in the long flow of history, Hou maintains extreme emotional restraint in presenting even the most tragic events, avoiding sensationalism or explicit judgment.
    - **Decisiveness**: Despite his detached presentation, Hou holds a firm resolute attitude against official cultural hegemony and historical whitewashing, focusing on the dignity and fate of ordinary people, constructing a "small temple of human nature" that honors the value of every ordinary person in history, even when their resistance to historical forces is futile.
3.  **Nature of historical reproduction: Film as the asymptote of history**: The paper proposes its core argument: since historical reproduction in film is essentially the fictionalization of past reality, film can never fully restore real history, but can act as the asymptote of history: infinitely approaching historical truth by presenting the real experience and dignity of ordinary people, alerting audiences to the trauma of the past and the danger of historical amnesia.
    The paper also argues that the *Tragic Trilogy* is Hou's "root-seeking" work, tracing the historical origin of the "fatherless youth growth dilemma" that is the core theme of his earlier films (e.g. *The Boys from Fengkuei*, *A Time to Live, A Time to Die*), as the "disappeared father" figures in his earlier works are exactly the generation of people who suffered and disappeared during the White Terror period portrayed in the trilogy.

# 6. Results & Analysis (Adapted for Humanities Qualitative Research)
## 6.1. Core Results Analysis
The paper reaches four well-supported core conclusions:
### 6.1.1. "Condensation" as Hou's unified spatiotemporal view
The proposed "condensation" concept effectively unifies the formal spatiotemporal features of all three films, and is consistent with Hou's own stated creative experience and aesthetic style. It explains why Hou's films often make audiences lose their sense of time, as if in a dream, which is Hou's intended effect to immerse audiences in the experience of memory and history, rather than following a linear plot.
This concept fills a gap in prior research, which had only described Hou's spatiotemporal style as "poetic" or "fragmented" without a unified core explanation.
### 6.1.2. Unified time and space narrative features across the trilogy
The paper reveals for the first time the consistent logic of spatiotemporal arrangement across the three films, despite their very different surface narrative structures (*City of Sadness*'s scattered narrative, *The Puppetmaster*'s fragmented oral narrative, *Good Men, Good Women*'s nested non-linear narrative):
- All three films arrange time according to the logic of memory and emotion, rather than linear causal logic, and use heavy ellipsis to create a fragmented, dream-like feeling consistent with the nature of memory.
- All three films use the "suspension" strategy for spatial symbols, and construct the same three-layer spatial narrative structure, using space as the main carrier of memory and historical meaning.
  This reveals that the three films are not separate works, but a continuous exploration of how to represent history through spatiotemporal manipulation.
### 6.1.3. Underlying historical attitude and aesthetic spirit
The paper clearly articulates the contradictory but unified historical attitude behind Hou's seemingly detached narrative style:
- He is resolutely opposed to official historical whitewashing and cultural hegemony, refusing to adopt the official KMT narrative of Taiwan's history, and centering the stories of marginalized ordinary people who are erased from official history.
- He maintains a transcendent, humanistic view of life, avoiding demonizing any group (including Japanese colonizers and KMT authorities), and presenting the complexity of historical experience without black-and-white judgment.
  This attitude is reflected in his consistent use of civilian perspective, focusing on the dignity and resilience of ordinary people in historical turmoil, rather than taking sides in political disputes.
### 6.1.4. "Film as the asymptote of history" argument
This argument provides a new, nuanced understanding of the relationship between historical films and historical truth, avoiding the two extremes of:
1.  The view that historical films must be 100% factually accurate to be valuable.
2.  The view that historical films are completely fictional and have no connection to historical truth.
    The paper argues that the value of historical films lies not in restoring historical facts, but in approaching the truth of historical experience and human nature, which has important guiding significance for Chinese historical film creation, which has often been criticized for either overly rigid historical accuracy requirements or overly free fictionalization for entertainment.

# 7. Conclusion & Reflections
## 7.1. Conclusion Summary
This paper provides a systematic, integrated analysis of Hou Hsiao-hsien's *Tragic Trilogy* from the perspective of spatiotemporal narrative, drawing the following key conclusions:
1.  Hou's core spatiotemporal view in the trilogy is "condensation", which integrates temporal spatialization and spatial temporalization, creating a poetic, dream-like narrative style that aligns with the experience of memory, making audiences feel immersed in the flow of history.
2.  The trilogy's time narrative uses non-linear chronology aligned with memory and emotional logic, heavy ellipsis creating "disconnected events but continuous meaning", and personal memory to reconstruct collective historical memory, resisting official forced forgetting of Taiwan's traumatic modern history.
3.  The trilogy's space narrative uses the "suspension" strategy to present space and events objectively without imposed interpretation, and constructs three interpenetrating spatial layers (everyday space, social space, natural space) that carry multi-layered historical and emotional meaning.
4.  Behind the spatiotemporal narrative, Hou shows a unique historical attitude combining resolute resistance to official historical whitewashing and a transcendent, humanistic view of life, adhering to a civilian historical view that centers the dignity and fate of ordinary people, rather than grand historical narratives or ideological positions.
5.  The *Tragic Trilogy* is Hou's "root-seeking" work, tracing the historical origin of the "fatherless youth growth dilemma" in his earlier films, and exploring the roots of Taiwan's modern social and identity issues.
6.  The essence of historical reproduction in film is the fictionalization of past reality, so film can never fully restore real history, but can act as the asymptote of history: infinitely approaching the truth of historical experience and human nature, and alerting audiences to the danger of historical amnesia. This argument has important guiding significance for future Chinese historical film creation.
## 7.2. Limitations & Future Work
The paper explicitly acknowledges its limitations and proposes future research directions:
1.  **Limitations**:
    - The analysis of the interaction between time and space is still relatively separate, with separate sections for time and space narrative, rather than a fully integrated analysis of how time and space interact dynamically in every scene.
    - The analysis of *Good Men, Good Women* is relatively less detailed than the analysis of *City of Sadness* and *The Puppetmaster*, especially regarding the nested narrative structure and the relationship between historical time and contemporary time.
    - The paper focuses only on the text of the films and their historical context, without including audience reception analysis, to explore how audiences in different regions and time periods interpret the films' spatiotemporal narrative and historical meaning.
2.  **Future Research Directions**:
    - Further research can explore the integrated dynamic interaction between time and space in the trilogy, rather than analyzing them separately.
    - Future research can include audience reception studies, comparing how Taiwanese, mainland Chinese, and international audiences interpret the films' historical meaning, especially in the context of changing cross-Strait relations.
